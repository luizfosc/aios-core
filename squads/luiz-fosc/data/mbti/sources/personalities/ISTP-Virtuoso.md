# The Puzzle of Personality
When they receive their personality test results on our website, most people’s first
question is, “What do these letters mean?” We are of course referring to those
mysterious acronyms – INTJ-A, for example, or ENFP-T. As you may have already
learned from the type descriptions or articles on the website, the five letters of these
acronyms each refer to a specific trait, with certain trait combinations forming
various types and type groups. But before we discuss those traits in depth, let’s
explore their historical foundations.
Since the dawn of time, humans have drawn up schematics to describe and
categorize our personalities. From the four temperaments of the ancient civilizations
to the latest advances in psychology, we have been driven to fit the variables and
complexities of human personality into well-defined models. Although we are still
some time away from being able to do that, the current models account for our most
important personality traits and can predict our behavior with a high degree of
accuracy.
Personality is just one of many factors that guide our behavior, however. Our actions
are also influenced by our environment, our experiences, and our individual goals.

# The Puzzle of Personality
This profile describes how people belonging to a specific personality type are likely to
behave. We will outline indicators and tendencies, however, not definitive guidelines
or answers. Significant differences can exist even among people who share a
personality type. The information in this profile is meant to inspire personal growth
and an improved understanding of yourself and your relationships – not to be taken
as gospel.
Our approach has its roots in two different philosophies. One dates back to the early
20th century and was the brainchild of Carl Gustav Jung, the father of analytical
psychology. Jung’s theory of psychological types is perhaps the most influential
creation in personality typology, and it has inspired many different theories. One of
Jung’s key contributions was the development of the concept of Introversion and
Extraversion – he theorized that each of us falls into one of these two categories,
either focusing on the internal world (Introvert) or the outside world (Extravert).
Besides Introversion and Extraversion, Jung coined the concept of so-called cognitive
functions, separated into Judging or Perceiving categories. According to Jung, each
person prefers one of these cognitive functions and may most naturally rely on it in
everyday situations.
In the 1920s, Jung’s theory was noticed by Katharine Cook Briggs, who later co-
authored one of the most popular personality indicators used today, the Myers-
Briggs Type Indicator® (MBTI®). Briggs was a teacher with an avid interest in
personality typing, having developed her own type theory before learning of Jung’s
writings. Together with her daughter, Isabel Briggs Myers, they developed a
convenient way to describe the order of each person’s Jungian preferences – this is
how four-letter acronyms were born.

Due to its simplicity and ease of use, the four-letter naming model is now shared by
a number of diverse theories and approaches, including Socionics, Keirsey
Temperament Sorter®, and Linda Berens’ Interaction Styles, among many others.
While the acronyms used by these theories may be identical or very similar, however,
their meanings do not always overlap. As a result, if you meet five people who say, “I
am an INFJ,” their understanding of what that means may vary significantly.
Regardless of its structure, any type-based theory will struggle to describe or
characterize people whose scores lie near the dividing line. A different way to look at
personalities is through the lens of a trait-based rather than a type-based model.
What do we mean by that? Instead of creating an arbitrary number of categories and
attempting to fit people within them, a trait-based model simply studies the degree
to which people exhibit certain traits.
You may have heard the term Ambivert, which is a perfect example in this case.
Ambiversion means that someone falls in the middle of the Introversion-Extraversion
scale, being neither too outgoing nor too withdrawn. Trait-based theories would
simply say that an Ambivert is moderately Extraverted or moderately Introverted and
leave it at that, without assigning a personality type.
A trait-based approach makes it easier to reliably measure correlations between
personality traits and other characteristics – for example, political attitudes. This is
why trait-based approaches dominate psychometric research, but that’s more or less

With our model, we’ve combined the best of both worlds. We use the acronym format

such as cognitive functions, or their prioritization. Jungian concepts are very difficult
to measure and evaluate scientifically, so we’ve instead chosen to rework and
rebalance the dimensions of personality called the Big Five personality traits, a model
that is very popular in psychological and social research.
Our personality types are based on five independent spectrums, with all letters in
the type code (e.g. INFJ-A) referring to one of the two sides of the corresponding
spectrum. We’ll talk more about these concepts in the next chapter. This approach
has allowed us to achieve high test accuracy while also retaining the ability to define
and describe distinct personality types.

## Five Personality Aspects
### Mind: Introverted (I) vs. Extraverted (E)
The distinction between Introverts and Extraverts may be the oldest notion in the
history of personality theories. It has long been observed that some people are
expressive and outgoing, while others are reserved, quiet, and more comfortable
alone. The first group recharges by engaging with the external world and
communicating with other people, whereas members of the second group are
energized by spending time alone and connecting with their own inner world.
We focus on these differences in our first scale, which we call Mind. This scale – which
is based on a person’s level of expressiveness and the degree to which he or she
seeks external stimulation – determines how we see and approach the outside world,
including people, objects, and activities. This scale correlates with a number of other
personality traits, such as willingness to volunteer, desire to engage in thrill-seeking
activities, and romantic assertiveness, to name just a few.

### Energy: Intuitive (N) vs. Observant (S)
Energy is the second scale in our model, and in our opinion it is the most important.
While the other four scales determine how you interact with the world (Mind), make
decisions (Nature), schedule your activities (Tactics), or react to external feedback
(Identity), the Energy scale actually determines how you see the world and what kind
of information you focus on.
All personality types can be divided into those that favor the Intuitive energy style
and those that favor the Observant energy style. Intuitive individuals tend to be
visionary, interested in ideas and abstractions, and attracted to novelty. Observant

### Nature: Thinking (T) vs. Feeling (F)
The third scale, which we call Nature, determines how we make decisions and cope
with emotions. While we all have feelings, there are significant differences in how
different types react to them. This scale influences a number of areas of our lives,
particularly our interactions with other people.
People with the Thinking trait seek logic and rational arguments, relying on their head
rather than their heart. They do their best to safeguard, manage, and conceal their
emotions. “Whatever happens, you must always keep a cool head” – this is the motto
of Thinking individuals. These types are not cold-blooded or indifferent, however.
People with the Thinking trait are often just as emotional as those with the Feeling
trait, but they subdue or override their feelings with their rational logic.

## Roles
The Role layer determines our goals, interests, and preferred activities. Each of the
four Roles covers a set of personality types that are very similar, and we will use these
groups later in this profile to draw contrasts and similarities between personalities.
They also serve to highlight the importance we have placed on the Energy trait. Each
personality type will share either the Observant or the Intuitive trait with all other
members of their group, as well as one other key trait. Virtuosos belong to the
Explorer Role group, along with three other personality types: Adventurers,
Entrepreneurs, and Entertainers.
### Analysts
Shared traits: Intuitive and Thinking
The personality types in the Analyst Role – Architects, Logicians, Commanders, and
Debaters – embrace rationality, excelling in intellectual and technological pursuits.
The Thinking trait makes Analysts exacting, and the Intuitive trait lets them apply
their minds to almost anything, with their imaginations aiding calculated, strategic
thinking, or seeing just how far the rabbit hole goes with a crazy scheme or thought
experiment.
These personalities are driven to understand and create, and have no problem
switching between speculative musing and frank, solutions-oriented approaches to
whatever problems are at hand. Broad, intelligent vision gives Analysts an ingenious

these abilities and use them to advance a worthwhile cause or project.

### Diplomats
Shared traits: Intuitive and Feeling
Diplomats – Advocates, Mediators, Protagonists, and Campaigners – tend to be

traditional senses – it’s as if they have a full set of tuning forks in their hearts that can

### Sentinels
Shared traits: Observant and Judging
Sentinels – Logisticians, Defenders, Executives, and Consuls – are cooperative and
practical. Their grounded approach helps them feel comfortable with who they are,
defining themselves not by individualism, but by character and competence. These
personality types seek order, security, and stability, and tend to work hard to
maintain the way of things, leading to a deserved reputation as the core of any group
or organization, from family to church to the office and the factory floor.
These types embrace teamwork, but expect the same performance and
respectability from others that they strive for themselves. Sentinels often dislike
strident nonconformity because they envision progress through collaboration and
hard work within a known set of rules – to let everyone do their own thing on a whim
would be anarchy. Sentinels prefer proven methods and honest accomplishment to
self-indulgent exploration and esoteric ideas. Because these personalities are deeply
prudent, they also tend to be self-motivated, and rarely need external inspiration to
be productive.

increase their skill levels on their own. With interest and passion in place, they may

# Professional Development
The Virtuoso personality is probably the most mysterious among all types; however,

more theoretical and abstract approach favored by academics. People with this
personality type are very real-world oriented and prefer the “hands-on” approach.

forensic scientists, etc. While they can work effectively in jobs that rely on theory if

Virtuosos are very results-oriented, self-confident, and loyal. Long-term thinking isn’t

difficulties in their careers if they choose the wrong path. People with this personality

that don’t utilize or recognize their efforts and strengths, as these Introverts may lack
the assertiveness skills to promote their talents. Ultimately, job satisfaction can boil
down to picking careers that are good matches for their traits and skills.

### Virtuoso–Analyst Cooperation
Virtuosos and Analysts both like to search for answers, but their styles and scope of

#### Balanced Cooperation

While Analysts want to play with ideas and systems, Virtuosos would rather solve
problems in a hands-on fashion. Analysts may not always see their ideas through to
the end, but it’s hard to get Virtuosos to wait long enough to check with others before

work with overarching systems that are more conceptual than tangible and often

build it better on paper, there’s nobody like Analysts.
When Virtuosos and Analysts work together, they must understand that they’re each
part of a two-step process. Analysts can be weak at implementation while Virtuosos
specialize in it. Virtuosos can put together the pieces that make Analysts’ models
work. Virtuosos sometimes don’t pay enough attention to the “big picture,” and focus
on the details, whereas their Analyst colleagues find this integral to their thinking. If
each type understands they both have good things to stir into the same pot,
Virtuosos and Analysts can create powerful working relationships.

the capsule home. Communication between ground control and the astronauts was
vital to the success of the rescue. This metaphor loosely demonstrates how Virtuosos’
and Analysts’ different functions and styles can work together to fulfill the same goals
– particularly between theorists and craftspeople.

coworkers don’t feel the need to dig into minutia as they do.

Analysts want the same things they do – the successful resolution of problems and
challenges. Both types are puzzle solvers, but each has a different technique. It may

#### Balanced Cooperation
The common ground between Virtuosos and Diplomats is somewhat intangible, but

something become better. For Virtuosos, it’s finding and implementing novel,
interesting solutions to specific problems. Virtuosos can look at a tangled mess of a

For Diplomats, it’s more broadly about personal growth, moral development, and

Virtuosos want to know how they can manipulate each little piece and use it to create
something new and better – especially if it meets a concrete need or feels like solving
a puzzle. If you want a “vision,” call on Diplomats. If you want Diplomats’ visions
realized in down-to-earth ways, call on Virtuosos.

care of their colleagues’ or clients’ feelings or welfare. They may take risks, cut
corners, or cavalierly develop plans that might have a negative impact on others.
They may trust their instincts a little too much, doing something on their own without
taking other people’s opinions or standards into account. Such occasions can create
conflicts between Virtuoso and Diplomat coworkers.

The Doers and the Dreamers
Diplomats may see Virtuosos as shallow, while Virtuosos may regard Diplomats as
impractical dreamers. Virtuosos tend to live in the “here and now,” while Diplomats
live in the infinite time of imagination and thought. Diplomats are more likely
concerned with long-range results while Virtuosos are more interested in immediate
actions and solutions.
Let’s use a hospital metaphor here. Diplomats would be the case managers equipped
to handle patients’ long-term wellbeing and creating holistic treatment plans for
them. Meanwhile, Virtuosos are down in the emergency room doing triage and
reattaching limbs in their practical ways. Dissimilar views can create communication
difficulties between the case managers and ER doctors because of their decidedly
distinct interests and styles. However, someone running a hospital wants both
around if they’re going to save lives in the long- and short-term.
#### Rebalancing Cooperation
Discovering Motivation
If Virtuosos want to discover what motivates their Diplomat coworkers, they can first
try to discover their values. These coworkers are likely to extend their values and
their ideals into their work lives as much as they reasonably can. The best way to
communicate with them is to be careful not to bog them down with too many details
or methods. These things hold little interest for Diplomats, who prefer talking about
broader ideas. Virtuosos who want to find common ground with Diplomats may want
to explore questions that start with, “What if?” (In return, Diplomats may want to ask
questions that begin with, “How?”)

### Virtuoso–Sentinel Cooperation
It’s easy to get the impression that Sentinels and Virtuosos are opposites, as they
often approach things differently. But they aren’t without some commonalities. They
both prefer to gather information directly from the world around them, instead of
filtering it through internal mechanisms – neither type has much interest in theories.
While Virtuosos are more directly and immediately in touch with things, Sentinels
usually judge their experiences through remembering how things have always been.
They’re generally sticklers for rules and structures that support a traditional
perspective. Virtuosos relish the new and exciting, while Sentinels favor the stable.

#### Balanced Cooperation
Something Old, Something New
Ironically, Virtuosos and Sentinels most often complement each other when things
go wrong. Sometimes even the tried-and-true ways of doing things fail. This can be
disconcerting to predictability-loving Sentinels, but a compelling situation for
Virtuosos. In this ever-changing world, there are times when old methods no longer
work. There may be no other choice other than to develop a new way of doing things
– and there’s no type better equipped to help when things need to be done quickly
than Virtuosos.
Sometimes Virtuosos’ impulsive drive to find solutions can get off-track. Occasionally
the best answer, when a situation goes bad, is to retreat to a known starting place.
“Known places” are where Sentinels like to spend their time. Yes, sometimes, boldly
“winging it” can lead to effective solutions; however, there are also times when such
approaches create more problems than they solve.
From time to time, Sentinels can help Virtuosos “come home,” figuratively speaking.
Sentinels usually know how to repair a situation, or they know someone who does.
This can be of great help to Virtuosos who’ve wandered too far in the wrong direction.
If they rely on each other, rather than resist their differences, they can create
effective partnerships, whether formally or informally.
#### Unbalanced Cooperation
Clashing Styles
There are things about Virtuosos that drive Sentinels crazy, and, to a lesser degree,
vice versa. Virtuosos have the advantage because they usually go with the flow and

don’t worry much about what other people think. For Sentinels, however, their
problems with Virtuosos can be disturbing.
While Virtuosos have no problem cutting corners when necessary, Sentinels usually
dedicate their lives to making sure those corners remain intact – just the way they’ve
always been. Sentinels like things to be orderly and predictable. Virtuosos have a
freer attitude and love things that are new and interesting.
In life, Sentinels might prefer a favorite restaurant they go to most of the time, and
Virtuosos are likely to sample all the latest restaurants the day they open. At work,
Virtuosos can come across as reckless and undisciplined. To them, Sentinels might
appear boring and uninspired. While their opinions of each other are usually more
reactionary stereotypes than accurate assessments, such impressions can hinder
trust and open communications.
Varied Regard for the Status Quo
While keeping the status quo firmly in place seems essential to Sentinels, Virtuosos
have no problem doing what they feel is necessary to solve a problem regardless of
“how it’s always been done.” Sentinels see tried-and true means as the way to
guarantee a predictable end. Only a successful end concerns Virtuosos, and they may
use any means to get there.
It’s easy to see how Sentinels can perceive Virtuosos being at cross purposes with
them. They may devote an undue amount of time trying to get Virtuosos to walk a
straight line according to the rules. Meanwhile, Virtuosos may ignore Sentinels as
they do whatever they feel they must, either going in a straight line, serpentine, or
any direction that helps them master the situation. The possibilities for conflicts are
nearly endless.

The Value of Protocols
As the philosopher George Santayana wrote, “Those who cannot remember the past
are condemned to repeat it.” Virtuosos can be too quick to dismiss Sentinel
attachments to the past and settled protocols. But there can be much to learn from
their adherence to tradition and set standards. Virtuosos shouldn’t be afraid to ask
why they must do something a certain way – and Sentinels may want to avoid
justifying an act or decision by saying anything that resembles, “Because we’ve always
done it that way.”
Mutual Learning
Accepting Sentinels’ counsel may help avoid problems. Virtuosos may feel Sentinels
somehow limit their options, and it may even be true. However, Virtuosos aren’t likely
to permanently lose their spontaneity and edgy creativity if they play by the rules
occasionally. Relying on their Sentinel coworkers’ sensitivity to guidelines and
standards may help them stay around to create another day. (Sentinels may never
ask for it, but they’re always happy when someone shows appreciation. Doing so may
be a way to creating stronger work relationships between these two types.)

### Virtuoso–Explorer Cooperation
Obviously, Virtuosos and other Explorers typically find themselves “speaking the
same language.” Theoretical and conceptual problems, which thrill Analysts, are just
annoying to these types, who find no purpose in going there. Conversations about
solving problems or mastering a skill or situation are of greater interest. Virtuosos
and other Explorers gain energy and motivation from topics that revolve around
overcoming a problem and saving the day.
#### Balanced Cooperation
Whatever Gets the Job Done
Whatever gets the job done is what interests Virtuosos and Explorers who work
together. They may bypass rules, standards, or traditions that block the way of their
focus and strategies. Because of this, and their pronounced spontaneity and
independence, others often see them as risk-takers.
Virtuosos are comfortable with other Explorers also seeking to do things their way.
They like talking about the new and the different – if these discussions focus on
tangible matters and don’t wander into the arena of abstract theories. While people

# Professional Development
belonging to these types might appreciate talking about the capabilities of the new
iPhone, they’re likely to show less interest in a discussion of the future sociological
impact of constant digital communications worldwide. They might share a sense of
satisfaction if they figure out a unique use for the phone’s technology. The second
topic would be “just talk” to this action-oriented group.
Practical Innovation
Virtuosos and Explorers brought together within teams are likely to become
brainstorming powerhouses within their companies. They wouldn’t only resolve
problems, but also innovate and add to the growth of their organizations – but only
as it affects methods. The harnessed energy of Virtuosos and Explorers who bounce
ideas off each other would be a formidable asset to any organization.
Virtuoso and Explorer coworkers are likely to make any joint discoveries on the run
and are more likely to do so by applying their hands to problems rather than drawing
out diagrams and steps on a whiteboard.
#### Unbalanced Cooperation
But at What Cost?
The problems that occur between Virtuosos and Explorers likely have nothing to do
with mutual understanding. Instead, they may become stuck in their shared style,
without the benefit of insights from those who tend to deliberate a while before
acting. In business and life, it’s good to have varied personality types around for
balance.
On the off-chance that work becomes too “Explorer-heavy,” several factors can be
problematic. Grace Hopper, the late computer scientist and United States Navy Rear
Admiral, famously said, “It’s easier to apologize than to get permission.” Virtuosos

and Explorers often take this quote to heart, resulting too often in their running into
trouble with those in authority. Their willingness to take different paths and risks to
find unique solutions can be an asset in the workplace in small measure. However, it
shouldn’t go unchecked.
Just like their risk-taking, the often-complex relationships Virtuosos and Explorers
have with their coworkers may also need tempering. While they often enjoy and care
about other people, they just as often put practical solutions ahead of the feelings
and concerns of others. They may come across as having a brusque disregard for
their coworkers. They place people below solutions on their list of priorities. “It can’t
be helped,” is likely to be the shared excuse between Virtuosos and their Explorer
accomplices – provided they even discuss such matters. This might create a gulf
between them and other coworkers.
Solutions with a Small Perspective
Virtuosos and Explorers might also find their actions don’t align with the larger vision
of their organizations. Being detail-oriented and thinking in the present, these similar
types may disregard the larger view of their firms’ goals and focus. Just as it takes
one degree of a compass to send a ship hundreds of miles off course, so can a small
misguided solution potentially disrupt a company’s larger goals.
To Virtuosos working with other Explorers, their solutions may seem reasonable. To
their supervisors, it can appear as though they’re thumbing their noses at the vision
and values of their company. If not adjusted for, the immediacy of their shared
mindset can create a tunnel vision that ignores matters that are highly significant to
those in charge.

#### Rebalancing Cooperation
Commonalities and Moderating Influences
It’s always enjoyable when like minds work together. It’s likely Virtuoso and Explorer
coworkers enjoy simply spending time talking to each other, but they should also
consider using this time to brainstorm new and better ways of doing things. The
experience can go up a notch if they find something to tinker with while they’re
chatting.
Every personality type can benefit from the balance provided by other types. When
two types are so similar and comfortable with each other’s great ideas, a second
opinion from someone more “mainstream” can be useful. Even Virtuosos and their
Explorer teammates can grow as individuals and as teams when they expand their
comfort zones by exploring different approaches – even if they’re sometimes more
conventional than they prefer.
Virtuosos and Explorers who work together may find that they’re quite comfortable
exploring the “How?” questions involved in finding a solution. To rebalance their
efforts and keep them beneficial rather than harmful, learning to ask “Why?” may be
just as essential. This may again require a second opinion from someone who’s more
in touch with the bigger picture.
## Conclusion
Virtuosos can be paradoxes in the workplace. They’re happy to work on a team if
such collaboration assures results, but they’re also just as comfortable working
alone. Even within a team, they need to exercise independence. Their personal skills
must feel autonomous, as they’re essential to their happiness and motivate them to

actions, Virtuosos won’t be able to access that part of themselves that responds in
healthy ways to challenges.
To thrive in the workplace, Virtuosos must find a niche that’s their own. Most are
good at this, having practiced it while dealing with school systems focused on more
conventional things than they, as young people, could relate to. While not always
adept at commitment in any traditional sense in their personal lives, once they find
their career niche, they’re usually ready to settle down for the duration. This allows
them to become highly skilled and often specialized – making them valued workers
and professionals in many fields.

# Final Words
Creatively Practical
Virtuosos strive to be experts at tinkering and getting things to work. This can be
done in obvious ways – as a mechanic who fixes engines, for example – or in less
obvious ways, such as offering advice or counsel. They’re rare Introverts who don’t
mind collaborating as they seek to satisfy their curiosity, and they like leaving things
better than they found them. When they decide to act, they go “all in.”
In this final section, we tend to encourage most types to make a plan that uses their
strengths. But the truth is, Virtuosos aren’t likely to heed such advice. Instead, these
independent innovators follow their curiosity and need to make an impact wherever
they lead. It’s these intrinsic motivators that guide them to their next adventures in
life. This personality type rarely sees a complex and theoretical plan as necessary to
the action they crave. Priorities come naturally to them, and they don’t need to spell
them out ahead of time.

to improve something. Virtuosos can enhance their lives by working more on their
