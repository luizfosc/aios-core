# The Puzzle of Personality

When they receive their personality test results on our website, most people’s first

question is, “What do these letters mean?” We are of course referring to those

mysterious acronyms – INTJ-A, for example, or ENFP-T. As you may have already

learned from the type descriptions or articles on the website, the five letters of

these acronyms each refer to a specific trait, with certain trait combinations

forming various types and type groups. But before we discuss those traits in depth,

let’s explore their historical foundations.

Since the dawn of time, humans have drawn up schematics to describe and

categorize our personalities. From the four temperaments of the ancient

civilizations to the latest advances in psychology, we have been driven to fit the

variables and complexities of human personality into well-defined models.

Although we are still some time away from being able to do that, the current

models account for our most important personality traits and can predict our

behavior with a high degree of accuracy.

Personality is just one of many factors that guide our behavior, however. Our

actions are also influenced by our environment, our experiences, and our individual

goals. This profile describes how people belonging to a specific personality type are

likely to behave. We will outline indicators and tendencies, however, not definitive

guidelines or answers. Significant differences can exist even among people who

share a personality type. The information in this profile is meant to inspire personal

growth and an improved understanding of yourself and your relationships – not to

be taken as gospel.

Our approach has its roots in two different philosophies. One dates back to the

early 20th century and was the brainchild of Carl Gustav Jung, the father of analytical

psychology. Jung’s theory of psychological types is perhaps the most influential

creation in personality typology, and it has inspired many different theories. One of

Jung’s key contributions was the development of the concept of Introversion and

Extraversion – he theorized that each of us falls into one of these two categories,

either focusing on the internal world (Introvert) or the outside world (Extravert).

Besides Introversion and Extraversion, Jung coined the concept of so-called

cognitive functions, separated into Judging or Perceiving categories. According to

Jung, each person prefers one of these cognitive functions and may most naturally

rely on it in everyday situations.

In the 1920s, Jung’s theory was noticed by Katharine Cook Briggs, who later co-

authored one of the most popular personality indicators used today, the Myers-

Briggs Type Indicator® (MBTI®). Briggs was a teacher with an avid interest in

personality typing, having developed her own type theory before learning of Jung’s

writings. Together with her daughter, Isabel Briggs Myers, they developed a

convenient way to describe the order of each person’s Jungian preferences – this is

how four-letter acronyms were born.

Of course, this is just a very simplified description of the Myers-Briggs theory.

Readers interested in learning more should read Gifts Differing: Understanding

Personality Type by Isabel Briggs Myers. As we define personality traits and types

differently in our model, we will not go deeper into Jungian concepts or related

theories in this profile.

Due to its simplicity and ease of use, the four-letter naming model is now shared by

a number of diverse theories and approaches, including Socionics, Keirsey

Temperament Sorter®, and Linda Berens’ Interaction Styles, among many others.

While the acronyms used by these theories may be identical or very similar,

however, their meanings do not always overlap. As a result, if you meet five people

who say, “I am an INFJ,” their understanding of what that means may vary

significantly.

Regardless of its structure, any type-based theory will struggle to describe or

characterize people whose scores lie near the dividing line. A different way to look

at personalities is through the lens of a trait-based rather than a type-based model.

What do we mean by that? Instead of creating an arbitrary number of categories

and attempting to fit people within them, a trait-based model simply studies the

degree to which people exhibit certain traits.

You may have heard the term Ambivert, which is a perfect example in this case.

Ambiversion means that someone falls in the middle of the Introversion-

Extraversion scale, being neither too outgoing nor too withdrawn. Trait-based

theories would simply say that an Ambivert is moderately Extraverted or

moderately Introverted and leave it at that, without assigning a personality type.

A trait-based approach makes it easier to reliably measure correlations between

personality traits and other characteristics – for example, political attitudes. This is

why trait-based approaches dominate psychometric research, but that’s more or

less the only area where these approaches are dominant. Because they don’t offer

types or categorizations, trait-based theories don’t translate as well as type-based

theories into specific recommendations and takeaways. Assigned categories such

as Extravert or Introvert may be limiting, but they allow us to conceptualize human

personality and create theories about why we do what we do – something that a

more scientifically reliable but colorless statement, such as you are 37% Extraverted,

simply cannot do.

With our model, we’ve combined the best of both worlds. We use the acronym

format introduced by Myers-Briggs for its simplicity and convenience, with an extra

letter to accommodate five rather than four scales. However, unlike Myers-Briggs or

other theories based on the Jungian model, we have not incorporated Jungian

concepts such as cognitive functions, or their prioritization. Jungian concepts are

very difficult to measure and evaluate scientifically, so we’ve instead chosen to

rework and rebalance the dimensions of personality called the Big Five personality

traits, a model that is very popular in psychological and social research.

Our personality types are based on five independent spectrums, with all letters in

the type code (e.g. INFJ-A) referring to one of the two sides of the corresponding

spectrum. We’ll talk more about these concepts in the next chapter. This approach

has allowed us to achieve high test accuracy while also retaining the ability to

define and describe distinct personality types.

During the last few years, we have conducted more than 800 studies to uncover

trends and correlations between personality traits and various behaviors. Many of

these studies will be referenced in this profile, and additional information can be

found in our online Academy. Our goal is to give you access to our sources without

repeating ourselves or overwhelming you with information. If you are particularly

interested in a specific study or its statistical characteristics, please feel free to

contact us via our website, and we will do our best to provide additional

information.

With that aside, let’s dig deeper into the five personality aspects!

## Five Personality Aspects

### Mind: Introverted (I) vs. Extraverted (E)

The distinction between Introverts and Extraverts may be the oldest notion in the

history of personality theories. It has long been observed that some people are

expressive and outgoing, while others are reserved, quiet, and more comfortable

alone. The first group recharges by engaging with the external world and

communicating with other people, whereas members of the second group are

energized by spending time alone and connecting with their own inner world.

We focus on these differences in our first scale, which we call Mind. This scale –

which is based on a person’s level of expressiveness and the degree to which he or

she seeks external stimulation – determines how we see and approach the outside

world, including people, objects, and activities. This scale correlates with a number

of other personality traits, such as willingness to volunteer, desire to engage in

thrill-seeking activities, and romantic assertiveness, to name just a few.

Introverted individuals (the I letter

in the type acronym). You might

associate Introversion with being

clumsy or antisocial – but our

studies show a far more complex

picture.    Generally       speaking,

Introverts do not seek or require much external stimulation. As you might expect,

an Introvert requires less communication with others than an Extravert would, but

an Introvert’s preference for less stimulation can also influence his or her hobbies,

political attitudes, and even eating or drinking habits. To give a practical example,

our studies have found that Introverts are less likely than Extraverts to enjoy coffee

and energy drinks.

But         what     about     Extraverts?

Compared             with       Introverts,

Extraverts are more interested in

example, and they are likelier to wear bright and colorful clothes. Extraverts not

only tolerate external stimuli, however; they actually need these stimuli in order to

support their own energy.

Extraverts are often energetic and willing to take the lead, especially in social

situations. They enjoy pushing themselves to the limit and challenging themselves

and those around them. Unlike Introverts, who are more cautious in their approach,

Extraverts are likely to feel that they can handle life’s challenges without much

forethought. Whether that turns out to be true depends on many other

circumstances, but overall, Extraverts tend to be more proactive in experiencing –

and embracing – the world around them.

Worldwide, the three most Extraverted countries are Oman (61.66%), Yemen

(59.44%), and Saudi Arabia (58.95%). The most Introverted countries are Japan

(59.88%), Lithuania (55.85%), and Portugal (54.25%). In the United States, New York

(50.92%), Mississippi (50.87%), and Illinois (50.64%) top the list for Extraverts, while

West Virginia (53.62%), Alaska (53.6%), and Maine (53.41%) report the highest

percentages of Introverts. Interestingly, if the District of Columbia were included in

the comparisons, it would easily top the Extraverted list. In our study, the average

Extraversion score for the capital city’s 23,310 respondents was 53.06% –

significantly above New York, its closest competitor.

Executives are Extraverted – they tend feel recharged after spending time in the

company of other people, and consequently often have excellent social skills. As

Extraverts, Executives enjoy taking the lead and speaking their mind, often going

ahead and voicing their opinion as they develop it. People with this personality trait

tend to instinctively connect with others, regardless of their background or

personality, making them popular and well-liked in many circles.

### Energy: Intuitive (N) vs. Observant (S)

Energy is the second scale in our model, and in our opinion it is the most important.

While the other four scales determine how you interact with the world (Mind), make

decisions (Nature), schedule your activities (Tactics), or react to external feedback

(Identity), the Energy scale actually determines how you see the world and what

kind of information you focus on.

All personality types can be divided into those that favor the Intuitive energy style

and those that favor the Observant energy style. Intuitive individuals tend to be

visionary, interested in ideas and abstractions, and attracted to novelty. Observant

individuals, on the other hand, prefer facts, concrete and observable things, and

the tried and true.

It is important to stress that this

scale has nothing to do with how we

absorb information: Intuitive and

Observant     types   use   their   five

senses equally well. Instead, this

scale shows whether we focus on

what     is     possible      (making

connections intuitively) or what is

real (observing the environment). If you are familiar with the Big Five personality

traits, you might recognize this scale as a reworking of the openness to experience

concept, with a focus on preference for (and tolerance of) novelty and ambiguity.

Individuals with the Intuitive trait rely on their imaginations and think in terms of

ideas and possibilities. They dream, fantasize, and question why things happen the

way they do, always feeling slightly detached from the actual, concrete world.

Although they observe other people and events, their minds remain directed both

inwards and somewhere beyond – always questioning, wondering, and making

connections. When all is said and done, Intuitive types believe in novelty, in the

open mind, and in never-ending improvement.

In one 16Personalities study, we asked people whether they wish they had been

born in the Age of Discovery (roughly speaking, the 15th through the 18th centuries).

Our results showed that Intuitive types were much more willing to give up the

convenience, comfort, and predictability of modern life in exchange for the

excitement of exploration, distant civilizations, and the undiscovered mysteries of

the New World.

In contrast, individuals with the

Observant trait focus on the actual

world and things happening around

them. Preferring to see, touch, feel,

and experience, they are happy to

leave theories and possibilities to

others. They keep their feet on the

ground and focus on the present,

instead of wondering why or when something might happen. Consequently, people

with this trait tend to be better at dealing with facts, tools, and concrete objects as

opposed to brainstorming, theorizing about future events, or handling abstract

theories. Observant types are also significantly better at focusing on just one thing

at a time instead of juggling multiple activities.

The Energy scale influences communication style as well. Intuitive individuals talk

about ideas and have no difficulties with allusions or reading between the lines,

while Observant types focus on facts and practical matters. This is why Intuitive

types may find it challenging to understand someone with the Observant trait, and

vice versa. Intuitive types may think that Observant individuals are materialistic,

unimaginative, and simplistic, and Observant types may see their Intuitive

conversation partner as impractical, naïve, and absent-minded. These assumptions

and biases can be quite damaging, and it takes a mature person to get past them.

Geographically, the Middle East and Asia have the largest percentage of Observant

individuals, with 8 countries in the top 10 list. Saudi Arabia (71.94%), Oman (71.03%),

and Yemen (69.76%) have the top scores among Observant countries, while Nepal

(48.92%), Albania (48.51%) and Maldives (48.44%) top the Intuitive table.

In the United States, North Dakota (59.43%), Minnesota (59.23%), and Iowa (59.04%)

are the most Observant states. On the opposite side of the table, we have Nevada

(43.92%), Rhode Island (43.75%), and West Virginia (43.47%).

People with the Observant personality trait, like Executives, spend most of their

time focusing outward, observing concrete details about their surroundings. They

excel at remembering various details, noticing discrepancies and maintaining a

grounded perspective. Executives aren’t that concerned about what might happen

or why it happened – they focus on what is happening. Executives keep improving

these skills throughout their lives, which makes them diligent, keen-eyed observers.

This trait is also one of the reasons why Executives find partners easily: many traits

shared by people with the Observant trait, such as reliability, meticulousness and

desire for security, are often considered attractive.

The Observant trait comes with drawbacks: with their mental resources directed

outward, Executives may struggle to make sense of their intuition. A preference for

concrete facts can make dealing with abstract, theoretical matters, or jumping from

one idea to another difficult. Keeping up with the facts comes naturally to

Executives, but the same can’t be said for brainstorming possibilities.

### Nature: Thinking (T) vs. Feeling (F)

The third scale, which we call Nature, determines how we make decisions and cope

with emotions. While we all have feelings, there are significant differences in how

different types react to them. This scale influences a number of areas of our lives,

particularly our interactions with

other people.

People with the Thinking trait seek

logic      and   rational    arguments,

relying on their head rather than

their heart. They do their best to

safeguard, manage, and conceal

their emotions. “Whatever happens,

you must always keep a cool head”

– this is the motto of Thinking individuals. These types are not cold-blooded or

indifferent, however. People with the Thinking trait are often just as emotional as

those with the Feeling trait, but they subdue or override their feelings with their

rational logic.

In several of our studies, we analyzed the attitudes of different personality types

toward philanthropy and charitable causes. Thinking types are significantly less

likely to engage in charitable giving or to be touched by charities’ emotional appeals.

Does this mean that they are unwilling to help? Not necessarily. It turns out that

Thinking personalities don’t believe that giving money to charity is the best way to

make a difference. They may look for other ways to be of assistance – for example,

investing in education for the disadvantaged.

In contrast, people with the Feeling trait follow their hearts and care little about

hiding their emotions. From their perspective, we should not be afraid to listen to

our innermost feelings and share them with the world. These individuals tend to be

compassionate, sensitive, and highly emotional. They would rather cooperate than

compete, although they are not

naïve or easily swayed.

In fact, people with the Feeling trait

are likely to fight tooth and nail for

their values and beliefs. For many

Feeling types, principles and ideals

are much more important than, say,

professional success. Although this

perspective might not seem rational,

Feeling types generally adhere to their own highly individualized – and perfectly

valid – logic.

Worldwide, Iran (51.91%), Georgia (50.74%), and Albania (48.94%) report highest

scores on the Thinking side of the scale. On the Feeling side, we have Singapore

(61.41%), Australia (60.1%), and New Zealand (59.98%).

In the U.S., northern states tend to be slightly more Feeling, but not significantly so.

One interesting example is the contrast between Utah and Nevada: two

neighboring states that are each first on opposite sides of the scale. In Utah, the

average score is 61.19% on the Feeling side, well above Minnesota (59.61%) and

Idaho (59.54%), which take second and third place, respectively. Nevada, on the

other hand, is the most Thinker-heavy state in our study, with its 34,576

respondents getting an average score of 42.99% on the Thinking side of the

spectrum. While that still means Feeling personality types outnumber Thinking

types significantly, we rarely see such differences between neighboring regions.

Other top Thinking states include Delaware (42.71%) and New Mexico (42.7%).

Executives have the Thinking trait. These types trust and prioritize logic, and they

rely on rational arguments over emotional appeals. They try to keep their true

feelings and emotions deep below the surface, and focus instead on what can be

assessed, compared, and verified.

Individuals with the Thinking trait, like Executives, bring tremendous value to

enterprises   that   need    careful   assessment       and    calculation   to   succeed.

Unfortunately, for all the good it represents, the Thinking trait may distance

Executives from much of the population, as connecting at an emotional level takes

more effort for these intellectual types.

### Tactics: Judging (J) vs. Prospecting (P)

The Tactics scale determines how we approach planning and how we handle

available options. The implications reach well beyond our calendars, however. At its

core, this scale determines our attitudes toward certainty and structure in our lives.

People with the Judging trait do not

like to keep their options open.

They would rather prepare five

different contingency plans than

wait for challenges to arise. These

individuals enjoy clarity and closure,

always sticking to the plan rather

than going with the flow. It’s as if

Judging types keep a mental checklist, and once a decision is crossed off that list, it

is not open to reassessment. This mentality applies as much to grocery shopping as

it does to life goals, like buying a house.

Individuals with the Judging trait tend to have a strong work ethic, putting their

duties and responsibilities above all else. Our research indicates that Judging types

are also strict when it comes to law and order. Of course, this doesn’t mean that

Prospecting individuals are lawbreakers, but if you see someone go out of his or

her way to use a crosswalk, that person is likely a Judging type.

In contrast, Prospecting individuals are flexible and relaxed when it comes to

dealing with both expected and unexpected challenges. They are always scanning

for opportunities and options, willing to jump at a moment’s notice. People with

this trait understand that life is full of possibilities, and they are reluctant to commit

to something that might prove an

inferior option in the future. They

also focus more on what makes

them    happy     than    what     their

parents, employers, or teachers

expect. If a specific task is not particularly important or interesting, a Prospecting

individual will always come up with something better to do.

Looking at the geographical data, the global Judging-Prospecting chart has

interesting similarities with the Extraverted-Introverted one. While these scales do

not influence each other from a statistical perspective, many of the countries

topping the Extraverted-Introverted charts have correspondingly high Judging-

Prospecting scores. At the top of the Judging table we have Oman (58.05%), Yemen

(57.78%), and Kuwait (55.8%), while the most Prospecting countries are Japan

(55.21%), Nepal (53.74%), and Lithuania (53.62%).

The map of the United States, however, does not show significant overlaps between

these two scales. On the Judging side of the spectrum, we have South Carolina

(51.05%), Alabama (50.97%), and Tennessee (50.8%). On the other side, the most

Prospecting states are Alaska (51.2%), Vermont (50.86%), and West Virginia (50.76%).

Executives are Judging types – they’re decisive, choose security over freedom to

improvise, and avoid uncertainty through decision-making. True to their name,

Executives are masters at logistics and execution. This makes them excellent

administrators, both able and willing to dedicate time for managing complex

operations.

People with this personality trait, like Executives, are more interested in the

practical side of work than the theoretical. They tend to avoid developing grand

plans like some other types do. Rather than dreaming big only to accomplish little,

these types work at a small, achievable scale.

### Identity: Assertive (-A) vs. Turbulent (-T)

The last scale, Identity, affects all others, reflecting how confident we are in our

abilities and decisions. In a way, this scale acts as an internal sensor, reacting to the

input we get from our environments – for instance, success or failure, feedback

from others, or pressure caused by unexpected events. The Mind and Identity

scales are the alpha and the omega of our model, acting as an external shell that

we wear in all of our interactions with the outside world. Later, we will discuss the

four possible combinations of these traits, which we call “Strategies,” but in the

meantime, let’s take an in-depth

look at the Identity scale.

Assertive    individuals      are   self-

assured,     even-tempered,         and

resistant to stress. They refuse to

worry too much, and they don’t

push themselves too hard when it

comes to achieving goals. Similarly,

they are unlikely to spend much

time thinking about past actions or choices. According to Assertive types, what’s

done is done and there is little point in analyzing it. Not surprisingly, people with

this trait are generally satisfied with their lives, and they feel confident in their

ability to handle challenging and

unexpected situations.

In contrast, Turbulent individuals

are self-conscious and sensitive to

stress. They experience a wide range of emotions and tend to be success-driven,

perfectionistic, and eager to improve. Always feeling the need to do more, have

more, and be more, Turbulent types may forget how exhausting this dissatisfaction

can be – both for themselves and for the people around them.

While the Assertive variant may seem more positive on the surface, that is not

necessarily the case. Turbulent individuals are more willing than Assertive types to

change jobs if they feel stuck in their current roles, and they think deeply about the

direction of their lives. Turbulent individuals may also outperform Assertive types in

certain instances, because they may care more about the outcome. The willingness

of Turbulent types to push themselves may ultimately enable them to achieve their

goals.

Worldwide, Japan is by far the most Turbulent country, with an average score of

57.48% on the Turbulent side. Italy (53.8%) and Brunei (52.89%) come in second

and third, respectively. On the Assertive side of the spectrum, we have Uganda

(57.91%), Barbados (57.11%), and Nigeria (57.01%).

In the United States, there is a clear east-west divide, with the East Coast being

significantly more Turbulent. West Virginia (49.3%) takes the lead on the Turbulent

side, followed by Rhode Island (48.84%) and Maine (48.73%). The most Assertive

states are New Mexico (54.7%), Colorado (54.66%), and Hawaii (54.28%).

Type Groups Now you know what each type consists of. But how do they fit together?

Our system has two layers: the first (inner) one defines our Roles, the second (outer)

one, our Strategies.

### Roles

The Role layer determines our goals, interests, and preferred activities. Each of the

four Roles covers a set of personality types that are very similar, and we will use

these groups later in this profile to draw contrasts and similarities between

personalities. They also serve to highlight the importance we have placed on the

Energy trait. Each personality type will share either the Observant or the Intuitive

trait with all other members of their group, as well as one other key trait. Executives

belong to the Sentinel Role group, along with three other personality types:

Logisticians, Defenders, and Consuls.

#### Analysts

Shared traits: Intuitive and Thinking

The personality types in the Analyst Role – Architects, Logicians, Commanders, and

Debaters – embrace rationality, excelling in intellectual and technological pursuits.

The Thinking trait makes Analysts exacting, and the Intuitive trait lets them apply

their minds to almost anything, with their imaginations aiding calculated, strategic

thinking, or seeing just how far the rabbit hole goes with a crazy scheme or thought

experiment.

These personalities are driven to understand and create, and have no problem

switching between speculative musing and frank, solutions-oriented approaches to

whatever problems are at hand. Broad, intelligent vision gives Analysts an

ingenious air, but they can become overwhelmed and scattered if they don’t learn

to hone these abilities and use them to advance a worthwhile cause or project.

The biggest pitfall is that their intellect can give them a false sense of

accomplishment. They tend to prefer the world of ideas to the sober reality of

follow-through, and by assuming the role of critic instead of participant, Analysts

sometimes risk being functionally outpaced by those who simply sit down and do

the work. They can earn themselves the unsavory title of “armchair analysts” while

those with more real-world experience continue to create real results. Analyst

personality types can lack a proper respect for those of simpler vision and

expression, whatever their background, and it can hold them back.

Analysts value their intellect above all else, and it can be a challenge convincing

them it’s worth checking their many hypotheses with an experiment or proper

evidence to be sure. To many Analysts, if something makes sense in their minds,

that’s as good as proved, and a great deal faster. Analysts can be brilliant, but

they’re still human; under the influence of ego, these personality types can get in

the habit of advancing clever opinions instead of objective facts.

Analysts’ positive self-identity stems largely from their formidable drive to learn,

and their clever and sharply witty banter can make them excellent debate partners.

Often insatiable readers as well, Analysts can be found stockpiling books,

questioning teachers, and driving conversations in forums across the world wide

web. These types prefer their own processes and pace, and are energized by

exercising their minds on their own time.

Analysts are also relentless self-improvers. Once they’ve recognized a flaw, they

apply all of this cold rationality, honest reflection, imagination, and desire for

results to make it right. Analysts’ forceful, imaginative vision, when trained and

focused, enables these personalities to conceive and accomplish things most

thought impossible.

#### Diplomats

Shared traits: Intuitive and Feeling

Diplomats – Advocates, Mediators, Protagonists, and Campaigners – tend to be

warm, caring, and generous individuals, shining in diplomacy and counselling.

These personality types promote cooperation and harmony, tolerating discord only

as a step towards positive change. Their perceptive abilities seemingly go deeper

than the traditional senses – it’s as if they have a full set of tuning forks in their

hearts that can resonate with people’s emotional states, and this empathetic

sensitivity creates deep connections with others.

This is not as mystical as it can seem. Diplomat personalities deeply value

understanding, and a lifetime of self-reflection grants them insights into others’

motivations that they might not even understand themselves. Diplomats can use

this to shape the way others feel, but it would be out of character for them to use

this for ill.

Diplomats’ people skills nurture friendship and healing with pure, genuine empathy.

Causing distress feels to them like wounding their own psyche, so they prefer to

gently nudge loved ones and strangers alike in a positive direction. Diplomats find

the ideals of humanism rewarding: kindness, understanding, altruism, and growth

warm them like a bonfire on a chilly autumn day. These personality types envision a

prismatic, kind world.

The Intuitive trait plays a big part in this optimism, but it also makes Diplomats

sensitive to injustices that would jeopardize that ideal world. They can be

aggressive against forces they perceive as evil, and are capable of steely action

when they feel they have moral high ground. Diplomats’ sensitive emotions may

flare to outright combativeness if provoked; with self-righteous momentum and

weighty ideals, these personality types can steamroll others. If they let inspiration

trump rationality unchecked, they can sometimes go too far, damaging the very

causes they hold dear, however justified the outrage.

Tempering this passion with cold practicality is a challenge for Diplomats. They are

often reluctant to make and carry out tough decisions or plans lacking in empathy,

and often struggle with carrying out a plan at all. They find greater satisfaction in

abstract self-exploration and understanding the issues that affect those least able

to defend themselves than mundane accomplishments and day-to-day tasks.

Diplomats emphasize experience and understanding rather than calculable

achievement.

Among friends and family, Diplomat personalities can range from quietly caring to

brightly gregarious, so long as they can pursue the mutual exchange of thoughts

and feelings they value so highly. But they can overextend themselves in this more

personal aspect of their lives as well – emotionally, physically, or even financially.

Diplomats’ open hearts make them vulnerable when others aren’t as considerate,

though they wouldn’t have it any other way. Someone must trust first, and they will

almost always be the volunteer.

Diplomats embrace travel as readily as anyone – they like exploring other cultures

and people’s colorful ways. These personality types are creatively inclined, often

practicing artistic expression in diverse forms, and see magical beauty where others

see the hum-drum of daily life. For a group with such progressive attitudes, they

also appreciate seeing things as they are, a pure expression of a way of thinking

and being. Diplomats can be passionately inspired by experiences: music, cuisine,

theater, nature, and anything else that goes beyond the obvious can rise to divine

art in their eyes.

Like a gardener planting in fertile earth, Diplomats seed the world around them

with progressive change and gentle beauty. Diplomats feel connected to forces they

may not fully understand, a deep sense of faith that may express itself, if not always

through traditional religion, as spiritual belief. Diplomats pursue things with an

underlying idealism and a sense of higher purpose; activism, spirituality, healing,

the arts, and charity are common interests for these personalities. They have a

conviction to serve a greater good, motivated by intangible rewards felt in the heart

and soul.

#### Sentinels

Shared traits: Observant and Judging

Sentinels – Logisticians, Defenders, Executives, and Consuls – are cooperative and

practical. Their grounded approach helps them feel comfortable with who they are,

defining themselves not by individualism, but by character and competence. These

personality types seek order, security, and stability, and tend to work hard to

maintain the way of things, leading to a deserved reputation as the core of any

group or organization, from family to church to the office and the factory floor.

These types embrace teamwork, but expect the same performance and

respectability from others that they strive for themselves. Sentinels often dislike

strident nonconformity because they envision progress through collaboration and

hard work within a known set of rules – to let everyone do their own thing on a

whim would be anarchy. Sentinels prefer proven methods and honest

accomplishment to self-indulgent exploration and esoteric ideas. Because these

personalities are deeply prudent, they also tend to be self-motivated, and rarely

need external inspiration to be productive.

Their grounded perspective leaves little room for random musings, but Sentinels

are not unimaginative. They experiment for anticipated gain rather than intellectual

thrills, and excel in making challenging situations work. Revolution does not appeal

to people from this Role, who tend to learn from the past and remain loyal to the

tested truths and traditions they were brought up with. Where some see fascinating

philosophy, Sentinels may see preposterousness: these personality types favor

practiced methodology over abstract theory.

Having authority allows Sentinels to practice their virtues. Effective in leadership

roles, these types motivate others by energetic example and feel satisfaction from

guiding a well-functioning group – they tend to make excellent teachers, managers,

and community officials, as well as parents and hosts. These personalities are

meticulous and traditional, excelling in logistical and administrative fields with clear

hierarchies and rules as well. Opting for successful completion whenever they can,

Sentinels can be controlling, but gladly compromise when needed to get things

done.

Sentinels feel rewarded by shepherding others, and enjoy coordinating and sharing

fun social experiences with friends and family. They believe it is their responsibility

to give their families safe, happy lives that prepare them for the real world. An

appreciation of strong relationships allows them to share generously with those

they respect and love – these types feel bolstered by having reliable people in their

lives, and will do what they can to make sure they hold up their end of the bargain.

Birthdays, dinner parties, and camping trips are delightful events under their

enthusiastic direction.

These personality types are happiest without drama, though their admirably

stubborn loyalty can certainly attract it if they feel they need to stand by a friend,

regardless of whether they’re in the right or wrong. Inconsistent people can test

Sentinels’ tolerance and provoke harsh judgement. Sentinels tend to prefer

predictability over novelty and familiar pleasures more than cutting-edge

excitement. It can be difficult for Sentinel personalities to accept people who lack

their studious ideals, but they often respond as engaged and caring mentors to

those who wish to grow in that direction.

#### Explorers

Shared traits: Observant and Prospecting

Explorers – Virtuosos, Adventurers, Entrepreneurs, and Entertainers – possess a

self-reliant mix of enthusiasm, quick thinking, and ingenuity that can lead to

impressive    personal    and   professional     accomplishments.   Comfortable     with

uncertainty and minimally concerned with preparation, these personality types

simply adapt and overcome as events present themselves. Explorers’ flexibility

helps them make snap decisions in the moment, and they’re unlikely to dwell on

the future or the past.

They aren’t obsessed with precise detail (unless they’re really in the zone, in which

case they can muster a level of precision and focus that would make a seasoned

engineer blush). Usually, they require workability rather than perfection. Explorer

personalities are utilitarian masters of diverse tools and techniques, ranging from

instruments and engines to the art of persuasion, and they distinguish themselves

in crises, crafts, and sales.

This versatile decisiveness doesn’t mean commitment, however. Explorers can

change their minds with minimal regret or second-guessing. People in this Role

dislike monotony, and often feel tempted to stray from obligations in favor of

entertaining new things. They experiment with many interests and live and breathe

whatever they connect with – for a time. If these personality types sometimes leave

business unfinished, let clutter build up, or misplace important things, it’s because

they’ve moved on to something fresh.

Explorers enjoy being free of obligations, where they can indulge themselves or

their interests on their own time. An Explorer might design and build their own

dream house, enthralled with the process, but end up procrastinating on simple

repairs after a few years. They are highly motivated when steering themselves

through something interesting, but don’t like being restrained by anything

“mandatory.”

Their relaxed, free-form attitude makes Explorer personalities socially dynamic as

well. They often seek out people and experiences that cater to their senses, finding

more pleasure in stimulation than planning. They happily approach appealing

strangers and interesting experiences, and when things get in the way of a good

time, they apply their ingenuity to getting around it. Explorers want to see what

happens next!

The same themes run through their friendships and families, though these

relationships have much more staying power than most other interests. Rather

than forcing relationships into or out of existence though, Explorers let things flow

naturally according to their desires. Compatible people simply become a part of

their lives. These personality types tend to be individualistic and freedom-loving,

going their own way with little regard for naysayers, but they do enjoy knowing that

the people they trust will be around in the end.

Explorers are highly spontaneous, connecting with and adapting to their

environment in a way that is almost childlike in its wonder and sheer fun. It can be

a challenge for them to work for things with a distant or unexciting payoff, as they

need a sense of immediacy to feel truly engaged, but when they are passionate,

Explorer personalities can move heaven and earth.

### Strategies

The Strategy layer reflects our preferred ways of doing things and achieving our

goals. There are four strategies: Confident Individualism, People Mastery, Constant

Improvement, and Social Engagement. Depending on their type variant, Executives

fall under either People Mastery (Assertive Executives) or Social Engagement

(Turbulent Executives).

#### Confident Individualism

Shared traits: Introverted and Assertive

Confident Individualists typically trust in themselves, and they often embrace

solitude to pursue their own interests rather than seeking out social activity.

Fascinated by personal projects, people following this Strategy often have an

impressive range of skills and interesting ideas. But projects are usually only

pursued for their own merit – Confident Individualists tend to feel that social

displays and bragging are time and energy wasted. These personality types are

proud of who they are, what they know, and what they can do, but they don’t feel

the need to prove themselves to others.

Confident Individualists engage their internal inspiration instead of searching for

motivation outside themselves. They favor privacy, and aren’t particularly fond of

interacting with society, whether in a strictly social sense or when embracing

broader societal goals. These personalities often favor substance over superficiality

and personal honesty over playing along, and prefer a utilitarian approach, even

when pursuing esoteric goals.

This utilitarianism also means that Confident Individualists are not easily drawn into

emotional drama. They hold their own opinions firmly, but tend to see little reason

to try to convince others. When drama does arise, these personality types express

their truth with little concern over whether they’ll cause friction or offense.

Confident Individualists tend to endorse self-reliance over cooperation, and are

rarely pressured into agreeing with or lending support to others unless they believe

in the cause.

This relaxed self-assurance means that Confident Individualists may not push their

boundaries. Their live-and-let-live attitude goes both ways: they don’t need to

convince others, and they tend to expect others to return the favor. While highly

capable, they can miss information and opportunities that challenge their views

because they simply don’t place much importance on factoring in others’ approval.

They can be tolerant in disagreement though, respecting others’ individualism just

as they respect their own.

When these personality types form friendships, they tend to be strong and honest.

Because these types don’t feel like they need other people’s approval, their loyalty

and affection is a deliberate expression of affection. Impressing a Confident

Individualist earns their respect and care, and these types make for dedicated,

passionate friends.

Self-reliance is central for these types, and they handle difficult situations well

because they tend to be emotionally secure, bold, and resistant to stress. They

rarely seek leadership or the spotlight, but when they do find themselves in these

positions, these personalities lead by an example of self-determination and

uncomplicated honesty – they appreciate grace, class, and form, and rarely seek to

impress by appearances alone. When acting with knowledge and wisdom,

Confident Individualists can be noble pillars of strength.

#### People Mastery

Shared traits: Extraverted and Assertive

People Masters are highly stress-resistant, maintaining confidence through life’s

challenges, social pressures, and any questions of their capabilities. These

personality types seek social contact and have solid communication skills, feeling at

ease when relying on or directing other people. People Masters’ mantra is fearless

engagement – to be is to do. This is not a group known for its timid opinions or

idleness.

People Masters feel rewarded by stimulation and challenging experiences. They like

travelling to see things, people, and places, where they can experience a richer

understanding of the world around them. These personalities like sampling new

foods, lifestyles, and cultures; even things they don’t end up liking can be

entertaining excitement for them.

Sometimes, though, People Masters are too confident. Insulated against self-doubt

and the need to prove themselves, they don’t always test, or even consider, their

own limits or ambitions. They can slip into a pattern of endless fun and comfort-

seeking, rolling their eyes at those who press themselves towards more

intimidating goals. At the other end of the spectrum, these personality types can

just as easily ignore sensible caution, putting themselves in extraordinary and

dangerous situations, confident they can find their way through.

Most fall somewhere in the middle, embracing healthy ambition and competition

so long as they play a meaningful part in social circles and leadership. Social

interaction plays a big part in People Masters’ happiness. While not dependent on

people’s approval for emotional security, they appreciate acclaim for their

accomplishments and bright personalities, and can be a touch showy from time to

time. These types are self-confident, but they are often lost without people to lead,

laugh with, and love.

Their charisma, self-assurance and boisterousness can sometimes be trying

qualities. People Masters prefer cooperation, but have few qualms about

dominating their opposition to achieve their goals, boldly promoting themselves

and using their social skills to get ahead. This Strategy understands people’s needs,

but can just as easily use this as a weapon rather than a tool of compassion if they

have their sights set on something big.

People Masters can compromise when needed, but these personality types tend to

be better talkers than listeners. They can be very demanding, criticizing people’s

performance regardless of their feelings because they expect no less of themselves.

That said, these types are moderate in their disagreements because their ego and

confidence aren’t at stake the way they often are for Turbulent Strategies. People

Masters are not fond of grudges, content to let bygones be bygones. They are more

likely to be socially idealistic, with the intent of bringing people together to make

things happen – petty grievances just get in the way.

People Masters’ charismatic virtues shine in social contexts, and these types have

little fear of rejection and a passion for inclusion. They prefer to take the lead, but

sharing the journey through other people’s eyes enhances their own enjoyment as

well. People Masters share in all aspects of people’s lives, making these

personalities strong, honest friends, parents, spouses, and colleagues, serving as

wellsprings of energy, joy, and mutual success.

#### Constant Improvement

Shared traits: Introverted and Turbulent

Constant Improvers are sensitive and contemplative individuals who enjoy having

their own space and freedom. They are often deep and creative individuals, though

often tense and more comfortable on their own than mixed up in the judgment of

the real world. With the two personality traits most representative of a sensitivity to

their environment – Introversion and Turbulence – they are reserved when dealing

with strangers or new situations, often turning inwards or focusing on

peacekeeping instead.

These personalities’ caution in the face of unfamiliar challenges can sometimes look

like a lack of motivation, but this is usually just self-doubt – Constant Improvers

have a strong drive, but it comes paired with a strong fear of failure. They invest a

great deal of their identity in their successes, and even a minor misstep or

embarrassment can be crushing.

The vigilance learned from a lifetime of ups and downs gives these personality

types a knack for sensing trouble. This is a strategic wariness that can be quite

useful in situations that need to balance risk and reward, whether a financial

investment or a romantic opportunity. Though they can seem frustratingly over-

cautious to others, Constant Improvers would rather not face calamity, so they

excel at avoiding it with preventive insights.

Since they aren’t always comfortable with energetic exchanges with other people,

Constant Improvers often direct their attention elsewhere, striving to master

hobbies, careers, bodies of knowledge, or new means of self-expression. Their

restless drive, self-doubt, and solitary mentality can combine to create impressive,

beautiful results.

High-achieving and perfectionistic, Constant Improvers generally try to do their best

in their endeavors, dedicating tremendous time and energy. These personalities

can put so much pressure on themselves that they diminish their own impressive

accomplishments by fixating on the slightest flaws or dismissing successes as luck

rather than skill or dedication.

Taken too far, this perfectionism can also drive endless rumination – an unhealthy

obsession with perfection will reveal flaws in the best plans, and Constant

Improvers often feel forced to abandon a course of action because something

doesn’t line up right. Whether working feverishly or picking at the threads of an

idea though, Constant Improvers can be intense about their goals, and progress

towards internal balance can lead them to amazing personal accomplishments.

Alongside their personal efforts, these personality types also care a great deal what

others think of them. This can be very useful, but they can take this to a fault:

fixating on others’ expectations can sour social efforts into awkwardness. Constant

Improvers are easily thrown off-balance by conflict, and may communicate timidly,

or not at all, for fear of hurting people’s feelings, provoking a confrontation, or

looking the fool.

Sometimes it takes peer approval and active encouragement for this Strategy group

to recognize their own virtues. These personalities often benefit from friends and

colleagues patient enough to get to know them and who give them a chance to

speak their minds. When treated considerately, these types become devoted

partners and begin to reveal their kindness, insights, heartfelt support, and the

complexities of their approach to the world, from sometimes (oddly specific) plans

for obscure situations to starry-eyed ambitions.

#### Social Engagement

Shared traits: Extraverted and Turbulent

Those with the Social Engagement Strategy are an interesting group. In some ways,

Extraversion and Turbulence conflict with each other: Extraversion leads to risk-

tolerance and boldness; Turbulence reflects self-doubt and sensitivity. Turbulence

also contributes to impulsivity, which Extraversion makes highly visible. This means

Social Engagers tend to do what comes naturally first and think about it later. In this

way, they are almost an exaggeration of their underlying personality types. This

isn’t a bad thing.

Social Engagers enthusiastically engage the external world, not content to live in

their minds, drawing emotional security from positive interactions. In business and

among loved ones, these types are usually the center of attention. Social Engagers

meet the needs of the crowd in social settings, but they sometimes hide their true

selves to impress other people. They appreciate social status, and often portray

themselves as the person they aspire to – usually a purer form of their personality

type – in order to achieve it.

Social Engagers often experience stunning highs and lows as their Extraversion

pushes their Turbulent need for success into overdrive and their Turbulence

creates an immediate sense of regret, warranted or not. These personality types

can be surprisingly competitive, measuring themselves against peers and hoping

others notice their accomplishments, then retreat, suddenly becoming averse to

conflict, leading them to apologize for or avoid confrontations – even those that

ended in beneficial discussions.

These personalities may falter occasionally in their self-confidence, but ultimately

they are driven to improve and achieve. At the end of the day, they have no trouble

blowing off some steam and enjoying a much-needed break, either. Social Engagers

enthusiastically pursue stimulating and exciting experiences, especially with a

group of friends. They much prefer to dive into an experience than to sit down for a

reserved evaluation, though many will naturally regret their hot-headed lack of

planning down the road.

This push and pull between ambition and alarm, impulse and caution, highs and

lows can itself be quite stressful, but it’s all part of this Strategy’s charm. These

personality types are often restless in pursuit of their goals to improve their

circumstances, but show their passion by indulging their desires through some

earned fun (and maybe shopping for something impressive). Social Engagers work

hard at their personal and professional development, striving to meet all manner of

stringent standards, but they lead dynamic lives as well, filled with companionship,

sharing adventures and accomplishments with others whenever possible. Whatever

direction they pull, they are sure to pull the crowd with them.

While the Strategies are important, this series of e-books simply doesn’t have the

space to explore them properly. For now, we’ll focus on the inner core: The four

Roles. Look to future updates to the series and our Academy to see this aspect

explored more fully.

Who is “The Executive?”

Throughout this book, we’ll discuss how Executives’ traits manifest themselves into

a complete personality type. The interactions among the traits result in a unique set

of behaviors, and we’ll examine the effects on personal growth, relationships, and

academic and professional development. The following paragraphs are a brief

overview of the Executive personality type, and subsequent chapters will explore

each area in much greater detail.

# Personal Growth

For Executives, the bedrock of a healthy life is a sense of order, tradition, and

stability. Like other members of the Sentinel type group, they feel a strong need to

belong – whether to a family, a community, or some other social group. Born

leaders, they often step into positions of responsibility and authority to organize

people and ensure that best practices are followed.

Thanks to the combination of Observant and Judging traits, these types live in a

concrete world of facts, data, and clear-cut boundaries. They have a firm sense of

right and wrong, and given their honest, direct natures, feel compelled to share

these views. Driven by an inner sense of responsibility, Executives have a strong

work ethic and tend to do everything in their power to fulfill their duties and deliver

on their promises. Social mores and conventions matter to people with this

personality type, informing the standards they set for themselves and others. As a

result, Executives often grow into “model citizens,” serving as pillars of their

communities.

Even if everyone turns against them, Executives stick to their principles. This trait is

admirable in many circumstances, but if it manifests as plain stubbornness or

intransigence, it can hold them back. People with this personality type may find

themselves resisting new ideas or perspectives, particularly those that threaten

established ways of doing things.

Given their firm convictions, Executives run the risk of imposing their values on

others. When others don’t meet their high standards, they won’t hesitate to share

their disapproval. They may express irritation or even wrath toward those who can’t

match their prodigious work ethic, attention to detail, or loyalty. Executives are

willing to make personal sacrifices to meet their obligations, and it frustrates them

when the people in their lives won’t do the same.

Friends, Family, and Relationships

As Extraverts, Executives love connecting with other people. They’re drawn to active

roles in traditional, respected institutions, such as civic organizations or faith

communities. In their free time, they may find themselves nurturing these

connections and participating in related events, from fundraisers to board

meetings. These types hate to let people down, so they develop reputations for

being straightforward and reliable, never skipping a meeting or flaking on a

commitment at the last moment.

That said, they take care not to let their social responsibilities compromise their

family lives. Many Executives consider family among their top priorities, and strive

to ensure a stable, orderly life for their loved ones. Given their respect for all that’s

time-honored, they can also be trusted to carry on traditions from one generation

to the next.

Practical and efficient, Executives aren’t prone to grand gestures or great shows of

affection. Instead, they show how much they care through acts of service. Rather

than buying flowers or writing poems, they might take care of a loved one’s car or

clean the kitchen. What they lack in emotional expression, Executives make up for

in consistency, loyalty, and devotion. They show up on time, deliver on their

promises, and stand by the people who matter to them.

Unfortunately, Executives tend to struggle to recognize and honor their feelings, let

alone those of others. Given their rational, concrete mindsets, they may be put off

by the irrational and sometimes messy world of emotions. To deepen and sustain

their relationships, they must learn to accept their feelings and respect other

people’s emotional needs. Luckily, Executives keep a cool head in charged

situations, and this trait can help them communicate clearly in the heat of the

moment.

Academic and Professional Development

Thanks to their integrity and clear convictions, Executives tend to excel as leaders

and managers. Whether they’re coordinating group projects or managing teams,

they lead by example, creating cultures that value hard work and discourage

cheating or cutting corners. Rather than grasping blindly toward the future,

Executives learn from the past, identifying and sticking to best practices and proven

protocols.

Unlike Intuitive types, they focus on concrete facts and data. Patient and detail-

oriented, they make excellent administrators and decision-makers in many

different environments. As students, they’re self-motivated, attentive, and

remarkably efficient, capable of balancing a heavy course load with significant

extracurricular obligations. Whether in the workplace or the classroom, Executives

find it easy to assess and implement various action plans. Given their focus and

dedication, they may make executing even the most complex projects look like a

walk in the park.

Executives respect rules and guidelines, and their loyalty and sense of duty make

them reliable, productive employees and students. They can be trusted to pay

attention to detail and adhere to rules and procedures, even when it would be

easier to do otherwise. Not surprisingly, people with this personality type gravitate

toward careers in institutions with strong traditions and explicit hierarchies, such as

public service, law, and the military. Executives feel compelled to meet deadlines

and deliver on assignments, so they may need to protect themselves from people

who try to foist extra work or obligations on them.

Strong-willed and decisive, these types aren’t afraid to defend their opinions. This

may put them at odds with their colleagues, teachers, or fellow students,

particularly those who are sensitive to conflict or criticism. To reach their full

potential, Executives may need to learn how to communicate diplomatically and

acknowledge other viewpoints. That said, Executives’ dedication often makes them

irreplaceable, and their self-sufficient desire to build diverse skill sets can lead to

achievements in multiple areas.

# Personal Growth

Exploring Possibility

Executives have excellent practical skills and use them effectively in leadership

roles. Traditional values, like a strong work ethic and duty to familial and

community responsibilities, form the framework by which these types live. As

Extraverts, they not only live by these rules, they also want others to live by a

certain code, for the good of all. Obligations are everything to Executives, and

“Duty” is their middle name.

Duty, Dependability, and Fairness

Executives who want to improve themselves have several resources to draw on. A

need to fulfill their duty (however they see it) motivates them to carry out their

work, whatever it may be. Dependability is another fundamental element of the

Executive personality – they don’t dodge personal accountability and want others to

perceive them as people on whom they can rely. They believe that no one is above

adhering to a certain set of standards, least of all themselves. It’s this definition of

fairness that they can use as the baseline for personal improvement.

Motivation and Development Executives can consider personal growth in five closely related, but distinct, areas.

The first three relate to the internal development of self-regard, and the latter two

are more about tactics to engage more fulfilling lives.

Motivation

● Self-esteem speaks to how much Executives like themselves. Accepting both

their flaws and strengths creates a base of internal appreciation that enables

change motivated by desire instead of shame.

● Self-respect speaks to their sense of worth. People with this personality type

value rational logic, and when they live their beliefs and values, a balanced

sense of pride in who they are follows.

● Self-confidence is the energy source that moves Executives forward in life.

When belief in their abilities is confirmed using their skills, Executives are

driven to make steady progress.

Development

● Self-evolution describes processes of conscious growth that Executives take

on to realize their potential. Knowing when to assess and expand themselves

enhances their abilities and happiness.

● Self-responsibility is taking charge of their lives by accepting accountability

for what they can do to improve things. Being conscious of their behavior

and direction in life helps them guide their own progress instead of letting

fate dictate a path.

Attending to each of these five areas can promote successful, fulfilling lives, while a

lack of attention can serve as a source of persistent difficulty. For example, when

Executives (and many other types) feel a lack of self-respect, they may engage in

intellectually aggressive behaviors that compensate for their insecurity – not often a

path to objective learning and growth.

Moving Forward

Conscious personal growth is about more than feeling obligated to change. When

inspired, these types seek new ways to be happier and more successful – including

an independent and personal definition of success. The goal is to spur their inner

drive with ideas, encouragement, and gentle guidance for possible problems along

the way.

In the following sections, we’ll first examine Executives’ characteristics in their most

beneficial forms. Using these motivational components in “balanced” ways helps

these types lead fulfilling, productive, and happy lives.

Next, we’ll describe “unbalanced” tendencies that can emerge – departures from

healthy, productive motivation strategies. We’ll also explain some adverse effects

Executives may experience as a result.

Finally, we’ll discuss what Executives can do to improve or “rebalance” each of the

motivational components, and provide exercises, reflection, and actions to aid in

personal growth and development.

### Self-Esteem

Self-esteem involves Executives finding value in themselves. When their self-esteem

is low, they feel they lack purpose. With high self-esteem, their sense of value is

firmly intact, and they believe they’re living up to the high standards they set for

themselves. These standards are grounded in the written and unwritten rules of

family,   culture,   nation,   religion,   profession,     or   other   community-based

expectations, and guide Executives as they make their way in the world, giving them

a rubric by which to measure themselves. The closer to the mark they see

themselves, the more satisfaction these types feel, but it’s a matter of perception:

self-esteem can be reflected in their external achievements, but, ultimately, it’s an

attitude that must come from within.

#### Balanced Self-Esteem

Finding Purpose in Protocol

Executives have great respect for structure and organization, which they see as a

way of bringing order to the chaos of life. In adhering to the laws of the land or the

rules of a contract, they show their deference to the wisdom of something greater

than themselves.

For these types, rules are meant to be obeyed, but only because following the rules

is often the best approach. If there seems to be a flaw in the rules, Executives may

question it, but only at the proper time, and through the proper channels. However,

when in doubt, they’re more likely to stick to the script than play the part of

renegades.

Finding Satisfaction in Effort

Wise Executives, however, soon discover that rules are something to aspire to more

than something to realize. No one can follow any creed with absolute dedication,

and these types must accept their fallibility. By the same token, Executives must

accept that they can measure gains in life only according to the effort they’ve

exerted, not in the gains themselves.

Every day, Executives wake up with the knowledge that for every success they have,

failure is waiting just around the corner to take it away. Rather than paralyzing

them, however, this knowledge energizes them to leap out of bed and start

attacking problems. And, every night, they go to sleep satisfied not with the number

of problems solved, but by the effort expended in solving them. Executives with

balanced self-esteem rest easy knowing that while they aren’t always right, they try

as hard as they can to get it right, every day.

#### Unbalanced Self-Esteem

No Excuses

Even Executives realize that there are times when rules have extenuating

circumstances, but those whose self-esteem is unbalanced often have trouble

seeing where these exceptions might apply. They may adopt a “no excuses” policy

for themselves and others, but this attitude seldom proves as productive as it might

seem.

The Effects on Others

Even the best-laid plans can go astray, but failure isn’t an option for Executives

whose self-esteem is unbalanced, and they may refuse to settle for even an iota

short of perfection. Such aggressive perfectionism can lead to burnout on their part

as well as low morale – or even mutiny – on the part of those who suffer under

their reign. Such Executives may see these effects as simply the price to pay to gain

results, no matter how incremental.

This perfectionism can take a personal toll as well, exhausting even these energetic

types. As they chase impossible standards and drive others away in the process,

they may find themselves alone, separated from people who would otherwise

freshen their outlook and efforts. Executives suffering from unbalanced self-esteem

can sustain this for impressive amounts of time through sheer force of will, but

their bodies and minds eventually give out. When it comes to self-worth, without

balance, they risk losing everything for which they’ve fought.

Relentless Perfectionism

Executives who have become too driven to perfection may win a few allies who

respect their unswerving dedication, but none ever truly live up to the impossible

standards they’ve set. While Executives may feel that those who can’t live up to

their unbalanced standards are simply not worth keeping around, people are more

complex creatures than a simple unbending set of rules can dictate.

In some cases, individuals are indeed toxic, and Executives are wise to steer clear of

them. But when these types become toxic themselves – demanding more than

those around them can (or should) reasonably give – then these guardians of

community may soon find themselves without communities to defend. Having lost

the trust – the confidence – of the people whom they once claimed to serve (albeit

in a leadership role), Executives may see their confidence erode. A leader is nothing

when there’s no one to follow.

#### Rebalancing Self-Esteem

Good Enough

Perfectionism naturally simmers in the background of Executives’ lives, with all their

standards and rules to maintain. When their self-esteem is out of balance, they may

need to guard against allowing fastidiousness to take over. While it’s hard for these

types to embrace the idea that sometimes “good enough” is, indeed, good enough,

finding ways to do so gives them a more balanced sense of self, and a more realistic

understanding of their strengths and weaknesses.

The world is more forgiving than these stringent perfectionists allow themselves to

imagine. Catastrophes happen – and, at times, we can avoid them. However, there’s

a difference between preparation and overpreparation, and smart Executives learn

when to tell which is which. If they’ve taken all reasonable precautions (and not all

possible precautions) when catastrophes strike, at least they can rest assured they

did their due diligence in preventing them.

Taking Breaks to Gain Perspective

The best cure for misconceptions is a dose of reality. Executives who despair that

all their efforts may come to naught have only to look at other people to see that,

while they may not have all their bases covered, they do cover more than most.

Those who become too insulated in their bubble of responsibilities may soon lose

touch with the obstacles others must overcome to pursue their goals. Here are

some steps Executives can take to reacquaint themselves to life outside their busy

agendas.

•     A few minutes to touch base with an acquaintance or two is often all it takes

for Executives to gain a newfound appreciation for their capabilities, which

they can be too quick to take for granted.

•     During these breaks, it’s important for these types to listen to better

understand those around them. Often, people express problems in ways that

are difficult for Executives to relate to. They’re bold and rational, but not

everyone shares these traits. Emotional perspectives can leave Executives

wondering why others don’t just run through the facts, and issuing

complaints without wanting solutions can be downright frustrating.

•     By taking a step back to exercise the patience and clarity they ask of others,

Executives provide individuals with opportunities to make themselves heard

and understood. Sometimes people need to get their feelings out before

they’re ready to address issues. When Executives exercise this skill, they find

themselves more respected and admired by those around them, boosting

their sense of self-esteem.

### Self-Respect

Simply put, self-respect is about liking who one is, rather than who one wants to be.

While similar to self-esteem in that it’s a motivator, self-respect is less about having

an overarching sense of worth and more about approving one’s actions. It asks the

question, “Do I like myself because of the way I conduct my life and the way I

express myself in the world?” Executives whose self-respect is balanced are

satisfied with not only what they have, but with what they must do to get it. For

them, the only thing more important than getting things done is getting things done

right.

#### Balanced Self-Respect

Doing Good by the Rules

Executives attain self-respect through their positive drive to make the world

effective for themselves and those for whom they care. Unlike many who might see

rules as somewhat arbitrary, these types sincerely believe that set standards are

essential to the world being a better place. When safeguarding these standards,

Executives see themselves as contributing to the well-being of themselves and

others – a very respectable thing to do.

It wouldn’t be accurate to say that these types demand unthinking obedience of

themselves or others. The reverence they have for rules comes from an in-depth

knowledge of them, and the maturity to understand that impulses must be guided

by reason – with a set of rules providing the reason that may otherwise be lacking.

Rules are a restraint, and though restraints can be confining, they can also be

freeing, if they free people from making adverse decisions that would only serve to

limit them in the end. “Nothing good happens between midnight and daylight,”

Executive parents may reason, and therefore see curbing such behavior with a

curfew as a restriction that only frees their teenage sons and daughters to pursue

activities that ultimately contribute to their well-being.

Diligent Attention

These types strive to maintain standards and make sure people do things as they

should – “should” being their culture’s established standard. Their need for self-

respect motivates Executives to oversee their work with careful attention to

protocols and expectations, and to raise their families and live their private lives

dedicated to upholding conventional principles for living.

As these types mature, their understanding of these principles typically develops as

well. Young Executives may look for older role models who exemplify the traits that

appeal to them – stability, integrity, achievement – and find commonality in a

shared set of standards they appear to hold. Reasoning that those who keep similar

convictions achieve comparable success, Executives may pattern themselves after

these exemplars. As time goes on, however, they’re sure to see that where there

are rules, there’s division, and even the clearest rules must be interpreted. In

deciding which interpretations of these rules to follow (or how to interpret the rules

for themselves), Executives take the first steps in becoming less slaves to tradition

and more masters of their destiny.

#### Unbalanced Self-Respect

Doubling Down on Standards

When Executives see a disconnect between the standards they hold for themselves

and their actual adherence to them, their internal need for coherence may manifest

externally in increasing demands for order in the behavior of those around them.

Unfortunately, this can awaken a zealous rigidity. As unbalanced self-esteem

manifests itself in perfection, stiffness plays a similar role for Executives seeking

self-respect. They can take an asset and allow it to “overheat” to make up for what

ground they believe is lost.

Being a stickler can be a good thing. It’s admirable to get things reasonably right.

Being too much of a stickler, however, can overwhelm people. This can result in

attempts to remove any human element, like emotions, from Executives’

endeavors. Communicated in a severe tone, statements such as, “We must work

harder and let the rules guide us exactly as they’re written,” can be dehumanizing.

Executives might reason that eliminating such distractions as exhaustion and

dissatisfaction in the pure pursuit of profitable goals isn’t only good for them, but

good for others as well. Unfortunately, different types of people have unique ideas

about the qualities that contribute to their self-respect. In such cases, Executives

may simply come across as overbearing and domineering, rather than the noble

companions and colleagues they could be otherwise.

The Dark Path of Surrender

Executives who pursue this rigidity to the point of misery and failure for themselves

or others may surrender to a darker impulse: an “all or nothing” mindset. If trying

too hard makes things ineffective, they may throw up their hands and declare that

any degree of trying is simply useless.

While Executives certainly have a deserved reputation for being hardworking, even

they have their limits, both physically and emotionally. If their time-honored tactics

of planning, consensus-seeking, and follow-through fail, it’s probable that their

motivation may disappear along with their lack of success.

#### Rebalancing Self-Respect

Time is Precious (and Passing By)

When we don’t like who we are, it’s primarily because we don’t like what we do. By

focusing on concrete actions, Executives move beyond abstract virtues and into

quantifiable values. It’s hard to say, for instance, who among a group of people is

the most honest, or the most reliable, or the most hardworking. But if these

individuals were to explain where their time went – to whom, and for whose benefit

– then a clearer picture would begin to emerge.

In the end, time is the most unrenewable – and therefore, most valuable –

resource. When time is spent on things, or on people, it shows they’re valued by

those who place their own irrevocable value within them. Executives who squander

their time squander their worth, and it’s often only when they see clearly where

their time has gone that they can begin to recover the self-respect they foolishly

expended on fruitless endeavors. To do this, Executives can try this activity.

•   Executives who want to reevaluate their self-image may find it helpful to

make two lists – their “pros and cons,” so to speak.

•   On the first list, they can write down all the ways they add value to others’

lives. Even at particularly low points, they’re sure to find instance after

instance where they have helped other people get where they’re going in life

– and where they continue to do so.

•   To deal with any concerns they have with liking themselves, Executives can

create a list of the ways their actions benefit others. Their effect on others

may be direct and personal, but it’s just as likely to be indirect and involve

supporting a system. The methods don’t matter; all that matters is, due to

some action on their part, someone they value saw a gain.

•   On the second list, Executives should list their “cons” – all the ways that

they’ve failed those around them, subtracting value rather than adding it.

•   Though less pleasant than the previous one, this list may be far more

instructive, highlighting those areas where these types should most seek

change. Not that change is always possible, or even desirable – sometimes

the fault isn’t with us, but with circumstances. Still, Executives owe it to

themselves and to the people around them to make a change if the fault

does lie in their hands.

Help from Objective Others

After taking a personal inventory, it might be helpful to compare notes with a

trusted acquaintance. The objectivity that comes from an outside perspective can

be invaluable in helping Executives come to more realistic appraisals of their

strengths and weaknesses. Objective others might be all too willing to add items to

Executives’ “pros” list, remembering the positive effects that might have escaped

their notice. At the same time, truly objective others can be just as quick to burst

Executives’ bubbles who claim too much credit or ignore the unintended

consequences of their decisions.

When looking at the second list – the “cons” – objective others might be even more

valuable. Together, they can assess tactics, helping Executives escape any

counterproductive ruts they might have fallen into.

### Self-Confidence

Self-confidence concerns trusting one’s abilities. When self-confidence is lacking,

every decision is shrouded in doubt and hobbled by hesitation. Self-confident

Executives, on the other hand, act swiftly and decisively. They know they aren’t

always right, but infallibility is an impossible standard. To be the best, they need not

be perfect, only better than second best.

#### Balanced Self-Confidence

Confidence from Reliability

Reliability refers to how Executives see themselves as people who can always be

called on as either a first resort or a last resort to do what must be done. They’re

ambitious, but only because of their self-image as the most capable defenders of

everything they hold dear. In their minds, if they fall down on the job, there’s no

one else to do it, or do it nearly as well. Executives work hard to ensure that

whenever they’re called on, they’re able to perform at their peak.

These types are team players, not lone wolves, however. They may seek leadership

positions, but happily serve capable leaders themselves, and may even insist that,

“Everyone has a boss.” Even if Executives see themselves as their team’s most

valuable asset, they still recognize that five, ten, or a hundred folks working in

coordination can often accomplish more in a short period of time than any one of

them could in an entire lifetime. If they want to be perceived as reliable, it’s because

they want to have relationships with other reliable people whom they can trust,

whether in business, romance, or simple friendship.

Pride in Achievement

Executives take pride in their accomplishments, which they see as merely the

consequent rewards of living successful lives. Medals and trophies are concrete

representations of the countless invisible hours of hard work athletes and other

competitors sacrifice to be the best. In much the same way, the manicured lawns

and well-kept automobiles of Executives tell the world that they’re sacrificing, too –

though they don’t see it as a sacrifice, only their duty as productive members of

society. Nice houses and cars aren’t mere luxury goods for them, but concrete ways

of showing the world they work hard for what they have, and they spend their

money with more prudence than extravagance.

After all, talk is cheap. People can claim to be caring spouses, supportive parents, or

industrious workers all they want, but Executives have the goods to back up their

claims: well-dressed families and well-kept homes are all the evidence they need of

their efforts. Those with balanced self-confidence know their success is apparent,

leaving them with no reason to restate the obvious by bragging about it.

#### Unbalanced Self-Confidence

Making Unreasonable Efforts

Executives who become too determined to prove their worth to others may begin

to lose the ability to distinguish between reasonable efforts to fulfill their

obligations, and heroic efforts that are out of proportion to the size of their original

commitments. Their insecurities may also drive them to tear down the

achievements of others, particularly if they appear more successful in some way:

wealthier, more attractive, more content. In both cases, Executives with unbalanced

self-confidence are looking elsewhere for reassurance, when it’s instead their

internal perception of themselves that’s flawed.

Over Commitment

In most cases, Executives realize that their time is finite, and keep their schedules

busy but manageable. But when these types feel their accomplishments don’t

match their self-image, they may compensate by sacrificing more time for others

than they can truly spare. Those with unbalanced self-confidence may assume that

they’re more capable than they really are and promise more than they can

reasonably deliver. Executives with multiple commitments may be forced by

changing circumstances to re-prioritize, leading to broken promises and damaged

trust on the part of all involved.

Little is more harmful to Executives’ self-image than the inability to follow through

on their commitments. They may attempt to shore up their self-confidence by

being harder on themselves – taking on more responsibilities at the expense of

losing precious free time. This vicious cycle helps no one, as increasingly burned

out Executives only become less and less capable of fulfilling even their original

obligations, not to mention any new ones they take on.

Insecurity through Envy

These types pride themselves on their ability to do anything that needs to be done

as well as, or better than, anyone else, so failures – even the most understandable

ones – can make them uneasy. Executives who feel they aren’t living up to their

internal standards may compensate by minimizing the achievements of others to

draw focus away from their own lack of success.

Professional setbacks may lead Executives with unbalanced self-confidence to cast

aspersions on their coworkers or professional peers. They may claim that any

advantages coworkers possess are due not to differences in ability or effort, but

instead to favoritism or other extraneous political factors. This attitude doesn’t only

come into play at work, of course – Executives with shaken self-confidence may lash

out at their romantic interests’ exes or other potential partners, for instance. But

the attitude in every case is the same – when their self-confidence is unbalanced,

Executives see others’ accomplishments as threatening because they always have

the nagging thought, “What has this person done, that I have not, to achieve

something that I want in life?”

#### Rebalancing Self-Confidence

Hard Work and Fair Play

For Executives, hard work and fair play are all it takes to succeed in life. When they

perceive that peers – or worse still, younger individuals – are experiencing greater

success, these types tend to reach one of two conclusions: either those people

work harder, or they have hidden advantages. While there’s often some truth in

both conclusions, Executives who dwell too long on what others are doing are only

ignoring their own unique contributions and strengths.

Managing Obligations

Little disturbs Executives’ sense of self-confidence more than failing to live up to

their obligations, which only becomes more likely as the number their obligations

increase. Although Executives are skilled at managing busy schedules, they tend to

leave little space in them for themselves, and even less when they feel they must

prove themselves. Here are some ideas these types can try to bring some balance

back to their obligations.

● Thinking twice before taking on new obligations is always helpful, but in

addition to being more protective of their time, Executives would do well to

remember that obligations are a two-way street. The people who rely on

them can be counted on in turn, and smart Executives know when to ask for

favors, as well as when to repay them.

● Not that they should keep track of every good turn they do for others, or for

every courtesy done for them, but Executives would be wise to have a

general sense of the balance of obligations in their relationships. When they

tip too heavily in someone’s favor, it might be time to re-adjust.

Accounting for Individual Differences

When Executives look at successful contemporary role models, or those from their

past, they’re often quick to discount any inherent advantages that may have

contributed to their success. After all, if certain things aren’t under their control –

such as circumstances of birth and upbringing – then there’s no use dwelling on

them. However, if it becomes apparent that someone has accomplished much

more than these types have, their beliefs in the power of persistence, and integrity

trumping luck and duplicity, are tested.

It can be tempting for Executives to try to ignore the disproportionate success of

the fortunate and the wicked alike, perhaps by pointing out their flaws in unrelated

areas, or by imagining the dire consequences that can befall them as soon as their

luck runs out – or their lack of character catches up with them. These approaches

only fuel the envy that’s so damaging to Executives’ self-confidence. Instead of

looking at others, they should pay more attention to what separates them from

others – such as the satisfaction they get from applying the principles of hard work

and fair play in their everyday lives. There’s always someone who has more,

whether acquired fairly or not, but mature Executives retain their self-confidence

because it’s predicated not on a belief in their own superiority, but in the

knowledge that living according to their principles gives a life of satisfaction, even if

success happens to elude them for the moment.

### Self-Evolution

Self-evolution is the power that Executives possess to change as circumstances

change around them. As much as they prize their steady, unswerving attitude

toward life, they know that too much determination can lead to an unproductive

rigidity. They may use tried-and-true methods, but they also continue to test them

as time goes on, keeping only the ones that remain useful.

#### Balanced Self-Evolution

Strength through Continuity

The power of Executives comes from their mastery of tradition, and those whose

self-evolution is balanced use this mastery of the past to solve the problems of the

present. In looking at the past, they search for patterns of success, which they then

seek to perpetuate into the future. The accumulated wisdom of the ages rests in

the traditions that guide Executives’ decisions, and those who balance their self-

evolution respond to it as they would respond to the advice of older mentors: with

a respectful ear, not blind obedience.

Conditions change, and sometimes methods must change with them. Executives

have a broad view of tradition, seeing it as a living thing that, like themselves,

grows, matures, and develops over time. They adjust, but they adjust for the better,

and always with certain constants underpinning all they do.

Addition from Subtraction

While it’s true that Executives prefer to use established methods, they continue to

test them as time goes on, keeping only the ones that remain of use. If a method

provides a suitable rate of return, then they use it unaltered. Even when a method

begins to fall apart, Executives prefer to change it as little as possible. Rather than

throw out the entire rulebook, these types simply edit out the more troublesome

rules.

By subtracting the weakest parts of a system, Executives add to its strength. But it’s

only by careful observation of where a system breaks down that they can

determine where the weaknesses are. Executives know where to adjust, and what

to leave unchanged.

#### Unbalanced Self-Evolution

The Challenge of Change

People with the Executive personality type view change with skepticism more than

disdain. Tradition has the weight of iteration behind it – years, decades, or centuries

of people who, repeatedly faced with similar problems, devised a method for

solving them. Others could then successfully copy the method without alteration.

Though changing circumstances may call for altered methods, Executives prefer to

see the tested method fail before introducing an untested one, no matter how

promising the latter might be. Indeed, even one failure may be written off as a

failure of execution – human error – rather than a flaw in the method itself.

With balanced self-evolution, Executives can recognize the difference between a

flawed method and a flawed execution. However, those with unbalanced self-

evolution may simply repeat formerly successful behaviors, no matter how

unsuccessful they now are.

#### Rebalancing Self-Evolution

Using Tradition as a Guide

Famed author W. Somerset Maugham once said, “Tradition is a guide and not a

jailer.” This aphorism can serve as a powerful insight for Executives. Using it as a

guide, they won’t diminish their respect for tradition, but they also won’t enslave

themselves to it, either. Here are some steps these types can take toward

rebalancing their traditional approach.

● Accepting the inevitability of change can be the first step.

● Learning to blend the old with the new can help these types create a more

balanced view.

● Spotting the difference between maintaining stability and being stubborn can

also help them better navigate a changing world.

Self-talk and self-examination, such as, “Am I offering helpful insights into how

things work, or am I just finding change difficult to handle?” and similar questions

may help.

Self-Care Supports Taking Care of Business

A well-maintained machine functions better than one that’s neglected, and

Executives who take care of themselves are better at taking care of everything else.

Self-care, then, isn’t an indulgence, but an investment – the time that they spend on

family vacations or long coffee breaks isn’t wasted, but comes back to them in

increasing dividends.

At times, these types can become so focused on fulfilling their obligations that they

lose sight of the reasons that they do the things they do. Taking time to remind

themselves of the inherent value of the things they cherish most – the communities

that they draw their strength from – can renew their resolve at the times that test

them most, allowing them to act as the bold, resilient leaders they are.

### Self-Responsibility

Executives’ sense of responsibility derives from their need to honor their rational

approach to tradition and accepted principles. Their personal sense of

responsibility and obligation to society can’t be entirely separated – they take care

of themselves when they take care of the world. Fulfilling their sense of

responsibility can help them achieve healthy self-esteem, self-respect, and self-

confidence.

#### Balanced Self-Responsibility

Seeing Things Through

For these types, self-responsibility refers to the need to fulfill the obligations they’ve

set for themselves. When Executives take on duties, their self-image becomes

linked to their ability to see them through. While this principle is true of even small

tasks – running an errand for a spouse, or picking up a friend at the airport – the

overarching obligations that constitute their lives are what drive them from day to

day. Whether at home or work, they seek security – and fulfilling obligations, for

them, is an effort to cultivate it in their lives.

When they do their part to hold up their end of a deal, they gain assurance that

others can feel compelled to do likewise. Those who can’t be relied upon shouldn’t

expect to rely on others, either; this isn’t only a watchful defense on Executives’

part, but a proactive search for threats to squelch and opportunities to seize. These

types aren’t risk-averse, but risk-aware, and careful to ensure that any gambles they

take are smart ones. They’re willing to risk investments – of time, money, or feeling

– if the rewards are promising and the odds are weighted heavily in their favor.

Executives don’t like to lose, but they know that to win, they must play: nothing

ventured, nothing gained.

Masters of Organization and Standards

Organization comes naturally to Executives. They know what to do and who to call

to make things happen smoothly, and tend to have all-encompassing resources

they become familiar with and achieve mastery over. This helps them organize

efficiently. At home, at work, and as parents, they’re likely to have just as much of a

command of resources.

Their widespread attention to detail isn’t for its own sake, as Executives connect it

to responsibility and accountability. Life for them is often a series of rubrics,

arranged and linked to provide an organized, consistent path.

The Value of Persistence

These types enjoy the gifts of patience and focus. Valiant in their ability to endure

hardships, they sometimes take on more onerous tasks than necessary simply

because they know they can do them and gain more than they lose, whether

regarding character building or more concrete rewards. The most mundane work is

no problem for Executives if they grasp its rational purpose, if it fits a paradigm

they’re familiar with, or if it’s paired with a powerful sense of obligation.

Executives’ persistence presents value in many ways. While they may not always

find motivation from within, once engaged, they’re finishers, and the idea of

something left undone if they don’t finish is a strong motivator. People with the

Judging trait find satisfaction in chapters that are completed and closed. Being

persistent enough to finish a task reinforces their sense of reliability and,

consequently, their self-esteem.

#### Unbalanced Self-Responsibility

Growth occurs when Executives use their sense of responsibility and dedication to

move forward in life. However, as with all good things, diligence can shift from a

powerful tool to a demanding tyrant if not properly balanced against other

considerations.

Too Little Self-Care

Executives build their self-responsibility upon their successful execution of personal

obligations and upholding established standards. When they aren’t feeling great

about themselves or their lives, their sense of responsibility may go into overdrive

as they search for a more positive sense of themselves. Instead of integrating self-

care and rest into their plans, they may feel an urgency to go in the opposite

direction, pushing themselves – and others – relentlessly.

At such times, these types may forget the softer side of dealing with people,

including themselves, the side where gentle coaxing is more effective than harsh

mandates. Executives aren’t always in touch with their feelings. Those who are too

driven to accomplish a task may appear unreasonable to people with other

priorities or methods.

Even Executives, who tend to be stalwart, become stressed when tension from their

overbearing attitude breaks down the morale, willingness, and effectiveness of the

very people they depend on most. Such breakdowns can start vicious cycles where

they’re disappointed in their performance, feel stressed about it, and then go

deeper into burnout. The personality type who always knows what to do can find

itself without a solution.

#### Rebalancing Self-Responsibility

Making Realistic Assessments

Executives who find themselves suffering from taking on too much responsibility

may need to assess who they are, what their duties are, and why they feel the need

to push so hard. They also might want to decide if their relentless efforts are paying

off or not. Working exceedingly hard may appear to be the right thing to do at

times. But it can also exact a toll on individuals. Matching the cost to the benefit is

essential.

These types typically choose action over ruminating over things. However, this go-

to tactic may not be helpful when doing too much is the problem. Learning to

prioritize activities that have proven to be important, and balancing them with

downtime and relaxation, can make this tactic more responsible. Burnt-out shells of

Executives are less effective than those who take the time to restore themselves.

Asking, “Why?” instead of pursuing less considered action can help set clearer paths

– especially when their need to feel responsible begins to “overheat” and results in

an explosion of activity.

Managing Stress

Stress can be a good thing – like a heavy weight, the more it’s handled, the stronger

handlers become. But the dangers of handling too much stress are much the same

as the risks of lifting too much weight: snapping under the pressure. And though

Executives are often adept at handling stress, they have their limits, too.

Extraverted Executives might find that stress relief is often less about finding a

peaceful place in their lives as it is about getting frustrated energy out in healthy

ways. Here are some ideas that can help.

● A night on the town with a few friends, or a pick-up ball game in the park,

may be more effective than a quiet evening with a book or a lazy afternoon

in a hammock – although of course, even Executives need rest sometimes.

● When they do need a more sedate outlet for stress relief, there are countless

methods available, including deep breath meditation (in which someone sits

still and focuses their entire, exclusive attention on the in and out of their

breath), respectful life planning (in which busy Executives identify reasonable

timelines and priorities, rather than pushing for what could be done, an

unreasonable long-term strategy), or simply planning in some time to relax

with family and friends, or even alone.

● Everyone needs a break, including busy Executives who see themselves as

indispensable. True, they often find themselves – or more accurately,

intentionally place themselves – in positions of extreme responsibility that

aren’t so easily abdicated. Then it becomes an additional responsibility for

them to find people who can take over so that the responsibility doesn’t

always have to fall squarely on these types, who may not complain, but

inevitably suffer under the burden.

● This is true as well for unforeseen circumstances – all it takes is one power

outage or faulty engine part to keep even the most diligent Executives from

fulfilling an obligation.

Learning to turn off their phones and laptops occasionally can help Executives

exercise responsibility for themselves by setting aside their responsibility for

everyone else. If they’ve delegated wisely, they should have no trouble leaving their

children in the care of capable babysitters, or their businesses in the care of

capable administrators.

Conclusion Reconciling the Heart with the Head

The emotional and rational sides of human nature are often presented as

antagonistic to one another – if individuals are rational, then emotions only cloud

their judgment. For Executives, who pride themselves on their discernment,

emotions must be an obstacle – something to be considered, perhaps, but only in

the sense that one must know an enemy to defeat it. Yet where do they get their

motivation from, if not the feelings of pride, honor, or love that are reinforced when

they act according to their principles, and are injured when they don’t live up to

their own self-image? Emotions, while messy and unpredictable, aren’t enemies,

but an integral part of human nature. Though Executives don’t need to indulge in

them, ignoring them – pretending that they don’t exist, or are irrelevant – is just as

foolish.

In some cases, these types might want to be more cognizant of their emotions as a

safeguard against their irrationality – leaving decisions or otherwise delegating

work to others, if the emotional stakes would be too high for Executives personally.

For the same reasons that surgeons are typically forbidden from operating on their

own family members and attorneys rarely represent themselves in court,

Executives should look to their own biases to see situations where others might be

more impartial, and more effective. (Having said this, though emotions can inhibit

people, they can also free them, and motivate them to be better than they are.)

Finding the Self in the System

Executives thrive when they find effective systems to thrive within. However,

systems have a way of swallowing up individual elements, and these types, more

than most, may have trouble maintaining their identities under pressures to

conform. After all, they feel little intrinsic pleasure in doing things “just to be

different,” so in the absence of a compelling counterargument, Executives may find

it easiest to go along with the crowd – at least superficially, until such a

counterargument arises. On certain issues, they may stand their ground no matter

what. But when it comes to something like disagreeing with a corporate dress code

or homeowner association lawn care policies, Executives may see compliance as a

smart compromise. Community standards may at times conflict with individual

expression, but when the alternative is sheer anarchy, there really is no alternative

at all.

Executives should nevertheless be prepared to fight, however, for “community

standards” are at times only a thin veil for the personal prejudices of a small group

of people rather than legitimate compromises done for group harmony and

efficiency. When their self-determination is balanced, they have no problem

complying with or even enforcing sound, logical rules – but they’re also able to see

when a rule works, and when it doesn’t. Though they may have their own principled

way of rebellion – resigning from a position rather than following an improper

order, for example – Executives are under no obligation to champion rules or

methods that don’t measure up to their personal standards.

# Romantic Relationships

Productivity and perseverance are common personal objectives for Executives.

Romantic partners who respect these goals are needed for these types to feel

happy in relationships. For them, tradition and security enable love to grow, and

they want longevity. They’re unabashed about their exacting requirements when

establishing such commitments, though, and finding someone to love is a

deliberate process for them.

However, it would be a mistake to oversimplify Executives’ rational natures as cold

or unloving. Status is very important to these individuals, and finding love falls

within their broad social ambitions. Executives want others they can trust and share

emotional connections that are so difficult for them to master on their own. Though

outgoing by nature, being deeply connected to others isn’t easy for them. They find

that loving relationships enhance their lives by fulfilling a fundamental human need

they might struggle with.

Executives’ love comes not merely from emotion, but from a desire for

advancement. When these types meet others who attractively reflect their values,

they see them as components of more complete lives. They tend to be motivated

and self-sufficient, but quickly find that lovers who complement them are major

assets. Love brings a sense of stability that strengthens Executives’ efforts in life –

one way or another, having a partner moves these individuals forward.

They approach love confidently, and as a serious matter. While not fluent in subtle

emotional understanding, they’re dedicated once they commit. These types don’t

appreciate those they see as unreliable, but they welcome into their lives people

who impress them. When someone also meets Executives’ deeper criteria and

enters their hearts – which isn’t an easy thing – they gain special status among all

others.

Once real partnerships form, Executives work hard to make them prosper. This

doesn’t mean such relationships are invulnerable. They can still succumb to the

things that destroy couples, especially if these types can’t find a way to be sensitive,

honest with their deeper feelings, and compromise their strict views. However,

romantic relationships are unlikely to crumble because Executives fail to apply

enough effort.

Dating and the Single Executive

If someone attracts Executives romantically, it’s usually because they’ve had time to

appreciate that person’s qualities. “Love at first sight” can happen, but they often

form a romantic interest in deliberate ways. They aren’t reluctant to socialize, and

are quite enthusiastic about branching out and meeting new people. Executives

rarely let physical attraction overcome their strict standards, but once others catch

their eye, they’re unreserved in their pursuit.

These types prefer traditional dating practices, typically finding potential romantic

partners in places where they first become acquainted with them casually, such as

work, school, or meeting through mutual friends. Novelty isn’t necessary – in fact,

spontaneity may feel like a distraction to Executives when they’re first getting to

know someone. They strongly prefer planned activities where they can focus on a

partner without a lot of surprises, and are usually the ones planning their dates.

Someone who is too spontaneous can disenchant Executives, who often interpret it

as irresponsibility. These types are confident, but prefer structure and rules, and

making emotional connections is hard enough for them without disorganization in

the mix. Not everyone they date may embrace their strict codes, however, and

Executives may repel or reject otherwise perfectly good romantic partners by

judging this too harshly.

If they don’t get past what they may initially see as someone’s flaws, they miss the

chance to know their admirable qualities, and a growing relationship might end.

Astute Executives quickly realize that pushing themselves to be more open-minded

keeps them in the prime waters of the dating pool. However, if the pursuit of a

potential mate consistently takes them outside their preferences, Executives can

lose patience. Once they decide against something, they’re often quick to remove it

from their lives.

Executives in the Bedroom

To put it in perhaps unromantic terms, Executives’ natural drive to do a good job

extends to sex, as they’re goal-oriented and focused. Their performance is

somewhere in their consciousness during physical intimacy, as well as a sense of

responsibility to their partners. Having strict personal boundaries, these types

aren’t particularly sensitive or open-minded in the bedroom, but ideally, they want

to please their partners.

Their firmly resolved nature can create conflict if their beloveds show an interest in

experimenting, as many do to keep their sex lives fresh. Depending on their

background, they may have narrow personal definitions of acceptable sexual

behavior. If this is the case, being pressured to be experimental can provoke sharp

refusal from Executives.

Despite being very outspoken, it’s rarely easy for Executives to understand and

express their deeper feelings or vulnerabilities. Learning to communicate honestly

and unreservedly about sexual matters is essential for them, and a wise priority to

address early in sexual relationships.

Taking Failure to Heart

Executives like steady, predictable advancement in life, and breakups are assaults

on those goals. They go against everything Executives prefer in relationships, and

feel like personal failures. These individuals assume they should be able to repair a

troubled romance with a calculated solution and vigorous effort, but logic and

energy aren’t always enough. They get frustrated when their direct approach

doesn’t gain traction in subtle matters of the heart.

When these types think they see an end coming, they may terminate a romance

preemptively with unapologetic swiftness. It may seem callous, but they prefer to

save themselves – and their sweethearts – from protracted pain. If there was ever a

“rip the bandage off” type, its Executives. This is also an attempt to maintain control

over their lives. Despite a sense of failure, they may judge it to be the best way to

restore stability and progress. Even in love, their practical rationality wins out.

After breakups, Executives are wise to seek the company of friends and family.

Social contact energizes them, and it helps remind them that a bad experience with

one person doesn’t diminish their ability to connect with someone else. Executives

are more emotionally detached than most types, and a breakup can push these

individuals into unbalanced states of cynicism and selfishness. However, that same

emotional detachment allows Executives to see experiences for what they were in a

balanced way, and avoid irrationally painting future romances with past

frustrations.

Potential Role Pairings Bold and decisive, Executives love to create structure, but people aren’t like puzzle

pieces that fit together consistently, nor can they be forced into alignment.

Executives can relax their vigorous, exacting approach a little when pursuing love.

Healthy relationships aren’t tasks to be accomplished, and other people generally

don’t like to be controlled.

The Right Approach

While romance is far from an exact science, Executive lovers understand that

putting their best feet forward achieves the best results. With so many personality

variables in play, this often means proceeding into the unknown with an open

mind, knowing that while they can’t control other people’s behavior, maintaining a

balanced, positive, and accepting attitude brings out the best in others.

The information presented here can help Executives build and navigate successful

relationships. Knowledge of other personality tendencies enables Executives to do

what they do best: decide and execute. These types excel at working toward

success, and since true love is unlikely to be found in a perfect package, they must

correspondingly work toward some flexibility of mind if they want to succeed.

With this in mind, we’ll offer a helpful look at some of the ways Executives and

other types are likely to interact, and insight to help them maximize their chances

for happiness by creating fulfilling and balanced relationships with those types.

Executive–Analyst Relationships

Executives and Analysts both approach finding partners with some degree of

calculation. On some level, most people employ a mental checklist when they meet

potential romantic candidates, but for these types, those checklists are more

deliberate and conscious. This systematic approach reflects their rational way of

avoiding expected problems, and is helpful if not taken too far or relied on entirely.

Balanced Executive–Analyst Relationships

A Romantic Mental Checklist

Executives’ checklist involves strict standards, personal traditions, and the desire to

craft stable lives, while Analysts have extremely specific likes and dislikes based on

their ideas and ideals. Neither are likely to be unsure of their preferences, and both

approach    relationships     with   a   sense    of      mental   negotiation.   Honestly

communicating the “must-haves” on their respective checklists sets up foundations

that increase their potential to build mutually satisfying love.

Effective Together

Although they might express it differently, both partner types in these couples

appreciate efficiency and accomplishment. For Executives, it’s real-world and

practical – they like things hassle-free and secure, and have the energy to ensure

these preferences. For Analysts, it concerns more theoretical ideas about progress

and improvement. They enjoy finding innovative and unconventional procedures

that wring the greatest benefit out of life, albeit it’s sometimes complicated. If these

lovers tap into each other’s strengths, they make formidable couples.

Executives are impressed by Analysts’ penchant for solving life’s puzzles – if it’s not

overdone. They find Analysts’ optimal solutions to be highly useful, and Executives

appreciate little more than usefulness. In return, Analysts find a helpful dose of

realism in Executives’ cool, decisive rationality. The fact that they don’t get lost in

ideas stabilizes ever-imaginative Analysts, something especially needed by those

with the Prospecting trait.

Both these types enjoy longevity in relationships. Executives want reliable partners

that create a sense of continuity, and Analysts, ever logical, fulfill this need. In turn,

Analysts find that having sensible, energetic Executive sweethearts as constants in

their lives allows them to focus on bigger issues. These two types agree that drama

and chaos aren’t good uses of energy, and finding others to love who make sense

to them is rare for both, so they treasure the accomplishment.

Unbalanced Executive–Analyst Relationships

Competing Priorities

When Executives want traditional approaches, and Analysts want to re-engineer

everything, it can lead to frustration. Their sense of satisfaction comes from

different things: Executives find satisfaction in being anchored and accomplishing

specific goals, and Analysts find it in exploring ideas and working on ever-evolving

projects.

Executives quickly lose patience with Analyst lovers always engaging myriad

possibilities in an unbalanced manner. If Analysts insist on perpetually

experimenting, or worse, spending time and energy on ideas that go nowhere,

Executives become irritated. These types invest heavily in keeping their day-to-day

lives in order, and individuals can easily become overly critical in an unbalanced

way when Analysts lose themselves in ideas that seem irrelevant to daily life.

Unfortunately, Analysts are deeply hurt when Executives dismiss matters they care

about. For Analysts, trying to master larger concepts is important, and having their

inspiration marginalized is a serious affront. Analysts can argue for their ideas well

beyond the balanced boundaries of logic, and being dismissed out of hand only

inflames them further. Arguments of principle versus practicality can quickly get out

of hand, causing serious damage to their relationships.

Active Minds, Closed Hearts

Both Executives and Analysts tend to live through rationality, and they very likely

experience a “meeting of the minds” that draws them together initially. While this

can lead to a spark of something deeper, both types often have difficulty

establishing or maintaining emotional connections. Executives and Analysts can

have unbalanced, yet functional lives together – even sexual relationships – and yet

markedly lack intimacy and emotional openness. Talking about their feelings might

seem confusing or unimportant to types who prize logic, and Executives and

Analysts may even see discussing feelings as some kind of admission of personal

weakness.

In ongoing love affairs, this is likely to become a problem. Executives and Analysts

have plenty of emotions, and when discomfort with those emotions prevents them

from expressing their feelings, they can’t effectively resolve unbalanced issues. The

frustration that even healthy, loving people occasionally feel with each other quickly

becomes dangerous when ignored or suppressed. Both types tend to focus on

fixing apparent problems with a rational approach, leaving inner emotional issues

continually festering.

A lack of emotional openness doesn’t just harm Executive–Analyst couples’ ability to

work through problems – it tragically decreases some of the most rewarding things

that loving couples can share together: warmth, reassurance, joy, childish

excitement, tenderness, and physical affection. Such things are rarely foremost in

either type’s mind, but they appreciate them just the same. In unbalanced

relationships, a lack of emphasis on these intimate behaviors diminishes the quality

of life for these lovers, and gradually diminishes the spark between them as well.

Rebalancing Executive–Analyst Relationships

Respecting the Needs of the Moment and the Future

Executives can respectfully point out to their Analyst partners that constant

exploring and experimenting not only bothers them, but it also consumes

significant resources within their relationships such as time, attention, and personal

energy. Analysts can remind themselves of their responsibility to manage their

shared resources, even intangible ones, in a way that’s acceptable to both types.

This means reigning in their imaginations now and then to focus on present needs

in their lives. They can show respect for their Executive partners by turning some of

their creative energy toward the major long-term advancement of their

relationships, as well as new ideas.

In turn, Executives can practice giving Analysts some respectful latitude. Analysts

can’t be happy ignoring problems or ideas, and are compelled to innovate,

sometimes learning much by creative failure. When Analysts demonstrate

responsible contributions to their relationships, they also earn the right to put time

and energy into their ideas and projects, no matter what anyone else thinks about

the merits. However, just because wise Executives indulge Analysts’ intellectual

exercises, it doesn’t mean they need to participate or hear about them constantly.

Cooperation, Together or Individually

These couples can agree to manage certain aspects of their shared lives

independently. For example, forward-thinking and imaginative Analysts could take

the lead in finding clever investments or shopping for deals on big purchases. Any

area where new or changing information must be evaluated appeals to their ability

to explore and optimize. In turn, Executives can organize and manage everyday

matters like ensuring bills are paid on time, and their schedules are kept, assuring

the stability of their shared lives.

These lovers may simply also have to compromise at times. These types are smart

to model their differing perspectives not as arguments, but as rational proposals.

Whether pushing for change or tradition, each must prove the benefits of their

method to the other. It logically follows that each type must also stay open to the

other’s ideas. Writing out benefits and costs can help Executive–Analyst couples

discuss compromises reasonably. Rather than wasting time endlessly debating, a

little hands-on demonstration with an open mind can also efficiently help them

decide what works best.

Keeping the Spark Alive

Emotional honesty is often hard for Executives, who want to simply force life to

cooperate without a lot of introspection. Emotional candor may feel like weakness

to them, as if exposing any vulnerability invites chaos. Analysts are similarly

reluctant, relatively ignorant about their inner emotions or those of others despite

their active imaginations. However, Executives and Analysts seeking to rebalance a

lack of intimacy must be open about their needs and feelings. This can start simply

enough with sharing their emotional states with their partners on a regular basis.

Instead of just discussing facts, partners can make a point of saying how they feel.

Here are some ways these couples can broach the subject.

● Saying, “I feel angry at what was just said,” is a fair way to give partners an

opportunity to make immediate amends before problems deepen.

● Or, if one arrives home from work stressed and frustrated, they can say so,

and their partner can offer support: “You do sound frustrated, want to talk

about it?”

● These types naturally fall into problem-solving together, but making a

conscious effort to also take care of each other on an emotional level helps

keep their sense of intimacy alive.

● Resolving issues with each other is important, but it’s just as critical for these

types to share positive emotions and experiences with each other. When one

is feeling happy or appreciative of the other, it’s important to take the time to

express it clearly.

● Improving intimacy isn’t just about supporting each other through

challenges, it’s also about sharing pleasurable, easy, and satisfying moments

in life together. Whether it’s cooking a special meal on a Friday night,

celebrating a professional triumph, or sharing a cozy massage, participating

in the sweeter parts of life alongside each other affirms strong, loving

relationships. For Executives and Analysts alike, it may take some conscious

effort to focus on these sentimental intangibles, but the gains are

tremendous.

Executive–Diplomat Relationships

Executives and Diplomats aren’t always easy romantic pairings, but while having a

great many differences, they share the ideal of dedication to the ones they love.

Diplomats feel their loved ones’ emotional states as if they were their own, wanting

to support their partners’ growth and happiness. Executives stride forward in life,

seeking to create a secure, prosperous environment for themselves and those they

love. Even though they develop their standards in different ways, both strive to be

instrumental in creating harmony.

Balanced Executive–Diplomat Relationships

Dedicated Couples

While Executives may not be overt with their feelings, they can give Diplomats the

steady support they need make functional progress. When Diplomats get lost in

their imagination or worry about what might happen, Executives pull them back to

the good things that already exist between them.

In return, Diplomats remind Executives that they can be loved for themselves, and

not just what they accomplish. Executives put a lot of pressure on themselves to

succeed and be recognized. Diplomats recognize them for who they are, putting the

well-being and happiness of their sweethearts above any material successes. To

Diplomats, the strengths and virtues of Executives’ character are just as worthy of

praise as their accomplishments. This might seem revolutionary to these practical

types, but it feels wonderful. Executives derive deep security from the fact that they

needn’t be always perfect to receive the warm devotion of their Diplomat lovers.

Dreams and Reality in Balance

Executives’ emphasis on practical accomplishment is the perfect supplement to

Diplomats’ focus on intangible development. Diplomats are inspired dreamers, and

Executives offer a pragmatic hand that counterbalances their airy ideas. Where

Diplomats might avoid life’s mundane details in favor of dreams, Executives help

them power through everyday responsibilities. Ultimately, Diplomats find this helps

make their dreams a reality, and love their Executive partners even more for

helping them grow.

Diplomats, in turn, try to help Executives grow beyond mundane routine.

Executives are highly goal-oriented, but the ambitiousness of those goals can be

limited by their focus on convention. Diplomats lovingly show them an expanded

view of their potential, inspiring Executives to new levels of success. Where

Executives might revel in achieving a high management position, Diplomats might

ask, “Why not start your own company, babe?” Through the supportive, idealistic

love of Diplomats, Executives discover exciting goals that they might not conceive

on their own, yet are within their reach.

Strong, Distinct Values

While they may arrive at their values very differently, these are both values-based

types. Executives settle on strict, tradition-oriented values through experience and

logic. Diplomats form values through philosophical exploration and a deep

empathetic connection to all life. Executive–Diplomat couples respect that they

both embrace balanced personal codes because they truly believe they’re the best

way to live their lives.

These couples can refine and update each other’s values. Executives may be so

focused on personal achievement that they fail to consider the advantages of

integration, dismissing anyone who doesn’t share their exact values. Diplomats are

all about universal harmony, though, and can help Executives develop their

personal status in balanced ways that connect to progressive, diverse, modern

values – more open doors mean more opportunities to advance.

On the flip side, Diplomats focus so broadly on moral and idealistic values that their

own lives often suffer. Executive lovers remind Diplomats that achieving great

things for themselves builds a strong foundation to express their dreams for the

world. Prosperity and personal power aren’t sins against humanity, and Executives’

values empower Diplomats beautifully. When these types each take a page from

the other’s book of values, they’re better off for it. Executives who are connected to

their humanity easily achieve noble status, and Diplomats with the tools to focus

and bring their ideas into reality are a benefit to the world.

Unbalanced Executive–Diplomat Relationships

The Practical Clashes with the Ideal

Executives and Diplomats loving each other is a wonderful thing, but these

partnerships don’t always unfold in balance. At first, Executives find that Diplomats’

focus on ideals appeals to their sense of rightness – they honor their tradition-

based values just as strongly. However, once the novelty wears off, they may

wonder how practical Diplomats are, and if they can turn talk into results.

Diplomats, meanwhile, may begin to feel that Executives lack imagination, and

wonder how such partners can truly understand what matters to them.

Executives want their sweethearts to become more grounded with time, as

achieving stable and prosperous lives together is always on their minds. However,

Diplomats often embrace continuous change rather than settling down. Their grand

visions for the future can seem to belittle the more realistic goals Executives work

so hard for, baffling these determined, practical types. For Executives, everything

worthwhile happens because someone dutifully follows a planned course of action,

something easily lost to Diplomats’ focus on things like politics, social justice, or

personal creativity. Often struggling to follow through on their big ideas, Diplomats

can just as easily forget to buy milk on the way home. Executives can be unforgiving

of this kind of failure, even in minor matters.

Executives are unlikely to see Diplomats’ grand-scale worries and dreams as

pertinent to their shared welfare. They perhaps can even see them as risks. These

types tend to be realistic about their concerns, and after a cursory appreciation of

Diplomats’ perspective, they may reject all the “what ifs” as an inefficient waste of

time and energy – nothing realistically actionable. Diplomats can be hurt by this

curt appraisal, often considering their open-ended thinking loftily superior, and

may allow this pain to deepen into unbalanced contempt for partners who “don’t

get” their high-mindedness.

When Emotions Confuse

Diplomats are far freer with their emotions than Executives, who prefer to control

or even hide their feelings. They may be in similar emotional states, but their styles

and degrees of expression are quite distinct. Diplomats’ freer feelings are often

quite confusing and troubling for Executives, who almost always prefer controlled

steadiness over openly expressed emotions. Likewise, Diplomats are confused by

Executives’ unbalanced refusal to emote, often worrying that it’s somehow a

reflection on them. Consequently, Diplomats may try to force Executives’ feelings

out, becoming even more upset if they can’t. Executives’ responses are likely to be

something unbalanced and unsympathetically practical along the lines of, “Get over

it.”

Executives don’t enjoy exposure to strong emotions, even by someone they love.

They may regard too much emotion – even their own – as weakness or self-

indulgence, and may respond in an unbalanced, callous way. Even worse, if

emotional pressure continues unchecked, it can begin to feel like a threat to the

stability of their lives. They may create distance as a form of self-defense, and

Diplomats sense this with dismay, further widening the gap. This is absolute poison

to loving relationships, so bringing emotional communication into balance is of

critical importance for these couples.

Rebalancing Executive–Diplomat Relationships

Open-Minded Dedication

Executives aren’t known for open-mindedly embracing change. Similarly, Diplomats

frequently ignore facts that contradict their ideals. For such couples, it goes beyond,

“We should do this,” versus “We should do that.” Balance starts with respectful

compromise, but also includes being truly open to each other’s points of view.

Appreciating that contributions to their relationships need not be similar to be

valuable requires both these types to loosen their strict priorities and consider each

other’s very different needs and virtues.

What this looks like on a day-to-day basis is asking how they can support each

other, expressing their needs honestly, and making a sincere effort to do whatever

it takes to make their time together happy, yet functional. In specific terms,

Executives must patiently acknowledge that Diplomats have a right to their less

tangible concerns and interests, even if they seem wildly impractical. If there’s one

thing that Executives understand, it’s the transactional nature of achievement, and

achieving fair and mutually fulfilling romantic relationships requires that they invest

hard work, just like any worthy goal. Here are some tips these couples can take to

heart.

● Whether it means attending a political rally, taking a painting class, or

learning to meditate, Executives can understand that sincerely engaging in

things outside their norm are ways to make fair contributions to happy lives

together with Diplomats.

● These practical types may also be surprised to find their interests expanded –

everything they love was also new to them at one point, and Executives are

rarely shy about engaging opportunities.

● If they can’t find any enjoyment in one thing, they can try something else, as

it’s the effort and openness that Diplomats appreciate.

In turn, Diplomats can understand the limits of their Executive partners: they can

only handle so much novelty and mystical openness without rolling their eyes.

Respecting Everyday Goals

In balanced relationships, Executives’ need for order, practicality, and advancement

in life aren’t only respected, they’re supported. This requires Diplomats to

sometimes focus their energy on mundane goals and methods instead of grand

ideals. If they’re not always taking an equal role in day-to-day responsibilities,

Diplomats can at least allow Executives to handle those things their own way.

Diplomats can be grateful to have lovers as rational, decisive, and dauntless as

Executives planning workable paths forward for their lives together.

Some days, instead of trying to push Executives to do something fun and inspired,

Diplomats can roll up their sleeves and join their partners in the routine trenches of

everyday life. This might mean mowing the lawn, deciding on a new investment, or

simply providing Executives with lunch and support while they figure things out.

Working hard on the practical priorities of the moment is a challenge for Diplomats,

but love for their sweethearts gives them a reason to try. A stable foundation and

progressive direction are both possible when these couples balance their time,

thoughts, and energy between ideal possibilities and current responsibilities. It’s

not easy, but the results of such balance are stunning.

Emotion and Logic in Proportion

Glorious potentials aside, reconciling Executive–Diplomat couples’ respective

tendencies to function through either logic or emotion is an ongoing challenge. It’s

critical that each type recalibrate their expectations of the other – frustration

happens when expectations are out of touch with reality. Executives get out of

touch by having unforgivingly strict standards, and Diplomats by having hopelessly

imaginative ideals. However, Executives can’t reasonably expect Diplomat partners

to be coolly sensible all the time, nor can Diplomats reasonably expect constant

emotional sensitivity from Executives.

With realistic expectations in mind, these partners can respect each other’s unique

ways of expressing themselves. Executives rarely give Diplomats the lush,

emotional feedback they crave, but they express their love and commitment

through sincere dedication and tireless effort. Diplomats can appreciate the way

their Executive partners enhance their shared lives with prosperity and security.

In turn, Executives understand that loving relationships include profound and

honest emotional connections. Diplomats can encourage them toward further

openness by ensuring that sharing emotions is a non-volatile experience. Both

these types grow as individuals and build stronger relationships by imitating

qualities of the other. Executives can work to explore and share their own

emotions, and Diplomats can practice judicious self-control of emotion.

In everyday terms, Executives can make themselves talk about what they feel, and

Diplomats can pause to really think before sharing. Both types can also take a hard

look together at the chain of events that led to any emotional issues between them.

Talking it through step by step gives them a chance to spot false assumptions,

explain themselves, and clear the air of overreaction. Making efforts to bridge each

other’s level of emotionality doesn’t mean sacrificing their unique natures, but it

allows these types to come together with true understanding and honesty.

Executive–Sentinel Relationships

Couples sharing many core traits have an easier time understanding each other, so

these pairings are often comfortable matches. Relationships between Executives

and other Sentinels thrive on their sense of duty, commitment, and mutual

constancy. While the specifics may differ, each understands the other’s desire to

build useful foundations and maintain the traditions that define their worlds. They

both appreciate a clear path forward, and try to proceed sensibly and smoothly

without complicating things. Diligence might be the first quality they notice and

admire about each other, beyond initial physical attraction.

Balanced Executive–Sentinel Relationships

Consistency

From the moment they decide to commit, things are quite serious between

Executives and other Sentinels in love. Both types feel a strong pull toward

stabilizing things. They both like to focus on building better, more secure lives, and

having significant others with the same goals satisfyingly reinforces their

momentum. When they feel sure of their lovers being “the one,” plans are made in

clear terms and engaged briskly. When two Sentinel types merge, genuinely

responsible mutual caretaking can develop that’s unsurpassed by other personality

types.

Sharing a Down-to-Earth Approach

Executives love Sentinels for their hands-on practicality. Together, they embrace

established wisdom and understanding rather than conjured theoretical musings.

That’s not to say their relationships are devoid of imagination, but they find that

putting their energy into practical matters and proven methods tends to be more

productive. Their sensible attention to immediate needs assures them that their

lives can improve steadily. When they dream of a better future, it usually happens,

as they embrace reasonable, achievable goals.

Unlike those who feel rewarded by dreaming itself, these couples feel a sense of

purposeful satisfaction when they accomplish practical goals for themselves. They

may have differing ways of engaging the world, but their respective approaches

lead to the same destination. These couples work well together from an early stage,

with Executives contributing a vigorous, logical sensibility and Sentinels adding their

own unique forms of prudence and perseverance. They have little trouble seeing

each other’s functional virtues, so fairness and cooperation are likely within these

love affairs.

Comfort with Differences

Because Executives and other Sentinels share some key ways of approaching life,

balancing other trait differences is also more approachable. For example,

Executives in love with Consuls can comfortably enjoy the warmer side of social

experiences. Their partners’ energy and boldness match their own, but with added

levels of empathy and charisma right at the surface.

Romances with Consuls or Defenders give Executives chances to connect through

emotions while respecting their focus on convention. Where Diplomats might try to

pull them too far afield, these Sentinels draw them close into reassuring, loving

nests, with Defenders especially placing great importance on their private lives as

couples. Within the personal traditions they build with these types, Executives find

it easy to open their hearts, relax their logic, and grow in balanced ways.

When paired with Logistician partners, Executives may begin to see the virtues of

reserved contemplation versus swift action. While both types tend to be logical and

sensible, Logisticians place even more value on carefully making the right decision,

and aren’t afraid to take the time to do so. Their caution is a good balancing

element for Executives’ often brash, forceful manner when confronted by life’s

challenges.

Unbalanced Executive–Sentinel Relationships

Too Much of a Good Thing

When individuals with similar traits fall in love, such combinations can also result in

too much of a good thing. Even tradition-bound Sentinels pass through stages of

change, and may become bored or frustrated with the very routines that once

made them happy. There’s a difference between satisfaction and joy, and these

types may become so focused on stability that joy drains from their lives, leaving

only predictable routines in its place.

Not only may these couples find that satisfaction alone eventually becomes

insufficient, but the danger is even worse if both Executives and other Sentinel

lovers don’t share the sense of stagnation. When one type questions the direction

or quality of their life and begins to desire change, and the other doesn’t, it can feel

like an abandonment of everything they’ve built together. Judgmental and bold,

Executives may be especially unforgiving in unbalanced relationships, regardless of

which side they’re on. They don’t take kindly to those who threaten the desired

course of their lives, and if their partners push too strongly for change or try to hold

them in unhappy routines, Executives can forcefully resist in unbalanced ways.

Missed Opportunities

Even if they never disagree on aspects of their lives together, such couples may

become insulated inside the worlds they know. Executive–Sentinel couples in

unbalanced relationships don’t pay much attention to anything outside of those

spaces, possibly ignoring highly beneficial opportunities that seem too risky. When

they reinforce each other’s reliance on the status quo, these significant others

never discover their options, much less reach out to them. Executives are far from

immune – while they don’t hesitate to surge toward their goals, those goals often

show a lack of broader vision. All their energy and willpower doesn’t guarantee

awareness or acceptance of new potentials, especially if their Sentinel partners are

also focused only on what’s already known.

Personal growth, success, and prosperity almost always entail a measure of risk.

However, Executive–Sentinel partners in unbalanced relationships support each

other’s security rather than growth. Trying new paths may seem pointless when a

perfectly good situation already exists – especially one they worked so hard to

create together. If these types reassure each other that change isn’t needed, they

hold each other back. Worse, they may dislike change so much that they

deliberately ignore circumstances that desperately need improvement.

Certainty, or Tyranny?

The Judging trait inevitably contains a degree of certainty, an intrinsic way of

thinking for both partners in Executive–Sentinel relationships. However, this in no

way assures common preferences between the pair – one may like chocolate, the

other, vanilla. The only thing they may agree on is wanting it their own way. Once

Executives decide something is correct, they expect to move forward with it, and

their blustery, autonomous decision-making can rankle their lovers.

Executives can be too pushy, but it may not occur to either of these types to ask

their partners before making “obvious” choices if they’re sure they’re proceeding

correctly. Such unbalanced high-handedness, even with the best of intentions, can

be very damaging to their Sentinel lovers’ sense of worth. Controlling behavior is

truly the dark side of willfulness, and is the opposite of the practical compromise

that all Sentinels embrace in balanced relationships. When one type dominatingly

overshadows decisions, long-term happiness can’t prosper because freedom isn’t

shared.

Rebalancing Executive–Sentinel Relationships

Venturing into Something New Together

Individuals in balanced relationships undertake necessary adaptive behavior to get

through life successfully. In this spirit, Executive–Sentinel couples can shake things

up occasionally to find new opportunities and joy. Improvement is, by definition, a

type of change, and must be sought consciously. A foray outside their routines is

the first step to their achieving greater prosperity, strength, and happiness within

their relationships. Fortunately, once these determined types engage something

together, that decision alone provides shared motivation, as neither type commits

lightly or gives up easily.

The key is in agreeing together to make an ongoing effort to expand. Emphasizing

accepting new things into their lives cures notable flaws in these romances, such as

stagnation and narrow vision. Partners in Executive–Sentinel couples might

periodically ask themselves, “How can I improve my life?” and then ask their lovers

to join them in testing the waters, be it trying a new restaurant, taking academic or

recreational classes, or even researching and planning major things like a new

career. Contemplating and engaging changes together takes much of the

trepidation out of the experience, as these partners have each other’s reliable

support.

Compromise is Necessary

The Judging trait, with all its certitude, doesn’t have to serve as a lock on the mind.

When it does, however, the heart provides the key. When these couples disagree,

they must remind themselves of the deep appreciation they feel for each other’s

presence in their lives. The love they share motivates compromise. Fairly

supporting each other’s preferences doesn’t require either type to change their

own, just that they trade off supporting what the other prefers, some of the time.

The following are some things these couples can keep in mind.

● Executives can make a special effort to support their Sentinel partners’ needs

and desires. They almost reflexively enforce their own views and methods,

and while this may sometimes enhance practical aspects of life, it has no

place when it comes to matters of personal preference.

● No matter how certain Executives are that pumpkin is the proper type of pie

at the holidays, their partners’ tastes deserve respectful inclusion, not

dismissal. To do any less is to say their partners aren’t equally worthy, and

won’t allow balanced relationships to prosper.

Always with an eye on their chosen goal, Executives place the success of their

romances above differences within their balanced relationships, and give as much

as they get.

Executive–Explorer Relationships

Executive–Explorer couples are frequently an odd combination, but such pairings

can be quite serviceable for both. As Observant types, both tend to be hands-on

and present-minded, but in different ways. Executives manage matters at hand

responsibly to assure long-term success, living in the present but planning for a

stable future. They consider consequences and then act decisively. Contrarily,

Explorers regard every moment as an opportunity for immediate satisfaction or

accomplishment, and future concerns aren’t as important to them. This allows

them to be flexible, and they’re often able to get around restrictions to achieve their

goals. This is helpful, as those goals are likely to change often.

Balanced Executive–Explorer Relationships

Opposites Do Attract

Opposites do attract. In fact, being vastly different can generate wonderful

relationships because it offers balance. Attraction between Executives and

Explorers sometimes happens because wise Executives realize they could use some

unconventional inspiration to help them meet their goals. Explorers bring plenty of

that, often spontaneously attacking and completing tasks that interest them.

Executives find such fresh, effective productivity very attractive, indeed, and can

jump in with Explorers, bringing their own energy to the party. On the other hand,

when Explorers tire of the practical difficulties of living spontaneously, Executives

may seem like a very appealing stabilizing element. Executives offer support that

keeps Explorers’ lives functioning, which ultimately enhances their ability to

explore.

Completing Each Other

Executives tend to create a secure space for themselves that’s tightly defined and

all about functional and efficient order. Being alone in that safe, stable world can

make their lives dull, though. Adding Explorers to their lives is a way of creating

unexpected opportunities that cure dull routines. Explorers bring options aplenty,

and Executives can maintain their energetic common sense while gathering

knowledge through diverse hands-on experiences with these lively lovers.

When Executives and Explorers combine their lives, it can result in very real and

satisfying unions. There may be some utilitarian symmetry in such relationships,

but these types can learn a lot from each other. They can each give essential, but

very different, kinds of support that help them both mature and grow. In balanced

romances, Explorers’ curiosity amends their partners’ rigidity, and Executives’

judiciousness tempers their sweethearts’ exuberance.

How Executives find rewarding relationships with Explorers varies as much as

Explorers themselves. Adventurers and Entertainers offer attentive sensitivity, with

the former always conceiving new ways that these couples can engage life together,

and the latter offering active social lives that appeal to Executives’ ambition.

Being capable and attaining goals is something Executives respect, and Virtuosos’

focus on mastering specific areas of skill is just such a quality. Likewise, the gainful

effectiveness of Executive–Entrepreneur couples is amazing; they might build

empires for themselves. If Executive–Explorer couples regard their differences as

beneficial counter-balancing assets and maintain a healthy degree of tolerance,

they can create outstanding bonds and much success together.

Unbalanced Executive–Explorer Relationships

Roles that Grow Old

Differences can bring about balance, but Executives and Explorers can also easily

fall into roles with each other that become so distinct and repetitious that they

create separation instead of unity. Executives tend to take on roles of organizing,

planning, and meeting obligations, and Explorers are likely to be the more

unrestrained, fun-loving members of these duos. These roles can work well when

each feels the benefits of the other’s approach, but quickly grow tiresome and

oppressive if taken too far.

If these couples aren’t careful, their relationships might start to resemble that of

parents and children. It’s burdensome for Executives to take all the responsibility

while Explorers enjoy a carefree existence, and Explorers resent being controlled by

rules they don’t understand the need for. Executives aren’t likely to be patient with

this imbalance for long, so these patterns can become severely problematic if

unaddressed.

Stability and Spontaneity in Conflict

Executives are usually deliberately set in their ways, while Explorers adapt their

opinions or interests easily. This arrangement can easily frustrate Executives’ need

for a sense of reliability in their partners. In turn, Explorers may see their beloveds’

unwillingness to change as a hindrance to their free expression. Explorers are

independent and have a great deal of difficulty with commitments that they find

confining. They rankle at rules and direction, two fundamental things to Executives’

way of thinking.

In contrast, rules help make Executives comfortable, and in their relationships,

they’re likely to insist on defining commitment early on. Unfortunately, Explorers’

independence often makes them resist signing on to a committed lifestyle, at least

right away. They can demonstrate great longevity in love, but not if stressed with

too many strict demands, as that kind of pressure just pushes them away. If one

type seeks commitment while the other yearns for freedom, the early stages of any

romance proceeds with great difficulty.

Rebalancing Executive–Explorer Relationships

Respectful Communication

The key to balancing tiresome relationship roles is viewing the issue as a matter of

respect. Executives can respect themselves enough to reject the burden of being

solely responsible in their households. If they’re playing the “parents” too often, it’s

time for a talk – again, with respect playing a starring role. There’s nothing wrong

with saying, “I’m super stressed and busy, can you do a load of laundry to help me

out?” When Explorers expand their participation in responsibilities, they can also

create freedom for themselves, as balanced contributions create a solid sense of

being equals.

Respect goes both ways in balanced relationships. Executives can remember that

they fell in love with someone who has a higher tolerance for disorder and a lower

tolerance for mundane activity. When the dishes need doing, Explorers’ attention

may be elsewhere, but finding ways to make such mundane things more exciting

helps these couples get them done together. A little music and wine can sweeten a

household chore into a more pleasant shared experience. In loving relationships,

nobody should confuse encouraging more responsibility with demanding it, but

both types must participate.

Benefitting From Each Other’s Ways

Compromise is crucial to finding a workable way past differences, and drawing lines

that are too firm is counterproductive. Below are some tips these twosomes can

try.

● Explorers’ flexible mindset can be a useful inspiration in this area. When

Executives borrow just a little of their partners’ open-minded outlook while

relying on their natural ability to organize practically, solutions that make

sense to both quickly appear.

● Rather than trying to push Explorers uncomfortably into detailed planning,

Executives can reveal each part of a plan in sequence, allowing Explorers to

focus more easily on what’s in front of them. This also creates a sense of

spontaneity that helps Explorers engage naturally.

● Likewise, when Explorers realize they need to step up their contributions to

the functional parts of their relationships, they can borrow their Executive

lovers’ dutiful mindset.

However, the good news for Explorers is that they needn’t force themselves into

the rigid, organized way of thinking natural to Executives – their partners already

have a firm enough sense of priorities and responsibilities. All they need to do is

willingly bring their natural energy, adaptable minds, and capable hands to the

party. Chances are, Executives can provide some productive, sensible ideas of

where Explorers can put these great virtues to excellent use.

Conclusion Embracing Possibilities

While they tend to be highly effective people on their own, relationships present

Executives with wonderful opportunities to grow. Normally, these types feel little

pull to open their mind to unplanned change, but love can give them powerful

motivation to advance beyond their limits and habits. Partners in balanced

relationships don’t expect this to happen overnight, but for their part, Executives

can push themselves out of a rut, appreciating inspiration and guidance from

romantic partners they love and trust.

Executives who give themselves permission to intimately embrace their lovers’

differences are likely to find more joy than they have ever experienced. All they

need is a willingness to relax and be open – they can maintain their common sense

and drive. Releasing themselves from certain routines may be a little destabilizing

at first, but the rewards are tremendous. Executives find their worlds brightened

and their minds inspired by loving people with different perspectives.

These types seek to create trust, stability, and successful living – and they’re willing

to share. While this kind of firm foundation isn’t always sought by every type, it’s a

pleasant surprise when it’s discovered. Executives offer lovers support and security

that allow them to reach higher and do more than they could on their own. When

initial chemistry is followed up by ongoing tolerance and honesty, there’s no reason

why Executives can’t enjoy lasting and balanced love with any personality type.

# Friendships

Earning Executives’ friendship isn’t quick or easy – people with this personality type

are vigorously exacting, and prefer to connect through standard practices and

values more than ideas and feelings. Most of their friends are made not through

random encounters, but through consistent contact, such as in class or in the

workplace, where they’ve got the time to get to know and admire each other. These

types like to see people’s merits proven before granting them their friendship.

High Standards

What makes Executives’ friendships grow is the mutual respect and trust that

comrades give each other. Functionality and stability are paramount to these types,

and friends who reflect these qualities are valuable assets in their lives. They rarely

find the orderly predictability they seek in other people, so they especially

appreciate friends who are reliable. Executives crave a world where people are

responsible, trustworthy, and capable – friendships that offer these values satisfy

them deeply.

Comfortable with Detachment

Executives give an energetic form of friendship that revolves around shared

commonality, but not much emotional intimacy. They take a brisk, no-nonsense

approach to life, and aren’t given to social softness or warmth. Trusted friends are

useful bridges in this area, showing people with this personality type the direct

value of deeper human connection. Through friendship, Executives balance their

somewhat brusque outlook with the practical benefits of having good people

around.

While they tend to be socially outgoing, these types don’t need friendship to feel

secure. Executives find people’s overt emotions tedious, often disdaining sensitive

or inconsistent people, and they’re quite comfortable on their own. So, when they

do find others who seem worthy, the favor they grant in the form of friendship is

significant. If these people disappoint, however, Executives become dismissive –

they don’t easily give second chances.

Strong as Stone

An uncompromising nature is just one side of the Executive social coin, as their

resolute core also provides them with appealing fortitude and energy. They’re

always ready to use their tireless diligence and intelligence to help their true friends

come up with thoughtful solutions. Vigorous, practical, and principled, Executives

make excellent friends, if their camaraderie can be won in the first place.

Where Executives’ rigidity can hold them back socially is their unwillingness to try

new things. They aren’t easily intimidated, but also aren’t easily impressed or

enthused by unproven possibilities. However, Executives do step outside their

boundaries if given a substantial reason, and good friends draw them into new

things by demonstrating the benefits. When these types deem others worthy as

friends, they also value their judgement, and this opens them up to sampling the

unknown.

Commonality and Community

Executives’ desire for stability and status is reflected by their social choices, as they

like being around those who respect their values and culture. Friendships are likely

to flourish in environments that reflect what they’ve decided on through their own

experiences. Of course, what’s compatible depends on their personal history as

individuals – the only sure thing that they’re very specific about is their beliefs.

Executives are socially outgoing in whatever community feels like home, but rarely

feel pulled to explore other ways of life. But those seeking to diversify their

friendships wisely look for groups formed around common subjects and pursuits,

from gardening to gaming to sports. This is an easy way for these types to get to

know and better understand people with different views and backgrounds. Sharing

an interest means agreeing on at least that one thing.

Pleasant Surprises

Executives’ exceedingly specific social preferences don’t prevent them from building

friendships with very different types of people – those people just need to impress

them consistently. If there’s one thing Executives appreciate, it’s finding others who

are reliable and capable. These types can’t help but respect extremely effective

individuals, regardless of how they operate, and it’s even better if they also prove to

be personally trustworthy.

Executives and Friends of Other Types Executives can enjoy friendship with almost anyone who meets such essential

standards. We’ll look at some of the virtues that these types can share with each

Role group through friendship, as well as some areas of potential difficulty. Most

importantly, we’ll also go over some helpful things Executives can do to make

friendships work with other personality types.

Executive–Analyst Friendships

Executive–Analyst friendships form easily, as these types have a lot in common.

However, the headstrong, often confrontational nature of Commanders and

Debaters can challenge Executives’ patience. Luckily, Executives are bold enough to

match the energetic willpower of these types, and can firmly offer some sensible

advice when they get too carried away. These Analysts, in turn, respect Executives’

strength of character and admire their steady, level-headed ability. When

Executives befriend them, they, in turn, gain gutsy, creative allies to help them

overcome life’s toughest problems, making for nearly unlimited possible success.

Balanced Executive–Analyst Friendships

Support and Assistance

Not all Analyst friends are so challenging to be around, though. Executives

appreciate Logicians’ and Architects’ quiet, thoughtful natures. They see how these

types take the time to figure out ingenious solutions and find their input helpful on

nearly everything. It may not always be easy to understand their curious musings,

but their calculated ideas appeal to Executives’ sense of logic. Analysts’ ingenuity

bolsters their ideas, and in return, they offer such friends practical steps to help

convert their creative thoughts into active plans.

Freedom to Say What They Think

Executives and Analysts share a common problem: they aren’t very sensitive to

people’s feelings, and sometimes use inconsiderate, direct ways of communicating.

When dealing with other personality types, this often results in conversational faux

pas, forcing them to reverse course and confusedly apologize to keep the peace.

Constantly having to adjust their words so that they don’t offend more fragile

people is frustrating – it’s as if they’re always on a leash and can’t be themselves.

In the company of Analysts, however, Executives find incredible freedom of speech.

They may be asked to explain their ideas and opinions, but they won’t need to

apologize for the way they voice them. These types can relax and speak their truest,

harshest views of the world together with little chance of bruised egos and hurt

feelings. Compared to their attempts to get along with more sensitive personality

types, this can feel like a relief. Executives find a dynamic conversational

playground among Analyst friends, who are always happy to hear their honest

thoughts.

Unbalanced Executive–Analyst Friendships

Disappointing Disconnection

There are challenges, of course – in unbalanced friendship, Executives can find

Analysts to be unrealistic and annoyingly focused on ideas rather than necessary

action. The fact that Executives operate on the proven, and Analysts like to theorize,

is a stumbling block even in social or recreational matters. These two types might

live in clashing mental time zones, with Executives focusing on what is, and Analysts

on what might be. In unbalanced relationships, Executives may roll their eyes

dismissively at Analysts’ grand, esoteric plans, and may, in turn, be frustrated by

Analysts’ utter indifference to their more realistic interests.

To make this disconnection worse, Executives and Analysts both share a neglectful

attitude toward maintaining friendships. Once they put in the initial effort to

establish a solid rapport, both types may begin to take it for granted, thinking

nothing of long silences or absences. This indicates a certain trust in each other, but

functionally, such friends may simply drift apart through mutual carelessness and

lack of effort. If they don’t engage often enough, they miss out on the long-term

benefits of the virtues they saw in each other in the first place.

Conflicts of Will

Another likely problem in unbalanced Executive–Analyst friendships is a clash of

will. Analysts love to argue their interests and opinions, constantly trumpeting their

views and mentally sparring with those who disagree. It’s as if these conversations –

or arguments – provide them with mental stimulation. Executives also hold firm

views and are unlikely to back down, but because they don’t share the joy of

animated debate, they’re quickly irritated by the ordeal of arguing with Analysts.

Sharing anything from political views to opinions on where to eat lunch can become

frustrating for both types. Analysts may think Executives are dull-minded, and

Executives, in turn, wonder if Analysts are all talk. Executives often just want a

decision, not a debate, and may try to steamroll Analysts for the sake of a

conclusion. Obviously, this can result in conflict. If these types don’t find enough

patience to talk things through, they eventually get fed up with each other’s strong

wills.

Rebalancing Executive–Analyst Friendships

Connecting to Each Other’s Virtues

Executives and Analysts can build and maintain healthy connections by tapping into

the logical benefits of being friends. Executives can foster realism and grounded

practicality – critical strengths that Analysts often lack. Given their reputation for

rational thought, Analysts sometimes use oddly narrow reasoning to justify their

moves, especially when fixated on an idea. Executives help them avoid getting lost

in interesting possibilities, as well as encourage them to be mindful of looming

probabilities. Executives might research and respectfully share some solid facts to

counter Analysts’ wilder ideas in an act of caring support, not criticism.

They can benefit from Analysts’ clever, unconventional views, as their tried-and-true

practices often prove limited. Times change, and they’re always looking to optimize.

Occasionally, changing their approach is an unavoidable part of this. Analysts love

beating challenges through innovation, and Executives can call on their friends’

creativity to help them find answers with their fresh perspective. Frequently

requesting and offering input back and forth on everyday matters is a great way for

these friends to stay involved each other’s lives.

Considerate Honesty

Because of their aggressive intellect, it’s difficult to match wits with Analysts, but

Executives have the strength to firmly and fairly express themselves. They don’t

need to argue: if they calmly explain the reason for their preferences, they get

results. It can be as simple as saying, “You always want to try new restaurants, but it

bums me out when they don’t have something I like. I’d go out more often if we

could also visit my favorite places.” There’s no rational argument against mutual

respect and consideration in friendships, but they require patient communication.

Consideration goes both ways, and Executives can work to respect Analysts’ many

ideas and preferences as well. Constantly resisting change or new ideas by saying, “I

don’t like it, so it’s not worth my time,” doesn’t represent respectful investment in

valued friends. They can support their Analyst friends’ interests now and then, even

if they’re somewhat abstract and speculative. They can bring their more reserved

rationality along for the ride, and expect to learn a few things.

Thoughtful Support

Functioning, balanced friendships with Analysts are certainly rewarding for both

sides. Here are some benefits both types can keep in mind while trying to keep

their friendships on the right track.

● Executives offer Analysts a sensible ear for their intellectual ranting that

helps soothe their frustration with a dysfunctional world. Being dutiful,

stable, and proactive is natural for these types, and Analysts need steadfast

friends with minds sharp enough to understand them.

● In turn, Executives gain sincere, thoughtful support from their Analyst

friends, whose vigorous minds are always at the ready. Analysts lead them to

knowledge they might not discover through their more conventional

perspective, but also offer nimble, reasoned reflection in times of trouble.

Finally, Analysts help Executives work through personal problems rationally without

prying into their emotions too deeply, and this is a great comfort.

Executive–Diplomat Friendships

Executive–Diplomat friendships aren’t always quick fits, but they do hold great

potential, especially if shared interests are present. These personality types both

want to make the world a better place, and deeply value cooperation, but take very

different paths to this harmonious ideal. Executives vigorously implement systems

to create functionality for all, and Diplomats create alignment by valuing individuals’

emotions and needs. These friendships maintain balance when each accepts the

way the other thinks and contributes – each brings a big dose of what the other

lacks.

Balanced Executive–Diplomat Friendships

Compatible Values

One of the most rewarding parts of Executive–Diplomat friendships is their

cooperation to bring their values into reality. Diplomats never tire of sharing ideas

for improving the world, though they often fail to separate true idealism from

realistic endeavors. Executives can sift through their bubbling idealism to find ideas

that work, and help make them happen. When it comes to order, duty, and

certainty, Executives relate well to the methods of Protagonists and Advocates, as

the Judging trait offers an overlap in perspective.

Diplomats, especially Mediators and Campaigners, can be overly whimsical, but

their creativity intrigues Executives. They make an effort to appreciate their friends’

sometimes unusual paths, and find personal inspiration in their active

imaginations. In turn, Diplomats in balanced friendships admire Executives’ precise

minds. Their instant grasp of reality is a refreshingly clear mode of thought to those

whose own minds are whirling with many ideas. Patience is required for these very

different types to form friendship, but it’s a worthwhile effort with a big payoff for

both types.

Discovering Knowledge

Executives thrive on knowledge, but despite their boldness, they aren’t often

adventurous by nature. This results in depth of experience, but not breadth –

leaving them relatively unprepared when life brings the unfamiliar to their

doorstep. However, Diplomats’ empathetic friendship is the perfect vessel for

Executives to try new things and broaden their capability. They’re unlikely to push

Executives into things they strongly disapprove of, as they’re very attentive to their

feelings. In this supportive atmosphere, Executives can take a few intellectual risks

to grow and learn, knowing that the more they experience in life, the more

successful their mastery of it.

The flip side is how wise and sensible Executives can be – they encourage and

support their Diplomat friends by sharing realistic alternative ideas for achieving

their grand dreams. They appreciate the way Executives forge smooth paths

through life, tirelessly turning solid plans into stable reality. Their focused, energetic

dedication is a lifeline to Diplomats who feel lost in chaos. When these chums each

put their best feet forward, they balance idealism and practicality to notably

improve each other’s lives.

Unbalanced Executive–Diplomat Friendships

Different Realities

Unfortunately, such friendships may experience friction with the realization that

their differing approaches or belief systems are incompatible in certain situations.

Executives look to the past for tried and true examples of what works best, while

Diplomats happily embrace revolutionary change. In unbalanced situations,

Diplomats may become frustrated at Executives’ insistence on working toward

stability instead of joining them in what they see as progress. Executives see

following the rules as an ideal, and in turn, may become disturbed when they see

Diplomat friends breaking the rules for an ideal. These two types often feel as

though they’re living in different worlds.

This really hits home when these types express opinions on what is or isn’t fair.

Executives see fairness in applying reasonable rules dispassionately and equally.

Diplomats, on the other hand, like to create fairness through conditional

approaches based on moral ideals. Executives may focus more on individual

responsibility and drive to achieve equality, whereas Diplomats often focus on how

people can establish equality by helping elevate each other.

Another difference is how Diplomats often chase unrealistic dreams. Executives

find this behavior increasingly tiring when those dreams always seem to be on the

horizon. These types respect realistic thinking, bold effort, and accomplishment,

and are bothered by how willingly Diplomats – especially Campaigners and

Mediators – embrace seemingly impossible dreams. Frustration can result if these

friends can’t reconcile their very different approaches to life.

Upsetting Each Other

The differences that can come to threaten these friendships aren’t just about

judgement or opinion: watching friends embrace things that seem imprudent, or

even perilous, is frustrating for Executives. Whether it’s dubious romantic interests,

implausible   beliefs,   or   precarious   financial    habits,   Diplomats’   sometimes

unbalanced, haphazard lifestyle worries characteristically practical Executives. They

can feel frustrated when trying to avert Diplomat friends’ questionable decisions, as

pragmatic logic isn’t always persuasive to those who live through emotion.

Unfortunately, Executives can also cause offense when they share sharply

cautionary advice, no matter how true it is. They become critical when something

seems flawed to them, and to Diplomats, this feels like someone stepping on their

dreams. They’re hurt by Executives’ firm rejection of fantastical possibilities, seeing

it as a lack of support. Such unbalanced conflicts threaten these friendships, as

either type may become dismissive: Executives can become exasperated and

unsympathetically “wash their hands” of the matter, and Diplomats may cease

contact   with   those   they     see    as    fundamentally    negative,   a    common

misinterpretation of even the best-intentioned criticism or correction.

Rebalancing Executive–Diplomat Friendships

Explaining Perspectives

Resolving clashing views within Executive–Diplomat friendships requires a different

approach from logical arguments. These friends don’t need to agree with their

lifestyles, but they can explain the adverse effects they feel from each other’s

behavior. For Executives, this may include functional concerns, and for Diplomats,

it’s more about feelings. Either way, honesty and respect are key. When Executives

learn that their behavior is hurting their friends, reason dictates a change if those

friends are truly valued and respected. Here are some ways Executives can

approach conflicts with their Diplomat chums.

● When Executives take the time to gently explain why they’re troubled, it

appeals to Diplomats’ sense of compassion, and they act accordingly. It can

be as simple as saying, “That doesn’t make sense to me. Can we talk things

over together, so I can understand you better?” Logical facts aren’t as

important to Diplomats as their friends’ welfare, and if asked respectfully to

change their behavior a little, they’re extremely receptive. These types

needn’t always see eye-to-eye to get along.

● The harder part for Executives is comprehending and meeting Diplomats’

need for emotional support. An effective approach is to figure out Diplomats’

implicit requests when they share their feelings, and give the desired

responses. When these types express themselves, they’re often just seeking

validation and support. So, when Diplomats say, “I’m worried about making

my rent,” the response that best helps them might be, “Everything will be all

right because I believe in you,” rather than, “You should move to a more

affordable place, so this doesn’t keep happening.”

● Less specific examples include listening and making friends feel valued by

offering reassurance or encouraging input. There’s also no harm in asking,

“What can I do here to help you?” as Diplomats appreciate any sincere effort.

This is also a supportive, subtle way for Executives to get Diplomat friends to

consider the practicality of their ideas – how something might or might not

be possible.

● Diplomats want attentive sympathy, and Executives can approximate this

through deliberate acts of vigorous support. They can also gently tell

Diplomats when they’re truly at their limits of emotional understanding or

participation in something foreign to them.

In turn, Diplomats can moderate their expectations, and respect Executives by not

placing heavy demands on them to frequently engage in emotion or fanciful ideas.

While Executives and Diplomats may grow very close, Diplomats can understand

they may need to find deep emotional engagement among other friends.

Stepping In, and Stepping Back

Diplomats may be sensitive to criticism, but caring Executive friends recognize

when it’s time to offer their advice and help – and boldly step in, as is their nature.

Executives can accept that Diplomats are dreamers, but there are moments when a

more grounded perspective is vital, even if it isn’t requested. It’s not always easy to

intervene, but they risk regret if avoiding emotional confrontations makes them

withhold guidance from friends in trouble.

If their advice is ignored, Executives can respectfully retreat, making their ongoing

support clear while holding true to their viewpoints. They may have little choice but

to accept a continuous stream of questionable ideas and choices from Diplomat

friends. If this happens, Executives can take pragmatic solace in knowing that they

tried to help, and decline to be drawn in. Avoiding participation in things they can’t

support helps them maintain healthy friendships with Diplomats, even if they

require their stepping back now and then.

Executive–Sentinel Friendships

Other Sentinels are good fits for Executives, as they share an appreciation for

dependability and practicality. These friendships support day-to-day activities and

general quality of life more than change, ambition, or curiosity. Uncomplicated

friendships with other Sentinels reassure and buoy Executives. Having others who

are steadfast and sensible in their lives is no small consideration for these types.

Balanced Executive–Sentinel Friendships

Reassuring Steadiness

Executives also find other Sentinels’ conventional social leanings appealing,

whether such friends are Introverts or Extraverts. Their discussions revolve around

grounded matters like work, current life events, and personal interests. Sentinels

aren’t big on esoteric topics, and consider those who constantly talk about broad,

impersonal matters a bit baffling – they like to use the time to catch up with each

other. Among Sentinel friends, Executives get a pleasant dose of real life rather

than ideological grandstanding.

These friendships go beyond casual compatibility, however. When serious

situations and major life events happen, Executives also appreciate how Sentinel

friends stand by their side through thick and thin. They offer the same in return,

acting as strong, vocal supporters for those who earn their friendship. Executives

and other Sentinels stand an excellent chance of becoming each other’s best

friends for a lifetime.

Stability in Action

Favoring stable foundations doesn’t mean that these friends don’t care about

progress. However, where Intuitive or Prospecting types see a future teeming with

endless possibilities, Executives like everything moving firmly in a controlled

direction. Those with the Executive personality type may provide more energy than

others ask for, but either way, having Sentinel friends with an organized way of

living and equally sensible goals helps everyone keep moving forward.

When difficulties arise, Executives are often the first to step forward with a helping

hand, and their Sentinel friends respect and sincerely appreciate their ready

devotion. In turn, Sentinels’ judicious touch perfect for Executives in times of

trouble. Whether offering thoughtful solutions or simply some dutiful, hands-on

help, these comrades are cores of support for each other. This creates the security

they need to form deep and grateful bonds with each other.

Relatable Differences

Opposing personality traits may be easier for Executives to relate to when other

Sentinels display them. Consuls’ compassionate Extraversion inspires them toward

warmth, as it demonstrates creating structure through personal connections.

Defenders’ quiet sensitivity is charming to Executives, and their pragmatic, patient

acceptance creates a gentle sense of connection. The reserved, thoughtful nature

of Logisticians impresses them, as they see how taking the time to contemplate

things before acting assures absolute efficiency.

Different Sentinel friends offer Executives doorways to growth, expanding some of

their limits while staying soberly grounded alongside them. Shared values focusing

on stability, tradition, and dedication make friendships within this Role a good way

for Executives to find supportive bonds.

Unbalanced Executive–Sentinel Friendships

Social Problems

While these types are compatible, Executives can fail Sentinels in emotionally-

charged situations such as breakups, arguments with other friends, or deaths in

their families. Executives don’t excel at interpersonal politics or hand-holding, and

feel unhappily out of their depth when friends need understanding. Instead of

showing warm, consoling reassurance, these types can point out problems in

unbalanced ways and come up with dispassionate solutions, leaving other Sentinels

feeling emotionally abandoned.

In turn, being in the middle of such situations can be baffling for Executives. When

Sentinels experience sudden, serious problems, Executives may simply not have

the emotional understanding to figure things out quickly enough to keep a handle

on these situations. This can be extremely frustrating to a personality type that so

deeply values control and personal capability. Executives do everything they can to

avoid drama in their own lives, so they tend to resent it when friends bring such

things to their doorstep.

Mutual Immobility

Executives and Sentinel friends build enjoyable, predictable routines together, but

unwillingness to try new things can hold back their advancement on many fronts.

Sentinels don’t offer Executives much inspiration to make even beneficial changes

to their lives, such as moving or going back to school. These friends may even shut

out other worthy people who bring ideas that don’t easily fit their social order.

Instead of embracing the risks of ongoing development, these pals seek to reassure

each other that things are OK.

Mutual reassurance provides a sense of comfort and solidity, but does little to

motivate improvements. This isn’t to say that these friends settle for failure, but

they may settle for existing success. Even the most vigorous Executives rarely see

the value in spontaneously changing what’s proved to be a serviceable routine.

Consequently, these friends may not push each other to excel beyond the security

of conventional accomplishments. They do each other a disservice if they don’t

occasionally step out of such routines together to see what life can offer, and what

they’re truly capable of when they push their boundaries.

Rebalancing Executive–Sentinel Friendships

Rational Peacemakers

When Executives can’t fully empathize with friends’ emotional situations, they need

not feel ineffective. Instead of artlessly trying to discuss feelings they don’t know

how to navigate, they can admit their limits while still offering firm support.

Authenticity grants these types a strength that’s reassuring to upset friends, so it’s

OK for them to say, “I don’t fully understand how you feel, but I am always here for

you.” Sentinel friends are grateful for such caring that’s sincere.

Once emotions have calmed a bit, Executives’ detachment becomes an asset to

friends in challenging situations. These types can take on roles of impartial

observers, and try to help friends see practical moves that ease the situation. If

friends are at odds, Executives are very confident and sensible mediators. Their

preference for a clear understanding of the facts, and their relative comfort in

rendering impartial judgement, helps them to stay fair for all parties concerned.

Sensible Change

Sentinels excel at being responsible, so Executives and Sentinels alike can keep an

eye not just on each other’s comfort and security, but on what truly benefits each

other long-term. These friends wisely advise each other when they’re in a rut, and

offer ideas when change is needed to create success. When this requires embracing

something new or unconventional, they can provide each other a judicious helping

hand as well as supportive encouragement.

This applies to recreational pursuits as well as more serious matters, and these

friends do well to try things out together. There’s no need to leap off a cliff or take

significant risks, but sampling new types of fun with trustworthy Sentinel friends

leads to experiences that Executives wouldn’t find on their own. These types aren’t

reluctant to take on challenges, and can likewise boldly explore new adventures.

Engaging the unknown together, at least occasionally, is a smart way for these

friends to explore their potential while sharing some fun.

Executive–Explorer Friendships

Friendships with Explorers are more likely to form through work, class, or mutual

friends rather than direct social attraction, but they offer Executives some notable

opportunities. Explorers live for the moment and its pleasures rather than long-

term stability. At first, this seems reckless to Executives. However, their ability to

lose themselves in enthusiasm is a great counterpoint to Executives’ careful,

methodical planning. These friendships provide a fresh outlook that helps

Executives see a broader perspective on life.

Balanced Executive–Explorer Friendships

Zest for Life

Executives benefit from being led into fun and curiosity by their Explorer friends.

Though it’s not easy, Executives can practice relinquishing control and taking things

as they come, especially in recreational matters where little is at stake. Developing

adaptability without losing their cool is very useful for these types. They can’t

always predict what life throws at them. Friendship with Explorers is a wonderful

way to practice mastering the unexpected, the unpredictable, and the uninhibited

aspects of life.

Offering Guidance

Executives appreciate how Explorers’ passion for discovery leads to knowledge, but

also see how such carefree enthusiasm can result in unintended consequences.

Supportive, tactical advice is where they shine in such friendships. When they earn

their Explorer friends’ trust, they become a respected voice of caution. While not

expecting Explorers to share their sense of moderation, they offer a respectful,

steadying hand with just the right amount of reservation to prevent calamity.

Executives who come to trust Explorer friends can also accept their guiding hand to

pull them out of their stoic sense of duty into some well-deserved gratification.

They have a hard time realizing, admitting, and exploring their heartfelt inner

desires. Explorers provide not only inspiration in this area, but practical help when

Executives are ready to relax and have some fun.

Surprising Capability

At first, Executives assume that Explorers’ more spontaneous style, so different

from their rigid approach, isn’t very effective. However, they’re impressed when

Explorer friends pursue things they care about to great success, and overcome

challenges on the fly with adaptive tenacity. Explorers might change their minds

frequently, but they throw themselves wholeheartedly into mastering interests that

are important to them.

When those interests align with Executives’ own, they find Explorer friends to be

helpful, involved companions. Explorers add a little refreshing excitement to

Executives’ goals – and lives in general. For example, Adventurers’ quiet curiosity

introduces them to subtle experiences and knowledge they might miss on their

own. They also very much appreciate the practical, hands-on side of Explorer

friends. The intent way Virtuosos hone their skills is impressive even to such a

capable type as Executives.

Motivation

Executives benefit from the undaunted, open-minded spirit of Explorers as well.

The outward-focused vigor of Entrepreneur and Entertainer friends matches their

energy, but with uninhibited enthusiasm. What Explorers lack in steadiness, they

make up for with zeal, approaching experiences and challenges with a flexibility

from which Executives can learn a lot.

In turn, Executives’ organized effectiveness impresses Explorer friends, especially

when aimed at a common goal. Explorers grab for what they want, and as masters

of logical planning and energetic execution, Executives can help them ensure

success. These friends lend each other very different kinds of help to move

successfully through life.

Unbalanced Executive–Explorer Friendships

Dangerous Games

There’s a dark side to living in the moment, as a lack of consistency and forethought

often brings about wild disorganization, social friction, and financial instability.

Explorers’ uninhibited lifestyle and impetuous decisions challenge Executives’

boundaries of acceptance. Being exposed to too much chaos is deeply troubling –

even offensive – to them. It’s not in their nature to abandon friends, but they’re

quick to defend the stability of their own lives. They’ll ultimately distance

themselves if they feel threatened enough.

Explorers rarely understand or respect these doubts and reservations in

unbalanced friendships, and either try to push Executives into things or lose

patience and act without them. In unbalanced friendships, any idea of steadfast

companionship can vanish into thin air when Explorers feel compelled to go in

directions that Executives refuse to consider. This mutual disregard causes both

types to question their friendships. Explorers resent the lack of support when they

want to follow their hearts, and Executives dig in their heels, weary of ever-

changing, exuberant disorder.

Disappointment and Frustration

A seemingly minor area where the often-fickle interests of Explorer friends can be

very troubling for Executives is planning. Executives demand organization, and

Explorers don’t exactly operate that way. Friends being late for, altering, or

canceling social engagements can be deeply frustrating to Executives. These picky

types become overly-critical when their value of reliability isn’t reflected. In turn,

Explorers are dismayed by their intolerance.

More seriously, Executives’ difficulty with emotions and human connections is

worsened in unbalanced friendships when Explorers’ attention is easily pulled in

new directions. It’s hard for Executives to communicate their feelings, even to highly

reliable friends, as they dislike showing anything like vulnerability. Being let down

by inconsistent people, unfortunately, reinforces this tendency to hide aspects of

themselves.

Disappointment goes both ways in these unbalanced friendships, however.

Executives offer Explorers little in the way of empathetic support, excitement, or

the unknown, and their rigid methods of consideration often hinder adventure and

discovery. Executives may not turn out to be the engaged, fun friends that

Explorers seek, and Explorers don’t put up with disappointment for very long.

Rebalancing Executive–Explorer Friendships

Respecting Limits and Needs

To achieve balance within their friendships, Executives and Explorers need to

consciously recognize and accept their differences. These types frequently test each

other, but this provides many opportunities for growth. Complaining isn’t

communication, and both must recognize when it’s time to stop wasting energy on

silent – or vocal – judgement. Instead, they can start talking about their needs

gently, respectfully, and honestly with each other. Here are some ways these

friends can grow their bonds.

● When Executives fairly communicate their true limits, Explorer friends can

avoid pulling them too far out of their comfort zone. Conventional outings

together – perhaps dining at favorite restaurants, shopping trips, or movies –

are good ways for these friends to share some fun when Executives have had

enough adventure. Explorers are all about indulgence and Executives

appreciate familiarity, so they’re wise to share some simple pleasures

together.

● Likewise, Executives rarely back down from a challenge, and can stretch

themselves when Explorers need company doing something exciting. An

easy way for Explorers to encourage Executives to participate in such

activities is to involve them in the planning, and then make sure they stick to

the plan. Executives are a lot more willing to approach new things when the

approach itself is well-organized.

● In any case, the importance of the friendships themselves form the common

focus, with an understanding that sometimes compromises must be made

for the sake of a worthy friend. Neither type needs to lead in a direction they

find uncomfortable, but they can try to follow now and then. This kind of

consideration isn’t automatic or easy for either Executives or Explorers, but

it’s an area where both types greatly benefit from conscious improvement.

Detached Understanding

There may also be times when these friends are wise to just do their own thing. It

may not be obvious, but pulling back and letting others make their own decisions is

a sign of tremendous respect from Executives, and true friends deserve that

respect. When Explorers engage in something too extreme for Executives to

support, they can practice respectful, rational detachment. There’s no shame in

letting their friends be wild and free without them for a while. They can hear the

stories and laugh together afterward.

Explorers can appreciate this respect, even if it doesn’t always come with

participation or complete approval. In return, they can respect Executives’ limits,

and understand that not everyone has as much excited curiosity in the face of the

unknown. When Executives need order, Explorers can just wait until they’re ready

and make another friendly offer later – no pressure. For these friends, respectfully

giving each other room to be themselves is necessary, and they can come back

together later for something they both enjoy.

Conclusion Mutual support and shared fun are among the greatest benefits of friendship.

These might not seem like top priorities to a type as driven and grounded as

Executives, but for all their logic, they’re still social creatures. Connecting to other

people is an integral part of their journey toward status, success, and happiness.

These types know the value of networking, and the efficient strength found in

cooperation.

Once they come to truly know other people, their assumptions and beliefs are in

for some challenges, but Executives are up for that challenge. Applying their

judicious value of fairness is an excellent way for these types to be open to

beneficial friendships with people who think and act differently. They’re strong

enough not just to hold convictions, but to explore other ideas and lifestyles

through worthy, capable friends.

Progress aside, Executives who take the time to focus some energy on friendships

instead of just life goals also receive a kind of inner fulfillment that’s a prize unto

itself. Status and success are genuinely valuable things, but having true friends who

respect and appreciate the individuals behind all that hard work is priceless.

Executives who invest in their friendships ensure that they always have someone

not only to congratulate their triumphs, but also to share laughter, trust, and joy

with them.

# Parenthood

Responsible and Respectable

The Executives’ mantra of “hard work, tradition, respect” is best exemplified in their

relationships with their children. In many ways, they’re the classic 1950s era

parental figures – strict guardians of family traditions who have no trouble

enforcing the rules and standards they’ve established while also providing solid,

pragmatic wisdom. Often seen as model citizens, these types expect their children

to continue the example they’ve set by being polite and respectful.

Executives have a very responsibility-oriented view of life. They share a firm belief

in the idea that one does what one can to contribute to the family and the

community. This view is, of course, extended to their children. As soon as they’re

able, their sons and daughters are expected to contribute in their way, by cleaning

their plates, keeping their rooms tidy, and going to bed on time – by maintaining

the order of things.

It’s not that they have wild, high-flying hopes of perfection for their children.

Executives are down-to-earth people, and merely want to see their kids develop

into respected, responsible adults. However, they also want them to be decisive

and capable, which can only happen with the chance to make their own decisions –

and to face the consequences of those choices, good or bad, with their parents’

love and support.

While yielding control may be difficult, Executives can exercise patience and

restraint by allowing their children to fall off their bikes, fail tests they didn’t study

for, or have their hearts broken by unrequited young love. These youthful

afflictions provide opportunities for parents to teach caution, resilience, and

thoughtful problem-solving skills that can benefit their children for the rest of their

lives.

Loyal and Honest

The moment they become parents, these types become unequivocally devoted to

raising their kids to the best of their abilities. Their loyalty is unwavering, and

they’re fully committed to providing everything necessary for their children to be

successful in life, though this is very different from giving everything their children

want. Using their natural ability to organize and delegate tasks, Executives can

create a stable and secure environment where their sons and daughters can truly

thrive.

Because their parents are never the type to mince words, Executives’ children

always know exactly what’s expected of them. These expectations are likely to be

thoroughly outlined and reinforced consistently – and their parents provide

feedback on how well they’re meeting these expectations on a regular basis.

Though they may not always communicate it sensitively, this feedback is still candid

and sincere.

Perhaps the most significant and enduring aspect of Executives’ loyal, honest

nature is the fact they’re modeling these virtues to their children. They expect their

kids to value these traits as well, and emphasize this at every opportunity.

Consistently observing and experiencing the benefits of the loyalty and honesty

their parents exude instills their importance. Children of Executive parents are

likely to respect and reciprocate these often-neglected virtues.

Overly Controlling

With their desire to always do “what’s best” for their children, Executives can quickly

spiral into unbalanced, excessively controlling behavior. They’re the personality

type most likely to believe they should guide their kids’ education, and the type

least inclined to think they need to give children the freedom to develop. This

system of beliefs can create a repressive environment, which prevents children

from engaging with their naturally curious instincts. Without the opportunity to

freely explore their surroundings, pursue their interests, and make mistakes,

youngsters can never develop the ability to take the initiative or bounce back from

failures.

Unbalanced controlling behavior tends to present itself in negative ways, especially

during stressful situations. Executives who believe their children are being

particularly difficult may dole out punishments that are harsher than necessary.

Even small transgressions can seem like personal affronts to their authority,

causing offended parents to become disconcerted. Discipline then becomes their

primary tool for keeping their children in line and making sure they follow the rules.

When parental discipline is focused entirely on punishment, children lose out on

the opportunity to truly learn from their mistakes. Constant criticism of their

behavior, rather than encouragement to improve, often manifests in kids as a

sense of defeat. They may think, “Why bother, I’m not going to ever meet their

expectations.” When they consistently feel this level of frustration, they may either

become withdrawn or begin acting out – feeling that what they do doesn’t matter

because they can’t please their parents either way. Whether children should

response this way or not isn’t relevant in this situation. The fact is, they can, and it’s

up to Executive parents to guide their development sensibly and realistically under

the circumstances. Ignoring the facts doesn’t help anyone.

Lacking Warmth and Sensitivity

When children need the warmth and sensitivity of emotional support, all the loyalty,

devotion, and structure that Executive parents model are of little use. While these

types can be sensitive in their way toward those they care about, it’s hard for

younger children and adolescents to recognize this tough love for the love that it is.

These parents may need to rely on more sensitive or empathic partners to fill this

role and create balance between clear, rational purpose and a harder-to-define

sense of emotional well-being.

Other situations that may inhibit them from parenting as effectively as they would

like are those where empathy is needed. Children make mistakes, act irrationally,

and routinely test boundaries, making interacting with them incredibly frustrating

at times. Executives’ lack of sensitivity can cause them to interact with their sons

and daughters in less-than-understanding ways, creating a cycle of frustration and

anger for both parents and children. Kids with the Feeling trait may especially be

crushed by what they perceive to be a rejection of their emotions – or even

themselves – and can display this in fearfulness, becoming withdrawn, and acting

out.

Creating Learning Opportunities

It’s easy to become caught up in controlling behaviors. Children certainly need

structure and benefit significantly from organized households. However, excessive

control of their environment, combined with unrealistic expectations, can result in

youngsters who are unable to regulate their behavior. Rather than focusing purely

on controlling their children, Executives can concentrate on providing them with

opportunities to learn how to control themselves.

If there’s anything that these parents hope to accomplish, it’s to raise children who

are hard-working, capable, and responsible, just like they are. The best way to teach

these lessons is by modeling, which Executives excel at, and allowing their sons and

daughters to learn through trial and error. Mistakes can be made into opportunities

to create teachable moments rather than inflicting punishment.

Toddlers may undoubtedly spill their milk the first time they try to make cereal by

themselves. Parents who show them how to clean up their spill, and carefully

model how to pour milk without spilling, experience less frustration than those who

immediately put their little ones in time-out. Not only are they teaching them a

valuable skill, but they’re also preventing the inevitable meltdown that occurs when

toddlers feel they’re unjustly punished. This technique takes more time up front,

but saves a great deal of time in the future as their children become more

competent and confident in their abilities.

Developing Effective Emotional Expression

In addition to creating learning opportunities out of mistakes, Executives who

desire meaningful relationships with their children must work toward building

strong emotional bonds. Improving their emotional expression, or how they

communicate their feelings with others, benefits their relationships with their kids

as well as anyone else with whom they’re in regular contact. This aspect of personal

growth can be daunting, and these types may fight the process by continually

arguing against its merits. Parenthood, however, is a very motivating experience

and may provide just the right level of pressure to convince Executives that it’s

worthwhile.

To improve their emotional expression, they may need to look at whether their

communication with their children is overly critical or insensitive. As a dominant

personality type, they may inadvertently steamroll their children when trying to talk

through their expectations. Kids who either withdraw or act out aggressively when

frustrated may feel as though their parents are demanding too of much of them

without taking the time to listen to their own needs and desires. Honest, patient

listening skills and measured, considerate responses can garner more respect and

more positive reactions from children of every age.

Parenting for Each Stage of Development Each stage of children’s growth presents both unique challenges and exciting

milestones. Learning how to navigate these stages can seem daunting, but the

opportunity to build healthy attachments and create meaningful memories makes

it worth the struggle.

Renowned      psychologist   Erik   Erikson   proposed         a   theory   of   psychosocial

development that identified eight stages, five of which take place between birth and

18 years of age. It’s important to remember that these stages aren’t set in stone,

and the ages listed for each stage are approximate. Children develop at their own

pace and meet each milestone in their own time. The following paragraphs briefly

discuss what to expect at each stage of development, as well as how the strengths

and weaknesses of the Executive personality type can affect their parenting at each

stage.

Building Bonds (Birth–1 ½ Years)

According to Erikson, the stages of infancy and early childhood last from birth until

approximately three years of age. Children in these stages are very dependent on

their parents to meet their needs. This is also when the bonds of attachment are

created by meeting children’s needs, developing trusting relationships, and

promoting autonomy.

The priority for infants in their journey to attachment is having their basic needs

met. From being fed to receiving affection, they learn to either trust that their

Executive parents can provide for them, or they suffer from a sense of mistrust in

all their subsequent relationships. If these types are inconsistent in providing for

their children, or are especially violent or neglectful – an extreme situation, to be

sure – their children can have difficulty creating positive, trusting attachment.

Attentive, affectionate parents, especially those who engage in consistent physical

contact such as snuggling or transporting their children in baby carriers on their

bodies, can develop trusting, hopeful relationships with their infants and are likely

to have little difficulty building comfortable attachments.

Balanced Infant Parenting

Building Reliable Bonds

Executives excel at providing structure for their children and are certainly able to

meet their infants’ needs in this regard. Reliability is vital in building relationships

with infants, and these parents are likely to be very attentive to their babies by

dependably feeding, changing, and comforting them when necessary. Most

Executives enjoy the infant stage, where their children rely on them entirely for

meeting their needs, and won’t hesitate to provide for them consistently in patient

and, ideally, caring ways.

As parents, these types have an extreme sense of duty to meet their children’s

needs, which can be motivating during the extraordinarily draining infant stage.

Where some parents may struggle with maintaining energy during this phase,

leading to mood swings or other inconsistent behaviors, Executives not only survive

this stage but even enjoy it. They can create a foundation for building trust and

instilling hope in their infants.

Endless Energy

Sleepless nights are inevitable, and exhaustion is almost certainly guaranteed

during this stage. Executive parents have an advantage in this regard, as they’re

high energy and are the least likely personality type to tire easily. This power can

carry them through especially exhausting days.

In addition to having the momentum needed to power through their sleep

deprivation, their excess energy, if utilized effectively, can help to insulate them

from some of the issues caused by exhaustion, such as feeling anxious, sad, or

overwhelmed. While all new parents have these feelings to some extent (and if

they’re severe or continue for several days, consult a medical professional),

Executives may thrive on and revel in the opportunity to make order out of the

chaos.

Unbalanced Infant Parenting

Inadequate Affection

Although they’re extraordinarily attentive and reliable, parents with the Executive

personality type may need to focus on regularly providing intentional affection.

During the infant stage, babies who don’t receive adequate physical contact with

caregivers can struggle with bonding and trust. In severe cases, infants who go

without loving physical contact can experience a failure to thrive, resulting in

emotional, intellectual, and behavioral issues that could continue to plague them in

future relationships.

Most Executive parents are unlikely to completely deprive their children of physical

contact, but they may see interactions with their babies as necessary exchanges

rather than opportunities to promote bonding. They may be tempted to let their

children “cry it out” when it’s time for them to sleep, even though they may respond

more positively to rocking or a soothing lullaby. Adapting to their infants’ unique

needs during this time can be especially frustrating for this personality type who

prefers to do things their way.

Struggling with Expectations

In addition to struggling with expressing affection, Executives may have unrealistic

expectations of their children during this stage. While they certainly understand

their infants’ capabilities at this point, they may expect them to achieve certain

milestones more quickly than is necessary, or fret that their children aren’t hitting

milestones “on time.” Specifically, they may be more concerned about getting their

children to sit up, roll over, feed themselves, or walk before they’re truly ready,

rather than appreciating their current abilities and engaging with them at their

level.

It’s certainly exciting to see babies roll over for the first time or sit up on their own.

However, trying to push children to achieve these milestones too soon only causes

frustration for everyone involved. Just because their infants aren’t saying their first

words at the same time as their friend’s children doesn’t mean they aren’t as

intelligent. Development, especially at this stage, isn’t a measure of parental ability,

and both Executive parents and their infants are likely to be much happier and

more relaxed if they’re allowed to grow and develop at their own pace.

Rebalancing Infant Parenting

Determining Developmental Needs

At this stage, it’s important to understand that developmental milestones are

approximate and aren’t set in stone. Additionally, little ones who attain certain

milestones earlier aren’t necessarily “advanced,” and those who achieve them later

aren’t always “delayed.” Labeling children as advanced or delayed, especially this

early, can be detrimental to their lifelong development and self-esteem.

Pediatricians and family physicians are parents’ best resource for determining

whether they should be concerned about their children’s development. These

professionals can provide resources that cover what to expect at different stages

regarding physical, intellectual, psychological, and behavioral development. Regular

appointments with physicians ensure that any issues are addressed as soon as

possible, and can help to put Executive parents’ mind at ease.

Interacting with Affection

Executives are extraordinarily motivated by a sense of duty to their families and can

excel at parenting through the infancy stage if they take steps to overcome

potential weaknesses. They’re generally hesitant to express their emotions, but

innocent children, especially those who reciprocate their feelings through smiles,

giggles, and other positive reactions, can bring out Executive parents’ sensitive, silly,

loving sides.

Reciprocating their laughter, facial expression, and even hand motions encourages

bonding and builds the neural pathways necessary for children to learn. Cuddling,

keeping their infants in wearable children-carriers, talking in a soothing voice,

singing, and encouraging eye contact are additional physical ways that Executives

can foster meaningful, deep attachment with their babies.

Testing Boundaries (1 ½–3 Years)

Once they’re mobile, young children become very interested in exploring their

environment with their senses. Crawling, walking, grabbing items, putting things in

their mouths, and verbal communication are just a few of the ways that toddlers

learn about the world around them.

During this stage, parents must provide a safe harbor for their children while still

allowing them to experience and explore their surroundings. Children permitted to

test boundaries yet are still receiving parental support and encouragement can

develop a sense of autonomy. Those who are overly restricted, criticized, or made

to feel guilty for their desire to test boundaries can ultimately experience a sense of

shame and may suffer from feelings of incompetence or low self-worth. These

feelings can last throughout their lives, and may eventually jeopardize their ability

to achieve goals and develop positive character traits such as reliability or

confidence.

Balanced Toddler Parenting

Patient and Supportive

Executives’ patience and stamina are especially useful during the difficult toddler

years. While this stage can certainly be challenging, these parents enjoy promoting

their children’s curiosity and independence while still maintaining a safe

environment. They take pleasure in helping their children learn how to pick out

their clothes and dress themselves, and how to use and manipulate toys and tools

(such as forks and spoons). Teaching their children to become self-reliant and

competent are among these parents’ greatest joys.

Incredibly supportive, Executives make an ideal “home base” for their toddlers

during at this point as well. Their consistency and reliability can ensure that their

little ones never feel abandoned, ignored, or unloved. Even though children at this

age are incredibly independent, they still crave (and need) trustworthy caregivers

who can meet all their needs, and Executives won’t disappoint.

Providing Safe Boundaries

Known for running a tight ship, Executives excel at setting up reasonable

boundaries that allow toddlers to feel as though they’re unrestricted, but still feel

controlled by them as parents. The perception they’re allowed to play or explore

freely is especially crucial for toddlers working to build a sense of autonomy.

Something as simple as setting up spaces where their toddlers can color, glue, and

paint without worrying about the mess, or allowing them to pick out their own

books at the library, can feel incredibly liberating.

Providing a safe, structured environment gives Executives the sense of control they

need to remain confident in their abilities. When constructed correctly, this

structure can expand and contract based on their children’s needs. As their sons

and daughters grow and mature, these parents can gradually increase the

boundaries they’ve set to provide for more opportunities and independence. On

the other hand, parents can choose to tighten them for children who struggle with

limits that provide a level of ambiguity they aren’t prepared to deal with. The

toddler years may be the first time Executive parents need to fully utilize their

natural ability to determine and successfully create the boundaries their children

need.

Unbalanced Toddler Parenting

Authority Issues

On the flip side, this stage can be especially challenging for Executive parents, as

they can have difficulty accepting their children’s desire to push boundaries. From

insisting on dressing themselves (and choosing their uncoordinated clothing) to

refusing to eat any form of fresh produce (or any healthy food for that matter),

toddlers often seem as though they’re challenging their parents’ authority at every

turn. There’s a delicate balance to be achieved between providing a safe space to

explore and not overly controlling their children’s environment. Toddlers who wish

to do things independently can become increasingly frustrated by parents who

continually deny them these opportunities.

Parents with the Executive personality type can be offended by kids who appear to

challenge their authority or don’t display expected behavior, especially in public.

Hierarchies are to be respected, especially between parents and children, and when

their authority is questioned or ignored, these parents are likely to become

frustrated. It doesn’t help that their toddlers are likely to acquire a new favorite

word they can use forcefully and frequently, “No.” Executives don’t approve of being

challenged and may find this stage of their children’s development to be one of the

most difficult to reconcile.

Rebalancing Toddler Parenting

Promoting Responsible Independence

Children at this stage seek both security and increasing autonomy, and Executives

need to tow the fine line between patiently supporting this desire to explore and

upholding essential rules to ensure their safety. Here are some tips these parents

can try.

● Finding ways to involve them in household chores, or giving them options

such as, “Would you like to wear your red shirt or blue shirt today?” can

enhance not only their skills but their sense of autonomy and achievement

as well, making them more confident and secure in their abilities.

● It’s easy to become frustrated when toddlers appear to be continually

questioning authority, but it’s important to understand the necessity of

testing boundaries at this stage. Children need to learn how to act

independently and become self-reliant, and they do this by testing their

parents’ authority.

● It helps to look at discipline, especially during this stage, as a learning

opportunity for kids rather than as a form of punishment.

● For example, toddlers trying to get their parents’ attention may attempt to do

so through hitting or yelling. Rather than shouting back or putting them in

time-out, Executive parents can first practice engaging with the children

through eye contact, crouching to meet them at eye level, hand holding, or

hugging.

After toddlers feel connected, their parents can use these opportunities to help

their children develop better ways of looking for attention. This can take practice

and consistency, and Executives excel at both.

Thirst for Knowledge (3–5 Years)

The preschool years, from approximately three to five years of age, are a prime

time for brain development, and little ones accumulate knowledge at an

extraordinary rate through constant activity and a seemingly infinite range of

questions. Learning at this stage takes place through play, especially the

unstructured kind, and lots of social interaction with family and peers. Creativity is

also exercised extensively through imaginary play, make-believe games, and

stories.

To master this stage, children must be given the opportunity to not only make

some of their own decisions, but also be allowed to initiate activities, ask questions

(and receive answers), and lead others in exploration play. Youngsters who aren’t

given these opportunities – or are made to feel as though their constant questions

or childish make-believe games are annoying –can experience feelings of guilt,

frustration, and a lack of competence. Consequently, they may suffer from poor

self-control or a lack of initiative.

Balanced Preschool Parenting

Natural Teachers

Executives are both patient and self-confident, which are valuable traits when

parenting curious, active young children. They happily answer their preschool

children’s seemingly ceaseless stream of questions and take great pleasure in

modeling practical skills, such as sweeping or washing dishes. Imparting their

knowledge in practical, useful ways is one of Executive parents’ greatest strengths.

Practical skills and academics aren’t the only topics these parents value – instilling a

sense of character into their children from a young age is just as important to

Executives as any set of skills. They teach their children to respect authority, from

teachers to elderly neighbors. They insist upon honesty, and are the type of parents

who order their preschoolers to not only apologize to a neighbor whose flowers

they dug up but also help them to replant those flowers. These parents feel it’s

their moral obligation to instill these important principles through modeling and

consistent reinforcement.

Promoting Confidence

Additionally, these types are incredibly confident in their abilities, and rightly so, as

they’re especially dedicated, motivated, and competent. These individuals can

achieve whatever they set out to do. This is an incredibly helpful mindset to model

and pass on to children, especially during the preschool and school years – the

stage for increasing both confidence and competence.

As natural leaders themselves, Executives have well-developed decision-making

skills and an incredible amount of initiative – qualities that are vital for preschool-

aged children to learn. As an often-assertive personality type, Executives parents

can actively encourage their children to step up and develop leadership skills. At

this stage, they can expect their kids to introduce themselves to other children at

the playground, and use their problem-solving skills to deal with any difficult-to-

play-with children they encounter. They’re unlikely to pander to their preschoolers’

desire for them to solve any issues they may face. Instead, Executives can work with

them to figure out age-appropriate solutions to their problems.

Unbalanced Preschool Parenting

Lacking Adaptability

Although they love promoting learning, these types may have difficulty following

their children’s lead during the preschool years. Parents may struggle with the

spontaneous needs kids often present at especially inopportune times. As very

schedule-driven, timely individuals, they can be annoyed by preschoolers who are

more interested in their coloring pages than getting their shoes on when it’s time to

leave. Temper tantrums and bathroom emergencies can derail the best-laid plans,

and Executives who focus on maintaining tight schedules can frustrate both

themselves and their sons and daughters.

This lack of adaptability can also manifest as an inability to interact with their

children in silly, playful ways. Parenting is serious business for Executives, who may

struggle with their children’s desire to engage in make-believe play or sing goofy

songs, especially if they ask their parents to join in. Spontaneous play isn’t

Executives’ strong suit or something they necessarily wish to cultivate in their

children, and they may find it downright annoying.

Occasionally Overbearing

Another area Executive parents can have difficulty with is genuinely embracing

unstructured play. These types may fall prey to the idea that free play isn’t as

valuable as “educational” play or structured activities, and may focus their energy

on providing learning experiences rather than allowing their children to take the

lead. If they take their love of routine and structure too far, Executives may leave

too little room for their children to make the discoveries – and even important

mistakes – they need to develop competence and initiative.

This tendency to be overbearing can be especially damaging to children who are

Introverted or Feeling types who struggle with social interaction or feel

overwhelmed by displays of dominance. Extraverted Executive types may mistake

Introverted children’s lack of desire to interact with peers at the park as a serious

lack of social skills instead of an utterly legitimate personality trait. Preschoolers

who cry easily when their parents raise their voices may be seen as “cry-babies” in

need of “toughening up.” These types may view sensitivity as a weakness to be

corrected, rather than a trait that requires a different style of communication from

their parents.

Rebalancing Preschool Parenting

Increasing Flexibility

Learning to gauge their children’s developmental readiness can be difficult for

Executives who have become too focused on timely achievement. Rather than

constantly worrying about their children’s development, these parents can focus on

allowing them to develop them organically. Children don’t need an excessive

number of play dates, “educational” toys and games, or structured learning periods

throughout the day. Playing with simple toys allows them to engage their

imagination, the fundamental building block for developing their ability to gain

knowledge as they grow.

Being flexible doesn’t have to mean all play and no work. One way these parents

can promote both learning and autonomy without being overly controlling is to

allow their children to define their “lesson” for the day.

● If their kids want to go to the zoo, make a point to ask them questions about

the animals they see.

● Seek out answers to any questions they may have about the creatures

they’re observing.

● If they’re playing outside, help them to identify bugs, birds, trees, and flowers

in a playful way. Just giving a name to the bird they’re observing – “That black

and white bird is a magpie!” – is learning.

Children allowed to seek out learning experiences on their own at this stage are

much more likely to do so throughout their lives.

Embracing Play Time

To add even more benefit for both themselves and their children, Executives can

take time to engage in play. A game of hide-and-seek, sitting on the floor putting

together a puzzle, or playing with toys are all great ways to both model the benefits

of play and to enhance the parent-child bond. Additionally, unstructured play time

is vitally important for children at this stage to gain competence, confidence, and

initiative.

Executives must learn to let their kids have plenty of free play time, ideally with lots

of time outdoors in nature, without a specific agenda. Even boredom is valuable, as

this is when children feel the need to try new things and learn new skills.

Preschoolers with this type of freedom can gain confidence in their abilities and be

more inclined to intentionally seek out educational opportunities later in life.

Creating Competence (5–12 Years)

The early school years are an important period of skill development for young

children. Not only are they still continually absorbing new information, but they’re

also working on becoming proficient in increasingly complex areas, from

relationships to mathematics to athletics. Children who are both challenged and

supported in these areas can flourish in both their abilities as well as their self-

confidence. If they don’t receive consistent support at this stage, they can begin to

feel inferior to their peers and are less likely to develop or work toward achieving

long-term goals. On the other hand, if they aren’t sufficiently challenged, they may

begin to feel overly-confident in themselves and their abilities, and can lack

modesty and compassion toward others.

Balanced School-Age Parenting

Supportive and Pragmatic

As they strive to be capable students themselves, Executives can promote

obedience, attentiveness, and respect for authority in their children as well. The

school years, whether spent in “traditional” educational environments or not, focus

on the development of academic skills and obtaining proficiency. Executive parents

tend to provide the necessary structure to help their children succeed, whether it’s

helping with homework, finding a tutor, or even just providing consistent support

for frustrated kids.

While very supportive and encouraging of their children, Executives balance out

these traits with their focus on duty and pragmatism. They’re no strangers to hard

work themselves, and can encourage children of all levels and abilities to achieve

their personal best. These parents are proud of their sons’ and daughters’

accomplishments. However, they loathe arrogance, and won’t hesitate to call out

kids engaging in conceited or pretentious behavior. These pragmatic types expect

achievement, and advise their children to focus on the task at hand instead of

seeking out acknowledgment for every little accomplishment.

Excellent Mentors

The school years are a time of intense learning, and Executives are natural teachers

who have no trouble modeling the skills needed for academic success as well as

real-world skills. Ever the pragmatists, Executive parents won’t shy away from

teaching their children the value of hard work and the importance of traits such as

dependability and persistence.

Practical skills are just as necessary as academics, and these parents are likely to

take every opportunity to pass on these skills. They can teach even young children

age-appropriate ways to help with cooking, cleaning, and home maintenance. In

fact, these teaching moments may be one of the primary ways that Executive

parents spend time bonding with their children. Working on the car together,

showing them how to make cookies, and even teaching them how to do laundry are

all fantastic opportunities to model skills and build their relationships with their

children. Relationships are built on quality time spent together, and helping their

youngsters learn how to succeed in life is unequivocally high-quality time.

Unbalanced School-Age Parenting

Overly Concerned with Achievement

Executives can become overly concerned about making sure that their children are

genuinely "good kids,” meaning they’re successful in school, socially connected,

engaged in hobbies, and well-behaved. This mindset can prove especially

detrimental to children at this stage by indicating that academic achievement and

good behavior are more important than holistic growth or their personal interests.

From Scouting programs to swimming lessons, parents may place a great deal of

emphasis on extracurricular activities they believe can help their children be

“successful” without realizing they’re overburdening them.

Executive parents who elevate their expectations to this extent may experience

emotional outbursts from their children when they become fed up by an excessive

focus on how they appear to others rather than how they feel. Conversely, less

confident kids may struggle to voice their frustration and, instead of rebelling, can

become overly dependent on their parents’ approval. This may result in difficulty

setting their own goals and can lead to a struggle to achieve a healthy level of

independence later in life.

Critical Without Appreciation

Another area where Executive parents struggle is their emphasis on success over

learning “how to learn.” True learning is often a product of failure, which is a hard

concept for many parents to grasp. Kids who struggle in math class that, through

hard work and time with a tutor, manage to raise their grades from an ‘F’ to a ‘C,’

are learning. Parents who overly focus on success in the form of all ‘A’s on their

report card, may not view their children’s ‘C’s as proof of learning, regardless of the

great strides they’ve made.

All children are different, and their unique talents, skills, and abilities should be

taken into consideration before others criticize their shortcomings. Executive

parents can be quick to point out faults in their children’s performance or behavior.

They believe that addressing these issues is more important than appreciating or

acknowledging their children’s strengths and achievements. They may think,

“They’re already getting good grades in English, so why should I worry about

praising them for getting an ‘A’ on their paper? I expect that.” This can have a

devastating effect on children who feel their parents only see their faults. They may

become withdrawn or start acting out – believing nothing they can do is good

enough to please their overly critical parents, anyway.

Rebalancing School-Age Parenting

Constructing Values Together

As much as Executive parents may want their children to succeed in specific areas,

it’s more important they focus on promoting their overall well-being. They’re very

devoted to their principles, especially responsibility and duty, as well as their

children. Using their devotion, these types can create a definition of success that

embraces a more holistic idea of what success truly means. Determining and

promoting their personal principles can help them guide their kids toward

developing their principles and pursuing their definitions of a successful life. Here

are some steps Executive parents can take to do this.

● Executives can start this process by looking at institutions that have played

an important part in their lives, such as their faith, their family, or even their

alma mater.

● Once they’ve determined the sources of some of their principles, they can

extrapolate the values they learned or developed because of their

experiences with those institutions. Their faith may have provided them with

a policy of honesty, their family may have instilled a deep sense of

responsibility, and an alma matter may have helped to develop their value of

hard work.

● These parents may identify dozens of principles that are important to them,

but for this process it’s best to focus on three to five principles they’d like to

emphasize for themselves and their children.

● Once selected, Executive parents should define how they can personally

practice and model these values. A sense of responsibility can be both

practiced and modeled by taking their children with them to volunteer at a

soup kitchen.

● They can practice hard work in their career, but an effective way to model it

to children who don’t see what they do at the office is to work on a project

with their kids. Projects could range from restoring an old car their children

may someday drive to learning a language together in preparation for a

family trip to a foreign country.

Discovering and acting on such principles can be a daunting process. There are so

many outside factors – such as teachers, peers, and even media sources – telling

people what they should prioritize. It may be difficult, but Executives who take the

time to engage in this process with their children won’t only promote their positive

values, they’ll likely grow closer to their children as well. Once they’ve gone through

this process, parents can rest assured they’ve done what they can to raise

competent, confident children with a firm sense of what they value.

Finding Thyself (12–18 Years)

During this stage of development, adolescents are transitioning from childhood to

adulthood and developing their distinct sense of identity. This centers around their

understanding of what their roles are in their relationships with themselves, others,

and society in general. To determine their roles, teens can begin the all-important

process of identifying their personal values and beliefs. Future education and

career goals can be made, and relationships can be developed based on how they

align with these values and beliefs, making this a pivotal stage for the rest of their

lives.

Balanced Adolescent Parenting

Promoting Positive Principles

During this stage of identity development, adolescents need the support and

understanding of their parents more than ever. Executive parents can encourage

their teenagers’ growth by promoting such positive principles as responsibility,

integrity, and reliability while providing a stable and loving environment. Although

they may struggle with gradually decreasing the amount of control they exert over

their children, Executives are still interested in assisting their sons and daughters in

the process of personal development to the best of their ability.

Regardless of the struggles they may face during this tumultuous time, these types

can remain patient and steadfast in their devotion to their children. Their reliability,

both in their attention and promotion of their kids’ positive principles, can provide a

solid framework that allows identity-seeking teens to venture further into self-

discovery while still feeling a sense of stability and love.

Honest and Dependable

As Holden Caulfield, the protagonist in J.D. Salinger’s classic novel, The Catcher in the

Rye, points out, there’s very little that adolescents hate more during this idealistic

stage than phonies. Executives also loathe dishonesty, and are at their best when

they can provide frank, forthright information. Their no-nonsense approach,

applied not as criticism but as a way of responding to questions and giving advice,

makes teens feel like they’re being treated more as adults, and with more respect.

If these parents can maintain this same frank, yet patient pragmatism when their

kids come to them with something dire – getting pregnant, getting suspended or

expelled, or even a rough breakup – they can establish themselves as a dependable

source of stability and wisdom when their children need it most. As chaotic and

terrifying as some of these situations may seem, Executive parents can tap into

their confidence and dependability and focus on aspects of these situations on

which they can follow through.

Unbalanced Adolescent Parenting

Overly Traditional

Parents with the Executive personality type may have difficulty accepting certain

aspects of their children’s newfound identities during this stage. Their strong

principles often coincide with a preference for “traditional” roles, and teenagers

who push the boundaries of these roles too dramatically could both shock and

frustrate Executives. Making sure their children’s values align with their personal

and family principles is of utmost importance to them.

They base their expectations of childhood on their past experiences, and societal

changes that take place with each new generation can be both confusing and

frustrating. Their adolescent children may appear almost unrecognizable as they

attempt to determine if the principles and traditions they were raised with truly

represent who they hope to become. With their principles challenged, Executives

may believe that their children have abandoned their values, making them feel as

though they have failed as parents.

Intolerant and Unforgiving

Executive parents can be both intolerant and unforgiving of the mistakes their

teens are bound to make. While all parents struggle with the challenges presented

during the adolescent years, Executives can effectively shut down their

relationships with their children with their lack of tolerance. Experimentation is

prevalent throughout this stage, with adolescents doing anything from trying

alcohol to engaging in sexual activities. While they must establish firm rules, these

parents must also be open to forgiving their teenagers’ indiscretions and focus on

helping them learn from mistakes.

Criticism and constant reminders of any missteps they may have made can

certainly breed resentment in their sons and daughters. Rather than forgiving their

transgressions and helping their kids deal with the resulting consequences of their

actions, Executives may focus on holding those mistakes over their children’s heads

as reminders of their failure. Often, they believe this is the best way to be sure that

their teens won’t repeat these mistakes. Unfortunately, unconstructive criticism

comes across as mean and unfair at a time when children most need

understanding and context to help them learn from their struggles.

Rebalancing Adolescent Parenting

Modeling Personal Growth and Understanding

Understanding is vital during this stage, as their children’s needs can change, and

their independence can grow with each year. Constant rebalancing of responsibility

and expectations are required as adolescent children strive to become more

capable, responsible, and mature. It can be difficult for Executives to decrease the

amount of control they exert over their kids – not necessarily because they view

them as incapable, but because they genuinely believe they know best. It’s helpful

for parents at this stage to encourage their children to develop their own personal

values – here are some ideas Executives can try.

● While they can draw from family values to do so, kids should be allowed to

privately focus on embracing values that are meaningful to their individual

journeys.

● Most importantly, Executives can take such opportunities to engage in some

self-reflection as well, perhaps even taking time to rediscover old hobbies,

find a club or class to join, or become more involved in a community group

or project.

● Modeling is the best way to pass principles and values to children, and

parents can benefit from focusing on their personal growth, showing what

productive, happy, and responsible adults do with their time.

Engaging in Active Listening

Finally, Executive parents should practice listening to their children’s needs,

thoughts, and concerns in open, non-judgmental ways. Adolescents who are

struggling to define themselves need understanding and acceptance in addition to

the boundaries their parents provide. Mistakes and failures promote growth, and

parents can use them as learning opportunities instead of points of criticism.

Executives can use active listening provide both the support that their children so

desperately need as well as the stability and structure they naturally exude.

● Actively listening to their teens and withholding comments and judgments –

especially while their children are speaking – is the first step toward

understanding.

● Withholding judgment can be difficult, especially when Executive parents feel

they “know what’s best.” However, taking time to see things from their

children’s perspective, and remembering how different that view is thanks to

their ages and unique personalities, is an incredibly important skill to

develop.

Teens who feel accepted for who they are can gain the confidence necessary to

face the many challenges their future can undoubtedly hold.

Conclusion Parenting is one of the most challenging experiences that anyone can face,

regardless of their personality type. Executives have unique strengths and

weaknesses they can leverage to build positive, proactive relationships with their

children. There’s tremendous potential for personal development that takes place

when becoming a parent, and Executives can gain a much deeper understanding of

themselves and others if they use this opportunity to do so.

The advice provided above is based on general growth stages for children and basic

strengths and weaknesses that many Executives share. All children are different,

and parents are the best judges of what their children need. The advice above

should be used in ways that work best for each family without adding additional

stress, frustration, or burden. For special situations, such as behavioral or medical

issues, it’s best to consult with professionals such as pediatricians or pediatric

psychologists to come up with plans that can work best for everyone involved.

# Academic Path

Executives learn best in environments that apply the following three principles:

structure, efficiency, and pragmatism. They thrive when these three principles are

combined in the classroom – but are likely to struggle if even one is absent. By

identifying their strengths and weaknesses, they can become very effective

learners.

How Executives Learn Structure

Students with this personality type prefer instructors who clearly define themselves

as the leaders of their classrooms. Executive students trust their teachers are there

because they have proper certification, training, and experience. In turn, they

dislike classes being handed over to the students, such as those heavy on group

discussion and opinion.

Executives’ ideal instructors set goals and clear objectives, but if they feel it’s

necessary, these types set their own. Even if the tasks assigned are routine and

repetitive, they don’t mind if there are clear reasons for them. They thrive on

timelines and benchmarks they can achieve through dedicated studying.

Efficiency

Unlike other personality types, studying is something Executives do very well. They

utilize traditional learning methods like rote memorization and flashcards and are

likely to dedicate time outside the classroom to mastering the topic at hand. Their

ability to sit down, study, focus, and then execute what they’ve learned is

unmatched by most other types.

This laser-like focus is attributable to this personality type’s no-nonsense attitude

about work. Executives strive for excellent results – while fun-loving and sociable,

they take their studies seriously. Perhaps the quickest way to irritate these students

is to goof off or slack off when there are important tasks to be completed. They

demand competence from themselves and aren’t afraid to demand it from others.

Because Executives are so focused on their work, they’re likely to leave emotions at

the door. This doesn’t mean they’re unaware of others’ emotions – they’re likely to

pick up on peers feeling sad or angry – but caring about them is more of a delayed

reaction. How they and others feel comes second to accomplishing goals, and

Executives are honest and don’t sugarcoat this. When criticized in turn, they don’t

view it as a personal attack. If anything, they’d be more uncomfortable with doing

something wrong and no one correcting them to “spare” their feelings.

Pragmatism

In academics, their overall goal is to one day occupy stable, prominent roles in

society. Often, this means a traditionally revered career – doctor, congressperson,

and editor-in-chief – or as leaders in broader fields such as mathematics,

communications,     and   the    physical    sciences.    They   also   understand   that

opportunities are more readily available to students who graduate from well-

established universities. This drive to one day be traditionally successful is the

source of their strict study habits and focus while they’re young.

Executives scan for how things “should” be, and are adept at spotting

inconsistencies and failings. They’re likely to find conflicting topics like art,

philosophy, and other liberal arts subjects to be less appealing – these types don’t

relish debate for its sake in the same way as Analysts. If they do find this material

interesting, they’re still reluctant to pursue it, given their pragmatic focus (the

exception is history, for its usefulness in understanding its modern-day context.)

Regardless of their opinions on these subjects, however, they tolerate them to

honor academic requirements. In any environment, Executives always work to

deliver reliable results. They strive to be diligent students, no matter how

uncomfortable they may feel.

Balanced Learning Habits

Fulfillment in the Community

Executive students learn so they can be contributing members of society. These

exacting types take pride in supporting the established order and do what’s

necessary to keep things stable. They build on the foundation society deems

successful, try to fit into their culture, and help mend things where they can. They

learn what is necessary to fill an existing gap or follow a path that society considers

not just acceptable, but prominent.

However, they understand that while it’s important to meet societal standards and

expectations, it’s equally important to fulfill their personal needs. This can manifest

in adults who are active community leaders, but also take time for an hour-long,

once-a-week cooking class to learn how to properly braise a turkey.

Responsible Work Ethic

Executives subscribe to a rigorous work ethic and are known to keep working

without breaks. They dislike the idea of not fulfilling their commitments and

obligations (even if they didn’t ask for them) and don’t dismiss deadlines lightly.

They complete all necessary tasks with diligence.

They also are likely to acknowledge the importance of good health to a balanced

life. These students do what they can to turn in excellent work, but avoid pulling all-

nighters and skipping meals. They evade such self-sacrificing methods by creating

schedules that commit the effort needed to succeed, but also to eat and relax.

These students also aren’t against letting loose occasionally. Executives take their

duties very seriously but are happy going out with friends, even the night before a

class, if the outing is reasonable. This is especially the case if the course is an

elective they already have a good grade in. As the saying goes, “Everything in

moderation, including moderation.” Even if they allow fun to infiltrate work at

times, they can rest easy knowing that the education system rewards students like

them.

Allowing Innovation

Proud to be reliable, competent students, Executives achieve this by working within

pre-existing structures and following traditional methods. This doesn’t mean that

they’re completely against the unconventional – just that they take it with a grain of

salt.

In the classroom, these types experiment (or allow experimentation to take place)

with new methods if they show promising results, plausibility, and the likelihood of

being better than their current ones. Despite their reluctance, they can find balance

with the understanding that innovation sometimes leads to better things.

Unbalanced Learning Habits

Rigid Learning Style

Executives adhere strongly to well-established concepts and vetted learning

methods. They may even go so far as to completely reject innovative methods,

mostly to avoid the frustration they’re likely to provide. Instead, they may rely on

well-practiced techniques, with all the efficiency of experience, rather than

something new and untested that shows promise.

This unbalanced approach can lead to a struggle with changing circumstances and

expectations in teaching styles or subject material. Physical science courses may be

a natural fit for these students: they establish base principles, then build on them

procedurally in organized ways. However, if teachers cancel lab days, switch to

“cooler” experiments at the last minute, or make other unexpected changes,

Executives may struggle to keep up.

If they take it too far in this unbalanced manner – and decisive minds certainly can –

these students may reject entire fields because of one bad experience with an

inconstant instructor.

Learning for Status and Expectations

There are two broad types of motivational focus – internal and external. Internal

motivational focus is based on what people want from life, while the external type

is based on what society tells them they should want. If students follow the latter to

the exclusion of the former, their personal needs remain unfulfilled, leaving them

educated but hollow. Executives who ignore classes they find interesting just

because they don’t fit their chosen majors can miss out on hobbies, friends, and

even some resume-builders that show a range of interests – a surprisingly essential

component of the interview process.

Expectations can emerge from internalizing a lifetime’s external demands of

meeting goals set for them by others, or from the real-time needs of family,

community, and even school officials. They may lose their sense of independent

decision-making, or even lose their characteristic decisiveness later in life. Adhering

to expectations may work for some Executives if the demands aren’t too exacting,

but rigid and excessive expectations can lead to disappointment and perhaps the

loss of a sense of individuality.

Both obligations and expectations give a sense of certainty, and there’s rarely any

doubt about how people should conduct their lives in their presence. But this

assurance can come at a high price for Executives: a life lived more narrowly than

needed, and experiences missed along the way.

Rebalancing Learning Habits

Learning for Personal Fulfillment

When weighing what they want against what society tells them they want,

Executives may prioritize the latter. This unbalanced approach can lead to them

feeling unfulfilled and, at times, resentful. To avoid this, these students can try

these steps toward focusing more on meeting their internal needs.

● They can start by taking courses that are fun but “unnecessary” to their

academic career. This should be doable, as these types aren’t afraid of

occasionally bold moves if they’re sensible as well. They’ll move cross-country

– for a lucrative career. They’ll buy a house – that’s well-priced in an

otherwise expensive neighborhood. They’ll get married quickly – if they meet

the person of their dreams). When changes make sense, they’ll take the leap.

● When it comes to academic boldness, granting themselves happiness is

sensible, too, as everything else hinges on it. They might learn about a

subject they thought was interesting in elementary school, yet also ties into

their general course requirements. Having an interest in the stars when

young can translate to fulfilling a science requirement with an astronomy

course, even if their major is accounting.

● By making small changes, Executives can gather the courage to take a

particularly big leap – like going to a university they enjoy but isn’t particularly

prestigious. They can even reason that society’s expectations demand self-

determination and flexibility – and what better way to prepare than by using

their interests to diversify academically?

Growing for Efficiency

Accepting innovation is what allows growth. From growth comes experience, which

ultimately leads to efficiency. However, Executives tend to subscribe to the idea of,

“If it’s not broken, don’t fix it.” They may be unwilling to try new methods to shake

the foundations of their academic career, whether they be alternative studying

habits, allowing another student to take charge of a group project, or even

changing their major.

To grow, to explore, and to continue learning, Executives need to become

comfortable with the idea that “new” isn’t synonymous with “inefficient.” They

shouldn’t be afraid to test out whether that new high-tech calculator is as good as

their old, faithful TI-83 rather than immediately writing it off. They could audit a

new course and decide afterward if consequent courses in the discipline are a good

fit for them.

It’s the fear of failure or frustration that stops Executives from exploring.

Remembering that failure speaks only for what’s explicitly tried, not for all things

new, reveals a way of thinking that more broadly applies the value of learning.

Rejecting learning experiences because they’re hard just isn’t a balanced approach

to knowledge. However, wise Executives also know that when they do fail, it can be

an indication that it’s time to move on.

Executives in High School Even as teenagers, Executives are typically ideal students. They have an unmatched

work ethic, are dedicated to turning in excellent assignments, and enjoy learning

within the constructs of an established institution. While other students are off

sulking or clowning around, these types flourish in the structured learning

environment that is high school.

Executives tend to be “leaders of the pack” in high school – being mother hens in

their social group, or taking control of team projects in class. As teens, they’re more

likely than their peers to know what they want from their academic career, and how

to achieve it. Their assertive nature can be a powerful tool in the high school arena.

However, they can be intolerant of those who don’t conform. Another area to work

on is their strict adherence to the belief, “Working time is meant for work.” Because

of this, they may not fit into the more fun-loving high school scene and may avoid

most teenage rebellion, an underappreciated phase of establishing independence.

In high school, Executives may benefit from their need to meet expectations.

However, this benefit could also be their most significant challenge.

Balanced High School Learning Habits

The Path of Least Resistance

Efficiency is one of Executives’ greatest strengths. They see a problem, gather

information, decipher the clearest path to implementing the solution, and then do

what they can to execute the plan. In high school, this could look like something

small – studying for 30 minutes each day after school for a test that’s two weeks

away to achieve optimal results. On a larger scale, it could be deciding to be class

valedictorian because it can make them more appealing to their dream university.

Executives, even in high school, actively avoid time-consuming obstacles, especially

long arguments. While they’re comfortable with confrontation, they’re more

comfortable with cooperation. They pick the path of being caring and patient with

their classmates, even if it can be just as easy for them to steamroll others. The

reason these students do this can be summarized in the adage, “You can catch

more flies with honey than vinegar.”

Arguing over moot points or dealing with a classmate’s hurt feelings, instead of

focusing on the project at hand, isn’t appealing to Executives. That’s why they

choose to walk the path of least resistance by considering the emotions and

viewpoints of others, even if it appears more difficult at the moment.

Peeking Outside the Box

These students excel within systems that enforce structure and rules. This is why

high school, in all its regimented glory, can be an excellent place for Executives.

However, it takes a balanced approach to understand that life outside the walls of

their school is far more complicated than third-period social studies ever let on.

They know that for all their strengths, there are also weaknesses that have the

potential to worsen after graduation.

To cover their bases, these masters of working “inside the box” can decide to peek

past its confines and consider the option that other methods can be more effective.

Executives aren’t closed off to the idea that their way could be wrong, but what they

do need is proof of it.

In high school, this manifests in these students taking a moment to consider the

perspective from opposing classmates, or testing out new subjects rather than just

assuming they won’t like them.

Levity in the Classroom

Executives have a reputation for being killjoys, but that’s not the case at all. They

can be as fun-loving, silly, and bubbly as anyone else. Things become an issue when

it comes to work and play – where others want to mix the two, Executives don’t.

Therefore, they can be easily irritated by classmates who goof off rather than focus

on the task at hand, while classmates may think they’re too buttoned up.

Executives with a balanced approach know that work and fun can coexist. While

they’re focused on getting the job done, they also understand that others don’t

have the same sort of tunnel vision when it comes to work. Rather than following

their inclination to say, “Forget it,” and plow through all the work themselves, they

instead figure out a way to communicate in a mutually beneficial way. This isn’t to

say that they let thing slide easily. They can allow a short amount of time for peers

to joke and laugh, before gently reminding everyone that there’s a task to complete.

Unbalanced High School Learning Habits

Doing Only What’s Expected

Executives are dutiful and committed, driven to meet the expectations placed upon

their shoulders. While this often works in their favor, it can also turn against them.

They may be so focused on meeting requirements they miss out on doing things

they enjoy.

Being good students involves meeting specific benchmarks, but it’s also about

finding joy in what is learned. Unfortunately, these types may find that they’ve lost

that joy on the path of meeting others’ expectations. Take, for example, those

consumed with studying for a standardized test. Of course, they’ll do well and earn

stellar scores that qualify them for college. But they’ll also wonder if any of the

information learned has any personal value. This may be more the fault of the

education system than the students, but Executives have an obligation to

themselves to learn beyond what society tells them is important.

Since they run the risk of not discovering things they enjoy, they may never let

themselves find their passion or specialize while they’re still in high school. This, in

turn, could lead to steeper challenges in higher education or the workplace. While

there’s nothing wrong with taking the well-tread path, Executives should take care

to understand themselves.

Rigid Responsibility

One challenge that Executives face in high school is that they possess a certain

rigidity. When it comes to work, these students leave their emotions at the door

and expect others to do the same. They may see explanations from their peers as

contemptuous excuses.

When they have an unbalanced, hard-nosed approach to school work, Executives

may have trouble with their fellow students. Since upholding standards is a large

part of their motivation, seeing other students disregard rules or even take breaks

may upset them. There can be mutual frustration – these types can see their peers

as lazy and unwilling to work hard, and peers can see Executives as

uncompromising, perhaps even as bullies.

Even when they don’t intend to push their peers around, that’s how it appears

when well-intentioned Executives try to force their work ethic and standards on

others in an unbalanced manner.

Fighting for Control

Executives like to manage things, but what happens when they encounter those

who don’t want to be led? Students with the Executive personality type may opt to

make others conform to the best of their ability – or cut them out entirely. This

unbalanced approach can play out in many ways: harsh criticism, strict exclusion, or

merely finding reasons to report out-of-sync students to their teachers.

These types can’t see why others wouldn’t want to go down the established path.

They may even see it as an insult to their competency, reliability, public image, and

sense of fair play. This unbalanced approach can lead to their taking offense that

they should work so hard and others so little, while everyone manages to pass in

the end. Executive students can be swift and harsh in venting their frustrations.

Rebalancing High School Learning Habits

Utilizing Patience

Because teenage Executives take their work seriously, they may feel frustrated with

the ruffians and slackers in class. They may think to themselves, “Why can’t you just

do the work?” or “Is it so hard for you to sit quietly in class and listen?”

If troublemakers genuinely disrupt Executives’ learning or the learning of others,

they’re more than capable of letting their irritation be known. Before acting,

though, they should consider the perspective of the disruptive students, or their

outside situations, and then decide if there are issues to address. The disruptors’

actions could be due to misunderstandings or miscommunication, or they might be

acting out or missing deadlines because of complicated home lives. Always keeping

patience in mind, Executives can try the following when dealing with problematic

classmates.

● They can channel their pragmatic fairness when distributing the work, and

holding others accountable with clear goals.

● They can also make group study a priority, which can help keep others on

task by creating a sense of group purpose.

● Opportunities for mentoring, and the chance to offer a plan with mini-goals

to keep everyone on track, can also help.

Mixing Work with Play

These students want to make sure there’s a distinction between work and play. For

Executives, the goal is often the only thing that matters during a project, and they

find those who aren’t willing to work toward the goal just as quickly or efficiently to

be extremely aggravating.

To balance this, Executives can factor some downtime into their studies and group

work. Suggesting 15-minute breaks for every 45 minutes of work is a good start.

Studies have found that most people have a hard time concentrating on their work

after 30–45 minutes and that continuing past that time can lower productivity and

work quality. This isn’t just a matter of work ethic, but a valid limitation of the

human brain.

Despite this fact, Executives are inclined to push past their limits, even taking on

others’ work. However, doing this isn’t fair to young Executives, or to the other

students who then miss out on opportunities to learn (or fail) for themselves.

Setting up a group plan creates natural accountability measures, and can help

Executive students maintain a lower-stress approach while still getting results.

Beyond Expectations

Young Executives operate well in areas where expectations are clear, and they work

diligently to perform for those they care about – parents, mentors, teachers,

friends. They place immense pressure on themselves to succeed for themselves

and others. However, there’s a vast world of unique expression beyond the

boundaries of these underlying cultural assumptions. Clinging tightly to

expectations can be both limiting and exhausting. The high school years can start

the journey of self-discovery that lasts a lifetime, and releasing the grip of

expectations can strengthen the early stages of this discovery process.

Consistently impressive performance according to standards is a good thing. But to

grow, students with this personality type also want to balance this by adding

activities they enjoy. The high school environment allows them to begin building a

sense of who they are, and to find personal passions. Below are some questions to

find out what those passions may be:

● As a child, I thought of becoming_____________.

● Most people don’t know this about me, but I enjoy __________.

● I am the go-to person when my friends need help with ____________.

Once there’s some sense of what fills high school Executives with a sense of

purpose and enthusiasm, they can begin to explore in practical ways. They might

talk to a guidance counselor about what activities correspond with their interests.

They can join clubs where there are enjoyable activities and like-minded individuals.

Finding others in the community who might be willing to mentor them – a guitar

teacher or ski instructor, for example – can give them access to a skill, but also

someone more experienced in general.

In high school, even steadfast Executives are likely to change their minds and their

professional aspirations. Interests aren’t permanent, particularly in adolescence.

These students can give themselves permission to be flexible as they decide

directions and passions. Having a sharper image of who they are as people, both

through self-awareness and experience, they can start to determine what meets

their goals: the workforce, or higher education.

Work or College? Go to college, or go into the workforce? This fork-in-the-road decision plagues all

young adults as they prepare to leave the swaddling comfort of high school. While

personality type isn’t the only factor that goes into making this choice, it can be an

important variable. The question many Executives have is whether it’s more

practical to enter a trade early to begin establishing their lives, or to bolster their

future security by obtaining a degree.

Core to their consideration is cultural expectations, especially regarding what they

perceive as societal success. They can find themselves hard pressed to defy a family

full of college graduates. If the choice is available, they’re more likely to pick

prestigious (but perhaps expensive) universities over more economical community

colleges or trade schools – unless those options are themselves part of family or

community pride.

Either way, it’s important for Executives to know that they have choices available to

them. So, what are some things they may consider when making this decision?

Balanced Decision-Making

Understanding the Path

By the time their high school graduation rolls around, many teenage Executives

decide on what careers they want to pursue. If positions that meet their career

goals are immediately available – say, taking over the family restaurant – then they

may jump in. Using the same example, they’re also just as likely to balance taking

over the family restaurant while taking business courses at the local university.

On the other hand, they’re likely to go to universities that provide suitable learning

environments for their ideal careers. If they decide to be, for example, a

congressperson, they’ll choose a school that’s produced other reputable

government officials.

Most importantly for Executives, a balanced decision is based on not only the

school’s societal standing but how they feel about it – not to mention the broader

career path that pushes them there. Of course, they want to attend well-respected

universities, but they also want to feel they can fit in enough to participate in their

campus communities.

Understanding why they want to do what they’re doing can also provide a more

definite sense of what the future holds. Executives regard risk with some caution,

but a balanced approach helps them realize when they’ve spent enough time

considering, and when it’s time to take bold action.

Unbalanced Decision-Making

Risk of the Unknown

The idea of leaving what they know for an unknown future can be terrifying, so

Executives ground themselves in the established – but an unbalanced approach can

take this too far. They may assess a situation, but assume anything new at all is

risky, and find it lacking any assurances.

Fear of the unknown can force Executives into early, entry-level work and keep

them there, whatever ambitions they may harbor. They don’t necessarily stay

because they want to or because they like their work. Instead, the choice of the

road less traveled may seem too stressful, and they opt for the comfort of the

familiar.

A risky path has no outcome that guarantees where they’ll end up, and they don’t

know if they’ll waste their resources pursuing something that means nothing in the

end. The risk Executives fear is that they won’t succeed – or that, in their success,

they won’t be able to rise to new responsibilities. Home and work may appear to be

safer harbors, and school as a mere hiding place from transitioning to the working

world. They may choose safety, and consequently miss professional and personal

opportunities.

Adhering to the Checklist

Risk and cost are both a part of growth. There’s a difference between choosing

prudently and choosing from limited options, and if a checklist becomes too rigid,

the first can become the second. In their diligence, Executives may too quickly

eliminate anything outside of overspecific criteria. Because of this, they may miss

opportunities that can otherwise benefit them.

It may be important to remember that college, especially when entered in young

adulthood, offers more than a diploma. It’s often students’ first exposure to

independence, and those who move away from home learn to take care of

themselves on a new level. Ideally, different institutions and programs encourage

college students to think for themselves to varying degrees.

Keeping this in mind, Executives on a college tour (which they’ll no doubt take)

should choose a university based on how well they fit into the environment, and

not just on the prestige of the institution. Ultimately, it’s better to have a positive

college experience that shapes them for the rest of their lives than just an Ivy

League name to fall back on.

Selecting for Others

Executives may choose to go to college because it’s traditionally the path family

members have taken, or what their community tells them is the best route. Even if

they have in mind a career that doesn’t need a college degree, they may not want to

go against the grain. They can foolishly spend time, money, and enthusiasm

because of this.

When these students defer this decision solely to others’ opinions, they set

themselves up for passive roles in their own lives. Executives who don’t include

their independent opinions in their work or college decisions may be disappointed

in themselves later and wish they’d taken a stronger stand. Denying themselves this

first significant opportunity for deciding their unique life directions sets up patterns

that can be difficult to break.

Rebalancing Decision-Making

The Value of Risk

There’s a certain degree of perfectionism among Executives, and they may have it

in their minds that failure just isn’t acceptable. Their fear of risking the unknown is

often part of protecting their reputation for success. Taking a reasoned risk is

different from choosing a reckless one – it means taking action that could be

rewarding enough to outweigh the chance of failure. Those who unshackle

themselves from demanding perfection take risks necessary for greater rewards.

Choosing their paths, despite the unknown, can lead to the reward of personal

satisfaction.

Executives can take the following steps to unlink from perfection and excessive risk

avoidance:

● Make the unknown known. While this is, of course, impossible to achieve one

hundred percent, Executives can get a little closer by talking to people who

have had different experiences with entering college and making choices

about work or college. If real-time conversations aren’t possible, there are

over three million responses to the YouTube query, “Why I decided to go to

college.” For a more concrete look, examining job listing requirements in a

desired line of work can provide a clear answer quickly.

● Voice concerns. Talk to a trusted someone who is likely to be objective. If still

in high school, guidance or college counselors may be helpful. Older family

members or family friends might be able to lend an ear and some of their

experience. Those who listen may be able to offer solutions, examples from

their personal decision making, or just provide an audience for venting.

● Attitude check. Examine personal attitudes about failure and what it means.

Few failures leave a permanent mark – more often, they teach and show that

people are moving forward and challenging themselves. By assuring

themselves success – eliminating the risk of failure – Executives hold

themselves back. This attitude is a choice, but by embracing future options, it

doesn’t have to be a permanent choice.

Allowing Checklists to Evolve

Executives’ college checklist can be fraught with limitations, whether self-imposed

or imposed by others. Expanding one’s options is a creative act that reaches

beyond assumptions and explores possibilities outside of the obvious. Executives

are likely to protect their decisions and beliefs, and that’s fine. But novel options

might also fit into the list and expand it. Or, there may be completely different

alternatives worth exploring. Either way, Executives working to rebalance must

make the decision that they owe it to themselves to explore all practical options,

rather than assuming no others exist.

Executives can start exploring some options beyond the obvious by identifying a

choice, and some beliefs about that choice. What makes this option viable? Why is it

a choice to begin with? This list should be as detailed and comprehensive as

possible.

With this analysis in place, they can ask themselves whether there are options

worth exploring or beliefs that are useful that didn’t make the cut. What would their

opposite type – many of whom carry on very successful lives by any measure –

come up with? It’s possible that nothing practical may arise from this exercise, but it

also carries minimal risk to try.

Finding Personal Value

One of Executives’ best traits is that they’re honest. If they honor this trait, it may

temper the influence that outside forces have on their decision-making. Their

internal motivations are likely to be more consistent over time, and being honest

with themselves means finding answers within and aligning the best ones with

society’s standards. These students are more likely to find their unique directions if

they consult themselves first, and it’s the least they deserve after all their hard

work.

When choosing a direction after high school, Executives can explore the following

questions with one- or two-sentence responses, remaining as concise as possible.

● When do you feel most engaged and enthusiastic?

● When did you overcome a difficult challenge successfully?

● When have you felt you were “in the zone?”

o What were you doing at the time?

o What were the principles you followed?

o What did you do to reach your goal?

o Did you feel personally satisfied by your accomplishment?

● Bearing in mind the challenges you face now (and possibly in the future),

how can you build on what you know works?

Answers to these questions are likely to demonstrate Executive students’ interests

and passions, providing a place to begin exploring the future. Those who commit to

something they believe in are likely to see it produce results more effectively. It’s a

relatively short step from discovering their interests to developing an action plan

that uses them well.

Executives in College College autonomy may disorient Executives at first, as it does most young adults.

Separated from their friends and family for probably the first time in their lives,

they no longer have the structure on which they’ve come to depend. This new

independence places them out of their element, but this is likely temporary as they

search out new benchmarks to pursue.

Balanced Approach to College

Conscientious and Responsible

Considering they spend their young lives looking for guideposts to direct them,

these types can find structure in university life in the form of clubs, study groups, or

campus positions. If they can’t find an organization that meets their needs, then

they can take it upon themselves to create one – a young accounting major who’s

perturbed by the lack of community in her field can tap into her Executive traits and

create an Accounting Society to bring accounting majors together.

Course choices in college are like the subjects these students chose in high school.

They excel in any detail-oriented work that needs widespread delegation and

follows established protocol, such as political science, business administration,

engineering, and law. For them, subjects aren’t nearly as important as their

application and purpose. These types like the most direct path to their goals.

Executive students aren’t generally attracted to less traditional courses, so they’re

unlikely to be found in a women’s studies course or a survey class of radical political

movements. Their interests tend to lie in more bedrock courses – perhaps

something their parents took a generation earlier.

These responsible students strive to set themselves up for success. Executives read,

and even study, their course syllabi (unlike their peers) like religious manuals, and

treat them as welcome frameworks to hang their college lives from. They avoid

classes at eight in the morning if they know it’s difficult for them to get up early.

They turn in quality work exactly when it’s due. In a sense, they trade in the rules of

high school for an updated set of rules for college. Unlike many other types, they

don’t flounder in their new environment, but instead find the well-worn path that’s

worked for those who have gone before them, and begin their journey by following

in their footsteps.

Gathering the Tools for a Good Life

Although some college students decide to party their four years away, this is far

removed from healthy Executives’ intentions. Their main reason for choosing

higher education is simple – to receive the certification or degree required to

advance them toward their chosen career and carry out their life plans.

College is an arena for personal experimentation for some, but these students see

it more as a hardware store from which they can acquire the tools needed to build

a stable, steady future. They utilize college resources to the best of their abilities –

campus services, career fairs, their professors, and, of course, information geared

toward making advantageous academic choices. For them, college is all about

forming strong foundations.

These foundations also include those of the social variety. Executives are inclined to

join social communities – Greek life, for example – because the prospect of making

strategic connections with their peers is very appealing. They don’t do it for

friendship, necessarily, but because it’s another tool to utilize for success.

Unbalanced Approach to College

Lost in the Halls of Education

In high school, grown-ups carefully directed students’ activities, but the autonomy

found in college presents a sudden radical shift from such micromanagement that

can overwhelm young adults. It’s common for first-year students to succumb to

feelings of depression and disorientation as they attempt to navigate the brave new

world of higher education. It’s reasonable to see why Executives may indeed feel

like lost souls when pushed into this more unstructured environment.

In college, all work is in some sense independent work, and guideposts and

supports are less available. For many of these students, it may be the first time no

one’s explicitly told them what to do. For those who are too dependent on external

structures, the early years of college can be painful and disconcerting. Executives

hate feeling ineffective, and a challenging start in college may be indistinguishable

from that feeling. If they aren’t careful, it might set the tone for the rest of their

stay.

Overload and Drudgery

In their desire to set solid foundations while in college, its natural for Executive

students to overload themselves. Whether it’s the pressures of the school’s

requirements or the expectations they pile on themselves, sooner or later, those

who haven’t learned the art of balancing self-care and responsible behavior

become overwhelmed and stressed.

In high school, their capability to stress themselves out may have been lessened by

smaller workloads and fewer expectations. In college, however, Executives have

free rein to express their perfectionism within their chosen courses and activities.

They can also add stress to their lives by pushing for too much detail and making

excessive demands of themselves. Such demands include not cutting themselves

slack when they make mistakes, overworking to achieve and maintain high grades,

and pushing themselves to meet high social expectation.

As the weight becomes heavier and heavier, Executives continue to smile dutifully,

even as they begin to falter under pressure. Despite their compliant smiles, this

stress can erode not only their effectiveness but also their morale. They’re likely to

push through and succeed anyway, but success drenched in stress may not make

the ordeal any less miserable for them. Instead of a rewarding experience, their

college years can soon represent drudgery and regret.

Rebalancing Their Approach to College

Finding their Way

Executive students tend to seek out the structure they need and find examples of

success to use as precedents to follow. Finding their way may come naturally, but a

lot depends on their maturity level. Younger students may need more help than

older students entering college. But the truth is, it may be difficult for these stoic

types to ask for the help they need at any age.

If Executives find themselves stressed out by higher education’s lack of structure,

they may want to make alliances with other students, preferably more experienced

scholars. Some colleges offer mentoring programs where older students take new

ones under their wings. Joining campus organizations can also be a place to see

how others navigate the autonomy.

Reshaping Unreasonable Expectations

Just as they may have had issues with taking breaks in high school, Executives in

college may be unwilling to deal with the root of their stress – meeting

unreasonable expectations, like maintaining a perfect grade point average while

taking a full course load of rigorous classes and working a part-time job. Any

objective assessment would call such a schedule an unrealistic burden.

The first step to dealing with massive expectations is to acknowledge they’ve

created an unreasonable situation for themselves. One simple way to check is to

ask, “Would I ask someone else to do all these things? Would I wish this schedule on

my best friend?” While they may project their expectations onto others at first, their

more rational side is likely to give an objective answer. (The correct answer is that

such schedules can prove to be soul-sapping burdens which shouldn’t be wished on

anybody.) For Executive students, making realistic assessments is an important step

in goal-setting.

These students suffer in silence because they believe they should be able to handle

everything expected of them. They view meeting expectations, no matter how

unreasonable, as a duty, so they grit their teeth and bear the pain. However, to be

better-rounded individuals, it’s necessary to understand that it’s okay not to be okay

sometimes. Pain, discomfort, and emotional stress are early warning systems that

alert people to change course or suffer further damage to their well-being.

Executives help themselves when they learn to treat these warnings with respect

and heed the information they provide.

Becoming literate in the language of one’s feelings can be as simple as raising

awareness. One method involves setting random alarms on phones or watches to

go off at unexpected intervals. When the alarms sound, make a note somewhere

with the answers to two question:

● “What is my current feeling?” Discouraged? Tired? Hopeless?

● “What is the feeling telling me?” The feeling of discouragement can

foreshadow an impossible afternoon filled with too many arduous tasks.

Many dutiful people learn to ignore their feelings. Bringing them back into the

conversation can help Executives deal better with unreasonable expectations and

consider their well-being more often.

Lifelong Learning For Executives, “learning” is very much about gaining applicable real-world skills to

create and maintain stable lives, including serving the people and organizations

they respect in improved ways. These types tend to continue to learn if doing so

supports the lives they hope to build.

Balanced Lifelong Learning

Reaching Outside the Comfort Zone

Mature Executives recognize that there’s knowledge outside of the scope of their

experience and education. To become better at what they do and who they are,

they understand they may need to increase their knowledge from time to time.

There may be occasional reluctance because learning means change, and that isn’t

always comfortable for them. However, with experience comes the understanding

that growth takes place outside of one’s comfort zone.

Having Time to Learn

As these types get older, obligations grow and time becomes a precious

commodity, making it easy to become completely swept up in maintaining what

they’ve built. Finding time to learn something – even if it offers a tremendous

benefit – can become challenging. Making time to become “better” may seem like a

luxury.

Executives who carve out time in their busy lives to pursue new information or skills

can make themselves more effective, and perhaps their lives more enjoyable as

well. While work and personal obligations are important, so is finding room to

incorporate new skills into their everyday routines. Novice cooks may need to take

some time out of their day to learn the skills to produce nutritious meals at home

from scratch, but the health benefits and enjoyment factor can make it all worth it.

Learning New Skills to Complement Old Lives

Executives don’t learn new things for the sake of novelty, but because they can

apply them to practical aspects of their lives. They take the time to examine life and

wonder how they can enrich it for both themselves and the people around them.

This preference for practical learning can manifest itself in pursuits like taking an

automobile maintenance course so they can handle minor car issues on their own.

Or, by picking up carpentry, they can create simple furniture pieces to pass on to

their children. If they learn how to grow a vegetable patch, they can use their

produce in healthy meals.

Enrichment learning may seem like “extra” to many busy Executives, but such

pursuits add texture and dimension to lives, and, when balanced, without

interfering with effectiveness. It’s not about novelty or an attempt to tamp down

boredom. It’s about taking a good life and making it even better.

Unbalanced Lifelong Learning

No Time for Learning

Those who decide they’re too busy to learn something new may be robbing

themselves of personal and professional growth. In this changing world, neglecting

professional development can mean the loss of a promotion or even a job. (Losing

a job would certainly solve the problem of being too busy.)

As far as personal growth, one of the issues that Executives often face is the vague

sense that they’re just cogs in a machine. Some lose their sense of individuality and,

consequently, personal value. Learning the right things can help them distinguish

themselves from others and give them a stronger sense of their uniqueness.

Settling and Stagnation

These types often choose contentment and reject change as a threat to it. When

deciding on a direction in life, they may decide against something new as a way of

leaving “well enough alone.” Unless there’s a very compelling reason to take a

course or to pursue a new direction in learning, they may forego it to protect what

they know. What others see as a type of stagnation, Executives see as a form of

maintenance. They may not see the value of disrupting their routine by learning to

grow a garden – there’s always the supermarket, just where it’s been for years.

Shaking up their predictable lives with new information can be difficult and perhaps

even threatening.

If their level of contentment is high enough, they might believe that there’s nothing

else of value for them to learn. They may question what they need to add to the

lives they already respect and value. Consequently, they can miss out on so much.

Rebalancing Lifelong Learning

Finding Time to Learn

Time may be the most significant obstacle in Executives’ academic development,

with other obligations distracting them from learning. However, it’s important for

these types to carve out time and put themselves first on occasion. Creating space

for self-care can help lower stress and improve their overall mood. Taking time to

grow in stimulating ways can be a healthy part of a stress management program.

They can begin by committing at least an hour a day to learn or practice a skill they

enjoy, incorporating the hour into their routine.

● Those who like to relax and watch television for a couple of hours at night

can also use that time to practice knitting or sewing. (It may take an

additional hour or two to learn the technique.)

● College level courses are now streamed through many devices and can be

entertaining – learning and relaxation available in one place.

● Crafty people teach artistic and artisan skills throughout YouTube. It’s a

miraculous age for pursuing lifelong education, and it’s rare that an hour or

so can’t be found to enlarge a life. There are many ways for the willing

individual to fit learning and growth into even the busiest day.

● There can also be a social aspect to learning, and the right classes might

involve meeting new people and making it well worth finding an hour a week

to learn. Single Executives may be especially interested in this aspect.

Combining one’s social life with learning (or vice versa) can deliver two

wonderful things in one package.

Varying the Routine

Being “set in your ways” isn’t necessarily a bad thing. However, there’s always room

for improving upon an existing routine. Executives can begin this by picking a

relatively mundane task – take, for example, a walk. Rather than taking the same

path every day, they can choose a different way every other week – a walk on a

forest trail, a walk through the city, a different route through the neighborhood.

Even a small variation can loosen up the tight grip an existing routine has on life

and begin to open new vistas of learning.

Using their small excursions, Executives can then adjust their routines based on

what they liked or disliked. They can decide in the end not to change their old

routine at all if that’s what suits them better. Or, they apply this exploratory spirit to

other parts of their lives. There’s always a new experience to have and new things

to learn. It doesn’t have to be earth-shaking or life-changing. Learning can be

beneficial without being too disruptive.

Conclusion Education can mean a lot of things to a lot of people. For Executives, it’s a way to

advance their goals in life and contribute to society, but it can be so much more if

given a chance.

The challenge isn’t so much in the hard work of learning. It’s more managing

expectations and finding a reasonable pace so that learning doesn’t become

soulless drudgery but a rewarding experience. For Executives, such balance

involves blending their sense of duty with expressions of their unique selves – a

balance that’s entirely achievable.

# Professional Development

Designating these types “Executives” wasn’t something done casually and, indeed, it

fits them well. In business, executives (small “e”) organize, delegate, and ensure that

groups meet goals successfully. Whether as the heads of major companies, small

department managers in tiny businesses, or as directors of entrepreneurial

enterprises, Executive types shine when they shape goals and lead others toward

their completion.

This doesn’t necessarily mean Executives must only do jobs with words like

“executive” or “manager” in the title, but they do find themselves most at home

steering endeavors toward success. Regardless of their actual job titles, they always

possess a drive to meet goals. Often, Executives start in the rank and file of

companies, and then work their way into leadership positions. To everyone else,

this transition can seem sudden and effortless, but Executives know that it was

likely something they strategized to achieve. However fast it happens, they earn it.

Mission Accomplished

If Executives have a catchphrase, it would be a triumphant, “Mission accomplished!”

Nothing is more gratifying for them than guiding their crews from concept to

reality. They analyze a goal, break it into the tasks needed, and organize their teams

to deliver. As Observant types, their targets are realistic and concrete, and they

know when they meet them because Executives design their goals with clear

metrics in mind.

However, these types won’t micromanage beyond a loose strategy. They focus

more on the outcome than the means. How their teams arrive at a goal isn’t as

important to them as reaching it. They cautiously choose trusted people to join

them, and because they expect much from their well-chosen teams, others can

expect much from Executives.

Focus Over Feelings

Those with the Executive personality type may come across as so focused on tasks

that they neglect the feelings or welfare of others. They can appear hard-nosed and

uncaring, but in many cases, nothing is further from the truth. The jobs they choose

and the styles they adopt aren’t always “warm and fuzzy,” but from their

perspective, professional growth and success is something they believe they share

with the people in their lives. Providing material benefits for themselves and the

members of their group means a lot to these types.

Under their sometimes stern exteriors dwells a healthy impulse to make their world

a better place for the people in it. Executives are natural team builders and work

best in this context. In their enthusiasm to meet goals they insist their teams do

their best, and sometimes this necessitates taking a strict stance. Their intent is

benevolent, and their goal is to coax others to do better.

Jobs and Skill Sets

Let’s look at some specific jobs that tend to fit Executives well. This list is by no

means comprehensive, as these types can succeed in any career they choose. By

the same token, even “suitable” jobs may not be ideal for all Executives. Individuals

must determine what careers are the most appropriate for them, including what

meets their needs and brings them both professional fulfillment and personal

happiness.

Suitable Workplace Roles

There’s no definitive list of jobs that fit any type exactly, and exceptions always

exist. However, this short list of industries shows where typical Executives might

feel at home. It provides an excellent place to start thinking about potential areas of

professional interest.

These jobs provide Executives with opportunities to exercise their strategic and

analytical skills. Each requires a strong work ethic, and many involve a focus on

projects and project management.

● Logistics

● Office Administration

● Asset Management

● Accountancy and Audit

● Law

● Military

● Data Analysis

● Medicine and Hospital/Clinic Administration

● Education (Particularly administrative positions)

● Banking and Finance

Less Suitable Workplace Roles

Just as there’s no limit to which careers Executive can enjoy, there’s no specific area

they cannot. However, certain jobs may present compatibility issues, and it’s not

always about what industries they choose as much as their roles in them. For

example, empathetic hand-holding outreach on the front lines of a substance

abuse treatment organization might strain this type’s skillset, but they can

absolutely shine in a clerical or organizer role in the same workplace. Likewise, their

patience might be stretched when dealing with customers in a restaurant, but as a

line cook or head chef, they may be a force to be reckoned with.

Executives have a mighty energy and often see things in very clear-cut terms, so

environments without clearly defined rules or hierarchies can be frustrating.

Anything that requires these types to constantly juggle irrational vagaries,

uncontrollable results, or hyperbolic emotion may be unpleasant. Executives thrive

in systems, even if they must craft these systems themselves, so an environment

that doesn’t support structured ways of doing things leaves them little place to

invest their energy. They’re usually happy to give abundant effort to an employer,

but if their enthusiasm is stifled in any way, it may rankle them – all they want is a

chance to succeed with vigor. Some work environments Executives may want to be

wary of committing to include:

● Jobs with no clear path of promotion or advancement.

● Jobs which reward employees based on arbitrary factors rather than

objectively measurable performance.

● Teams without a clear authority figure or arbiter.

● Workplaces with lax discipline (rules that are stated but not observed),

especially with weak management.

● Workplaces unwilling to implement improvements

● Jobs without tangible productivity goals – Executives prefer benchmarks for

their success.

● Workplaces with an excessively psychosocial or emotional atmosphere –

Executives may want to avoid a “high school” environment where personal

feelings and “he said, she said” gossip are rampant.

There is no job in which Executives cannot excel if they choose, but choosing wisely

according to their personal preferences and strengths can make the process a lot

easier – and much more enjoyable.

Workplace Cooperation Between Roles Understanding how Executives tend to behave is one thing – getting along with

myriad colleagues effectively, across a variety of personality types, is another

entirely. Now we’ll dig deeper to determine how Executives can best find happiness

and efficiency with their coworkers.

Executive–Analyst Cooperation

Executives and Analysts share a rational approach to life and find they have much

in common in the workplace. Executives are likely to use their analytical leanings to

create precise goals along paths that others have already established. Their Analyst

coworkers scan for ways to improve systems and methods, and are likely to see

their endeavors as challenging puzzles needing to be solved. While Executives may

not have the same interest in the broader vision, they eagerly latch onto Analysts’

visions if they end up where they want to go.

Balanced Executive–Analyst Cooperation

The Same, But Different

Stepping back and looking at these two types from a wider perspective, Analysts

are likely to create the mold into which Executives eventually fit. They like to shake

up systems and frameworks to make them better. However, much can be said for

stability, and Executives can play a role in taming change that’s too haphazard or

scattered. They slow things down enough to allow the people involved with the

system time to adjust to change. One offers a broader way of thinking. The other

domesticates it to fit the real, practical world.

Moderating Influences

As moderating influences, these two types can contribute to each other’s work in

significant ways. They’re both interested in meeting goals effectively, essentially

sharing the same vision, which adds value to their work relationships. Cooperation

between them is the product of accepting they both offer something of value that

leads to the same end – effective outcomes.

Where Analysts offer innovative solutions and visions, Executives are masters at

organizing their implementation and bringing them to reality. A sincere

collaboration between these two personality types is a crossroads where ideas

become achievements and old ways transform into more effective new ways.

Unbalanced Executive–Analyst Cooperation

Experimentation and Caution

Tensions are likely to mount if Analysts change things too casually. Executives

prefer proven solutions, and they need time to test new ideas before accepting

them. These types see anything too original as introducing chaos into their trusted

strategies. Analysts, however, are less likely to focus on the risks associated with

experimentation. They may even indulge in experimenting simply for its own sake,

providing no measurable contribution to the goals at hand. Consequently,

Executives are likely to reject them soundly.

Executives may label Analysts as loose cannons with wild ideas. In turn, Analysts

might think of Executives as uptight enemies of innovation. When they frame their

relationships as adversarial rather than collaborative, they can stop being useful to

each other, and the larger enterprise becomes weaker due to lack of cooperation.

If one type is subordinate to the other in Executive–Analyst work relationships,

either type maintaining a stubborn allegiance to a particular focus can be

problematic. Automatically taking a position without giving a full hearing to any

alternate views can be counterproductive. Very real and negative results may

happen when there’s a power difference, such as a lack of promotion, a lack of

basic respect, low morale, and even job loss.

Overabundance of Rationality

Despite their aforementioned differences, Executive–Analyst work partnerships still

pair two very similar types. Both rely on rationality. Effective solutions motivate

each type. Both are capable of neglecting the more human aspect of their

endeavors in favor of accomplishing determined goals.

Neither of these types is humble in their approach to life. Without a devil’s

advocate, these types can potentially barrel down the wrong path, tapping into

each other’s confidence and siphoning off energy and support. Executives stand

firmly in their convictions of the right way to do things, while Analysts often flaunt

their intelligence. Either form of pride may close off any input from third parties,

denying them some valuable information.

Rebalancing Executive–Analyst Cooperation

Breaking a Stalemate

If Executive and Analyst coworkers find themselves in conflict too often, they may

need to define for themselves what the other offers. In the heat of battle, it’s easy

to downplay the value of coworkers who aren’t in agreement. Reminding

themselves of each other’s value may restore balance. But more than a "touchy-

feely” approach, these types need to quantify real results or potential results for

each to take this assessment seriously. Doing so not only breaks the stalemate, but

can also help these coworkers revisit some strategic human resource possibilities

they might have forgotten.

Here is one way these types can approach chronically contentious relationships at

work:

● Take an inventory of each other type’s success (or measurable movement

toward success.) This inventory should be real profiles in productivity and not

merely a list of qualifications. Reciting qualifications may impress, but they

don’t necessarily speak to effectiveness and results.

● Once the inventory is complete, review how much of the material on the list

is still relevant to future work and goals.

o What have these Executives (or Analysts) done, or are doing well, and

how can this be applied to the job at hand?

o Where do these coworkers’ interests overlap?

o How can they strategically tap into each other’s skills or strengths to be

even more effective as a team?

In the busy workaday world, it can be easy to forget things that were once

appreciated. Sometimes it takes a moment of paying attention again to rebalance a

team.

Bringing In Fresh Perspectives

When Executives and Analysts become too insulated by their similar styles, they

need to discover methods to open their shared processes. Actively seeking out a

wider range of ideas can help craft a more robust strategy. In-house town hall

meetings that are open to all can expand points of view (if they’re in a position to

organize them), or simply eating lunch with a wide variety of people can accomplish

the same.

It’s easy to develop stereotypes of people with different approaches and dismiss

them based on those stereotypes. When this happens, the best-case scenario is

that it’s done unconsciously. However, sometimes individuals more actively reject

others based on differences. Executives and Analysts who develop an “echo

chamber” at work can examine any stereotypes of others and explore their biases.

Rounding out their ideas with the varied ideas of others, even if it makes them

uncomfortable at times, can contribute to their general effectiveness.

Executive–Diplomat Cooperation

The Pragmatic Doer Meets the Idealistic Dreamer

In classic advertising, there’s an old aphorism: “A good ad sells the sizzle, not the

steak.” This distinction is sometimes apparent when Executives and Diplomats work

together. Executives manage the steak, which deals with an essential concrete

product or service. Diplomats add the human element and strive to touch the

emotions or sensibilities of coworkers, consumers, or clients, thus providing the

sizzle. Executives ground Diplomats in the reality of concrete tasks and measurable

goals. Diplomats, on the other hand, help Executives experience the more “human”

features of an endeavor. When the pragmatic doer meets the idealistic dreamer,

interesting collaborations can occur.

Balanced Executive–Diplomat Cooperation

Complementary Perspectives

Another useful dynamic when Executives and Diplomats work together is also a

common feature when Intuitive types team with Observant types. Executives prefer

dealing with specific details and concrete realities, while Diplomats pull back and

engage the sometimes less tangible big picture. Understanding this difference, each

balances the other with equally valuable perspectives.

While Executives are busy with quotas and hard numbers, Diplomats can remind

them of things like employee morale and alignment with company missions

statements. Both can be vital perspectives needed for creating well-rounded

success. Learning to incorporate both into a single endeavor can promise more

enduring triumphs.

Unbalanced Executive–Diplomat Cooperation

Realistic versus Idealistic

To Executives, Diplomats’ approach of catering to the emotional needs and

sensibilities of those impacted may appear to be quirky and “soft.” Such lofty

idealism may seem outside the scope of their practical goals. Meanwhile, Diplomats

may see Executive pragmatism as hard-hearted and lacking in empathetic

connection to those in their orbit. Executives, in their opinions, are cold guardians

of a rigid system. Either may roll their eyes whenever the other expresses things

that give them their strength and employable value.

Clashes can occur when Executives pursue efficient and rational directions, but

their plans cross Diplomats’ sense of right and wrong. Because they focus on

outcomes more than processes, Executives can become baffled when intangibles

like fairness and job satisfaction influence Diplomats more than hard metrics.

Diplomats can obsess over processes if they believe they impinge on the rights and

welfare of others to any extent.

It all comes down to degrees of emotional intelligence. Diplomats are fluent in the

language of emotions and treat them as being greatly significant. As social

creatures, Executives are surely familiar with feelings, but aren’t as fluent as

Diplomats and they don’t allow them to have the same impact that Diplomats do.

Diplomats feel comfortable making decisions based on affective considerations,

while Executives are less inclined to do so.

Attention to Detail

Conflicts can arise between these types over either their respective attention, or

lack of attention, to detail. If some coworkers appear 10 minutes early for work, and

others are 10 minutes late, it’s easy to guess which are Executives and which are

Diplomats. It’s not that the Diplomats purposely thumb their noses at the system by

rebelliously arriving late. It’s just that the item “arrive on time for work” may not top

their unconscious priority list. However, there’s no doubt that punctuality is high on

Executives’ list of priorities.

These differences aren’t limited to arriving on time. There are many ways they show

up in a workday, such as protocols, for example. Diplomats are more likely than

Executives to ignore the chain of command. If they see an opportunity to advance

their humanistic agenda, they can take it right to the top rather than send their idea

through their more immediate supervisors – possibly knowing it might be tabled at

that level. Jobs are unique in the protocols and details they emphasize, and while

you can count on Executives to honor them, Diplomats can treat them more

loosely.

Executives rely, in part, on rules, protocols, and exacting behaviors as parameters

to use in defining their purpose and direction. As managers, they may be unwilling

to cut tardy Diplomats any slack. Diplomats may view Executives’ response as

“nitpicking,” condescending, and lacking substance. If these Diplomats are

subordinates, conflict can rise to a substantial level that includes reprimands and

perhaps even termination. If the two types are on a peer level, their respective

styles of handling details can be endlessly annoying and strain their working

relationships.

Rebalancing Executive–Diplomat Cooperation

Searching for Common Ground

People sometimes draw cartoonish, two-dimensional profiles in their minds to

represent others they don’t fully understand. Multidimensional Executives and

Diplomats are more alike than not. Many of their goals are likely similar. While their

differences may occasionally impact their ability to work together, they’re both

values-based individuals, even though their principles take somewhat different

forms of expression. In the end, they both come to work to earn a paycheck and

fulfill their ambitions.

Executives want to create a stable world where they can live responsibly, take care

of those they love and respect, and meet the material needs of all. They may not

always verbalize it – instead, they opt to demonstrate it. Diplomats, likewise, seek

an idealized version of this, where people realize their potential and have all their

needs met. For them, it’s physical, spiritual, and emotional, and they may wear their

hearts more prominently on their sleeves. Essentially, it’s the same desire

approached differently.

Toward True Cooperation

Reframing their approaches as different paths to the same results can help

Executives and Diplomats restore any lost cooperation. To accomplish this, each

must recognize there’s no superior path to the results they seek. Each potentially

contributes in valuable ways. (The amount of such valuable contribution isn’t

determined by type alone.)

Working together, the two types can practice active listening, so they comprehend

fully what each other is saying and has to offer. There’s no room for dogmatism.

Declaring, “This is the right way to do it,” or “This is the wrong way to do it,” without

some understanding that there can be options outside of this binary choice is likely

to kill collaboration with Diplomats. However, Diplomats offer their own binary

choices based on moral right or wrong. Active listening – with the goal of

understanding all nuances – helps dispel black-and-white thinking. Broader

understanding opens the door to genuine cooperation between these types.

Executive–Sentinel Cooperation

Working with other Sentinels appeals to Executives’ pragmatism, a characteristic

shared by all types in this Role. With Sentinel peers, they can avoid some of the

issues they face with less practical colleagues.

Balanced Executive–Sentinel Cooperation

Sharing a Sense of Mission

Executives and other Sentinels honor efficiency, details, and protocols. These

mutually supportive relationships reinforce and respect each other’s diligence. As

Executives ascend (as they typically do) to leadership roles, they can rely heavily on

other Sentinels to help them meet their goals. They can feel confident their Sentinel

cousins understand their objectives, and this like-mindedness can foster a sense of

cohesion.

Executives and Sentinels, sitting together in the lunchroom, tend to discuss down-

to-earth matters. Their conversations flow easily because they share fundamental

values. Work is serious business to both types, and among other things, they can

discuss how projects are progressing. They can talk about the strengths and

weaknesses of their coworkers. (They may base their judgments on coworkers’

effectiveness, but they’re just as likely to measure them against set company

standards.) These discussions revolve around “what is” rather than “what if”– topics

about concrete matters that lead to material results steer their conversations.

Sharing a Sense of Style

Whether they’re leaders or not, Executives find other Sentinels similarly attached to

successful outcomes. They share a belief that the path to superior results is clear

and direct. If they’re problem-solving, they might prefer using Occam’s razor as a

guide – the most straightforward solution with the fewest assumptions is to be

considered first.

For example, if the number of widgets produced in a day is too low, they may look

at the physical machinery and assembly process before they consider more

complex things like morale, production algorithms, and other less concrete

considerations. They may eventually need to consider intangibles like morale, but if

there’s a simpler answer, they’re likely to discover it quicker than types who are

more likely to spend time considering complex solutions first. In other words, their

shared pragmatism rules their evidence-gathering and decision-making, often to

their advantage.

Executives and Sentinels obviously think in similar ways, narrowing any potential

gap between stylistic approaches. Without Executives having to translate their style,

as they would with a type which employs a different process, their work with other

Sentinels is more streamlined.

Unbalanced Executive–Sentinel Cooperation

Inflexibility Breeds Boredom

Like-minded coworkers can be a pleasure. However, they can also help create an

insular environment. Pairing with a similar type can also result in a tedious

abundance of the same thing. For Executive–Sentinel collaborations, the tedium

plays out mainly through their established expectations and lack of flexibility.

Aegis of Standards

While embracing established methods promotes stability, being unreceptive to new

ideas bodes poorly in the modern business world. Few businesses have remained

as they were 20 years ago, and they’ll be unrecognizable 20 years from now. Too

many Sentinels in a room can potentially slow down innovation – and,

consequently, productivity – to a snail-like pace.

Executives and Sentinels may support each other in rejecting necessary

innovations. They can show an intolerance for others who introduce unorthodox

methods too quickly. Executives can approve of even the most novel concept if

they’re confident it brings positive results. However, too much combined Sentinel

reverence for tradition can render new ideas and methods a threat to their

established ways of operating, at least initially.

Leaders who depend on those too similar to themselves may discover such

advisors are a cluster of “yes men” who are less likely to offer constructive criticism

or useful pushback. The costly luxury of having a room full of like-minded

individuals can lead to a lack of needed change. Welcome to the dreaded echo

chamber.

The Problem with Occam’s Razor

While the simplest solution is often the best, Occam’s razor falls short when more

complex solutions are needed. Eliminating the simplest solutions first can be time-

consuming and ineffective, especially when answers are urgently needed. When

there’s a tyranny of style, with an Executive–Sentinel majority insisting on their

concrete methods, there may not be enough intellectual diversity.

Obviously, this isn’t about an ancient problem-solving principle that Sentinels might

prefer. It’s about the value of different approaches. As mentioned before, there are

various tools for different jobs. Too much dependence on a single personality style

leads to inefficiency and lower productivity. It goes even beyond the previously

discussed “yes men” to the problem of stagnation caused by shutting out other

necessary talents.

Rebalancing Executive–Sentinel Cooperation

Opening the Discussion

Support from Sentinels can empower Executives, but so can recognizing such

relationships have limits. Helpful alternatives may exist just a step outside of

Executive-Sentinel coworkers’ comfort zone. Here are some things both types can

keep in mind as they take that first step together.

● Rebalancing may be as simple as considering a current set of options may be

too narrow and making a deliberate decision to add diverse voices to the

conversation. In fact, sometimes the best collaboration occurs between

those with opposing ideas.

● Inviting dissenting voices to the table is difficult. People enjoy others

agreeing with them. However, if dissent is needed, an invitation isn’t usually

required – they’re probably already there. They may be sitting silently in the

background because of a rigid work culture. Sometimes, they’re simply

ignored.

Creating a more accepting environment or developing an individual attitude of

openness can bring these devil’s advocates out of the shadows. When Executive-

and Sentinel-dominated groups prioritize the search for right answers over the

comfort of being agreed with, valuable alternate voices begin to feel welcomed.

Experience New Methods

It may not be enough to open themselves to new ideas – it can take learning new

methods of looking at things or adopting new techniques and skills. Executives are

fairly facile at accepting the new, but Sentinels as a group like holding on to

methods they know, and can influence their Executive cousins.

Executives who spend too much time with fellow Sentinels at work can avail

themselves of training opportunities that explore new methods and techniques

whenever possible. Discovering the riches to be found in the constant flow of new

technologies alone can continuously enrich the skills of Executives and other

Sentinels.

Executive–Explorer Cooperation

It’s easy to get the impression that Executives and Explorers are complete

opposites. Executives are likely to view Explorers as impulsive and irresponsible.

Explorers likely see Executives as rigid and unadventurous. These might be

unbridgeable gaps between the two types if they didn’t share a deep respect for

practicality and mutual attention to concrete matters. They both gather information

from the world more directly and are less encumbered by the theoretical, with

which they have little patience or interest (although they may claim they do.)

Balanced Executive–Explorer Cooperation

Practical and Tangible

Executives and Explorers most often complement each other when things go

wrong. Executives look for results and solutions to problems. They aren’t always

concerned with the methods – as long as they aren’t too unorthodox. When

established procedures fail, and they call for improvisation, Explorer coworkers are

irreplaceable. They think on their feet and are masters at solving everyday concrete

puzzles, providing the results Executives desire.

Independent

Executives are expert delegators who prefer to either develop, or be part of, a team

which someone winds up and releases to run like clockwork. This fits Explorers

precisely, since they prefer to work without interference and excessive restrictions.

They crave independence.

However, Explorers must earn their free rein from Executives – and, luckily, this

resourceful personality type has little problem proving themselves to their

managers or their teams. If left to their own devices, Explorers typically pour

themselves into their work, paying attention to mastering the situation.

Unbalanced Executive–Explorer Cooperation

Tight Ship versus Free-Form Solutions

Executives may appreciate Explorers’ clever, spontaneous solutions that save the

day on occasion. However, there are limits, and they can reach a point where

enough of Explorers’ less-than-stringent regard for the rules becomes too much.

Rules represent stability to Executives. There’s nothing more important to them

than their department’s or company’s ship remaining upright. Explorers who take

too many liberties can begin to represent a serious threat to their overall

steadiness.

If Executives are their bosses or managers, they can clamp down excessively on

Explorers. Such excessive attempts to pull them into the fold and micromanage

them can damage sound working relationships by ignoring the strengths Explorers

bring. Tightening the reins may be an ill-advised solution.

Should Explorers be in charge, Executives can feel things are a bit chaotic. They may

continuously look to their Explorer leaders to enforce order, and then deal with

some disappointment when they don’t. Executives can lose respect for Explorer

managers they view as being “sloppy” with protocols. They may even feel

condescending toward their undisciplined superiors. Similar dynamics occur when

there’s a lateral relationship between Executives and Explorers.

Losing Autonomy

Executives can provide a strong tempering influence on Explorers’ need for

independence. But there are rules to follow, and if Executives sense a laxness on

the part of Explorers, they may overcompensate by micromanaging.

When Executives challenge Explorers’ autonomy, there’s the possibility of failure on

many levels. Constantly contentious relationships are never good in the workplace,

and Explorers who feel trapped always look for ways around obstacles – or develop

exit strategies.

Rebalancing Executive–Explorer Cooperation

Courageous Exchanges

Courageous Executives can look for ways to learn from Explorers’ attitudes.

Professional growth comes from trying things outside of their comfort zone, and

Explorers’ love of change, new stimulation, and independence can help Executives

broaden their perspectives a little if they occasionally go along for the ride with

them.

Likewise, Executives can help willing Explorers become more attuned to accepted

procedures. Whether they approve of them or not, there are always established

expectations in business. Executives can help them get a handle on them should

they wish to climb the ladder to higher positions or take their job skills to new

levels.

The Explorer Two-Step

Executives who work with Explorers can think of this experience as a kind of dance.

Dancing with a partner usually assumes enough flexibility where each dancer

anticipates the moves of the other, yet there’s some structure maintained in their

movements. The delicate dance of knowing when to assert boundaries and rules,

and when to let Explorers try unorthodox moves on their own, is essential for

Executives who work with them.

When it matters, Executives can enforce protocols. When it’s not that important,

they can loosen up a bit. Again, this doesn’t mean a wholesale disregard for the

rules, but Executives can find that, when it comes to Explorers, exercising a little

more flexibility and taking a bit more of a risk can pay off at times.

Conclusion Executives value their careers and professional experiences in profound ways.

Organizing and goal-setting that result in solid accomplishments help them find

meaning in their lives. While they may enjoy success and savor the perks that go

with      it,   those   are   just   ways    of   keeping     score   and   highlighting   their

accomplishments. While they may want to remind others of their shared success,

they remind themselves as well. On a deeper level, their primary concern at work is

their contribution to their companies, their families, and their communities.

Those who learn to include diverse personality types and styles in their work lives

find that they don’t just add more talent to their collaborations. Learning their

professional strengths and weaknesses, and those of other personality types, can

help Executives find a way to navigate work and life more effectively.

Some have said that when individuals come to the end of their lives, they’re unlikely

to think of all the hours spent at work as a highlight in their personal journeys. This

may be true for most, but it might not apply to Executives. For them, their work is

likely to be a defining part of what they happily consider as their legacies.

# Final Words

The Power to Help

Some Extraverted types like Executives often seek a pinnacle position for

themselves, and given their drive and capability, it’s not unusual for them to earn

such status in life. But, a high place can be a precipice with unique opportunities

and pitfalls; balance is required. One of the most worthy and balanced uses of

power is to help others avoid misfortune and act on their own potential. Executives

become admirable mentors and protectors when they unconditionally extend their

energy and wisdom to others in aid instead of judgement.

Being a positive force takes commitment, and while emotional connections aren’t

always easy for Executives, they maintain a deep sense of dedication to their

beliefs, including responsibility to other people in their lives. Sharing their energetic

sense of practical efficiency is more than a personal ethic for these types, it’s a form

of love. Executives who become facilitators of well-being achieve more than just a

high position, they gain genuine respect and appreciation, and a rewarding place in

their community.

The Foundation of a Happy Life

It’s not a bad thing that Executives love to establish order, putting things, events,

and even their feelings into a context that makes sense. Creating a stable structure

gives them a feeling of security, and a sense of continuity – when Executives

organize life, they can see choices and consequences, and it gives them the ability

rearrange things and change themselves for the better. These types benefits from a

foundation of order not because they want to stand still, but because a firm stance

lets them move powerfully – like a runner bracing before taking off.

There’s nothing wrong with thinking that things should be a certain way, if that

“should” reflects goals like freedom, happiness, prosperity, and love for themselves

and those around them. For these types, a very important cornerstone in the

foundation of a happy life is a balanced decision on what happiness looks like.

Executives’ drive is wonderful, but force itself doesn’t guarantee happiness, as

without harmony, it can lead to constant conflict. But, when Executives’ vigorous

drive and steely will are combined with respect for others – and their own feelings,

needs, and limits – their potential for growth and happiness is limitless.
